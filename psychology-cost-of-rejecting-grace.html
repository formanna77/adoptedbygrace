A Heart-to-Heart

Are You Sure You're Saved?

This is not a theology lesson. This is an intervention.

I need you to hear me. Not as a theologian. Not as a debater. As someone who loves you enough to say the thing no one else will say to your face.

There is something wrong with what you believe about your salvation. And the reason no one has told you is because the people around you believe the same thing. The whole room is sick, so no one notices the symptoms.

I'm asking for five minutes. That's it. Five minutes of honesty — not with me, with yourself. If I'm wrong, you lose five minutes. If I'm right, you may be hearing the most important thing anyone has ever said to you.

One Question

Where did your faith come from?

Where did the faith — the actual thing inside you that believed — where did that come from?

Did you produce it? Or did God put it there?

Be honest. Because your answer is the difference between grace and something that only looks like grace.

What You Actually Say

I know what you'll tell me. I've heard it a thousand times. You'll say:

"God did everything — I just accepted it."

You say that with complete sincerity. And you have no idea what you just said.

If God did everything, there is nothing left for you to do. Not even accepting. The moment you add "I just accepted it," God didn't do everything. You did the final thing. You closed the deal. You cast the deciding vote.

The word "just" is doing all the hiding. It makes the most important act of your existence sound like nothing. But it's not nothing. It's the one thing the person in hell didn't do. Which means it's the only thing that separates you from them. Which means you — not God — are the difference between your salvation and their damnation.

Read that again. You are the reason you're saved and they're not. Not God. You.

That is not grace. That is you saving yourself and calling it grace.

You would never say "I saved myself." But you believe a version of your salvation where you are the hero — and you've been calling it humility.

The Test You Can't Fake

Here's how you know whether you're trusting in grace or trusting in yourself:

Ask yourself this: "Why did I believe and my neighbor didn't?"

If your answer — at any level, in any form — includes something about you — your heart, your openness, your willingness, your free will, your choice, your seeking — then you have just named the thing that saved you. And it wasn't God. It was you. Something in you that your neighbor didn't have.

You just boasted. You just took credit. You just identified a work — a human contribution that determined your eternal destiny.

And you did it while saying "God gets all the glory."

Do you see how insidious this is? You can take credit for your own salvation while thanking God for it. The lie doesn't just deceive you — it makes you feel grateful while it's killing you.

What You're Actually Denying

When you say "I chose God," you are not just making a statement about your conversion. You are making a statement about your nature. You are saying that you — a sinner, dead in trespasses — had the ability to reach for God on your own. You are saying sin did not destroy your capacity to choose holiness. You are saying a sinner can choose the very thing that is opposite to his nature — which means he isn't really a sinner at all.

But Scripture doesn't say you were sick. It says you were dead. Jesus Himself said it: "Let the dead bury the dead." The dead are walking around. They function. They speak. They even attend church. But they are dead. And dead men don't make choices. Dead men don't "accept" anything. Dead men don't cooperate with the surgeon. If you were dead — truly dead — then every breath of spiritual life you now have was placed in you by someone else. Your faith is not your contribution. It is the proof that God brought you back from the dead. And calling it your decision is like a corpse taking credit for its own resurrection.

I know what you're thinking right now. "But God gave us free will."

That is the escape hatch. That is the phrase that lets you dismiss everything you just read without dealing with any of it. So let's deal with it.

What do you mean by "free will"? You mean the ability to choose God or reject Him. But you just said you were a sinner. A sinner's will is not free — it is enslaved to sin. That is what it means to have a sinful nature. Your desires, your inclinations, your will — they all bend toward self, not God. A will that is enslaved to sin cannot freely choose holiness any more than a man chained to the floor can freely walk out the door. The chain is not removed by wanting it gone. Someone else has to break it.

So when you say "free will," what you are actually saying is: "My sin nature was not strong enough to prevent me from choosing God." You are saying your will was powerful enough to override your own depravity. You are saying you — a slave — freed yourself. And that is not freedom. That is the deepest kind of bondage: the kind that doesn't know it's bound.

The Thing Nobody Will Tell You

Here is the part where I have to be honest with you in a way that might hurt. But I'd rather wound you with the truth than comfort you with a lie.

What you are doing — claiming your faith as your contribution, calling your decision the decisive factor, believing you played a role in your own salvation — has a name. It is called works-righteousness. It is the oldest enemy of grace in the history of the church. And it has never been more camouflaged than it is right now, inside the mouths of people who say "I believe in grace alone."

You are trusting in a work. You are calling it faith. And you cannot see it because the lie has dressed itself in the very language of the gospel.

This is not my opinion. This is what Paul said would happen:

You who are trying to be justified by the law have been alienated from Christ; you have fallen away from grace. (Galatians 5:4)
Galatians 5:4

To fall from grace is to add anything of yourself to what God has done. Even "just accepting it." If you added it, it's a work. And if it's a work, you've fallen from the only thing that can save you.

Why This Should Terrify You

You would never say "I earned heaven through good works." Of course not. Nobody would. But that's what makes this so dangerous — the lie is not in what you openly confess, it's in what your belief logically requires. If the difference between you and the person in hell is something you did — your choice, your acceptance, your decision — then your salvation rests on a human work. You just gave it a nicer name. You called it "faith." But a faith that you produced is not a gift. It is a work. And a work — no matter how small, no matter how sincere — cannot save.

If that is what you believe — and you have never repented of it — then you have never actually rested in grace. You have rested in yourself. And self-trust is not salvation. It is the very thing salvation rescues you from.

The most dangerous person in the church is not the atheist or the skeptic. It is the person who has built their entire identity on a faith they think they produced — and who will fight to the death to defend the credit they don't know they're taking.

So What Now?

If you belong to God, something is happening in you right now that you did not initiate. A crack is forming. A question is taking root that won't go away. That is not you being open-minded. That is the Shepherd calling a sheep that has been wandering in the wrong field.

The man who built this site ran from this truth for over a decade. Across continents. Through rebellion. Through his mother's death. Through a failing heart and a broken spine. And God would not let him go. On Christmas Day 2024, broke and bedridden, he finally stopped running. Not because he chose to. Because grace wouldn't stop chasing him.

If you are His, He will do the same to you. He will not let you rest in the lie that you saved yourself. He will hound you with this truth until you surrender it — gently if you yield, painfully if you resist.

But I must also say this: if what you just read makes you furious — if your instinct is to defend your contribution, to protect your role, to insist that you did something — ask yourself what is being threatened. The anger you feel is not righteous indignation. It is a work being exposed. And the harder you fight, the deeper it's buried.

If Something Just Broke Open

If you're still reading — if something in you won't let this go — that's not you. That's Him. The very grace this article is about, doing what it does. You don't have to figure it all out right now. You just have to stop defending the thing that's killing you.

Let go. You were never meant to carry the weight of your own salvation. The God who chose you before you existed will hold you long after you stop trying to hold yourself. He began it. He'll finish it. Not because you're strong enough to hold on — but because His grip does not fail.

Five minutes. That's all this took. But if the God of the universe used these five minutes to crack something open in you — then what happens next is between you and Him. And He is not finished with you.
