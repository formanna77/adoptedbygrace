The Cost of Rejecting Grace

When Claiming Your Salvation Becomes Your Damnation

To claim credit for your faith is to make faith a work. And what is not grace cannot save. Here's why rejecting the doctrines of grace is not theological disagreement—it is the most subtle and dangerous form of works-righteousness.

The Ancient Deception

There is a lie as old as Eden, and it wears many disguises. In the Garden, it whispered: "You will be like God." In the throne rooms of Pharaohs, it roared: "I will not let you go." In the synagogues of Jerusalem, it dressed itself in phylacteries and the scrolls of the law. And today, it speaks in the language of spiritual maturity and theological sophistication.

The lie is this: You have a right to claim credit for your own salvation.

It doesn't announce itself that way. It never does. Instead, it comes with a question: "If God chose you, did you really choose Him? If He decided, did you really decide? If faith is His gift, didn't you exercise some human autonomy in receiving it?"

These questions sound reasonable. They sound biblical. They sound like they're defending human dignity and free will.

But listen carefully to what happens when someone insists they answered these questions by choosing God themselves—that their decision saved them, their faith secured their conversion, their autonomy made the difference:

They have just claimed the credit that Scripture says they cannot take.

When someone rejects the doctrines of grace and claims credit for their own salvation, they do not simply disagree with a theological system. They contradict Scripture itself.

The Problem With Claiming Your Own Faith

Start with the clearest statement in all of Scripture:

For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God—not a result of works, so that no one may boast. (Ephesians 2:8–9, ESV)
Ephesians 2:8–9

Notice the precision. Paul does not say "grace is a gift, but faith is your achievement." He does not say "God gives grace, and you generate faith." The verse carefully specifies: "this is not your own doing"—and in context, "this" includes the faith itself.

The phrase "and THIS is not of yourselves" is the beating heart of this passage. What is "this"? The entire apparatus of salvation—not just the forgiveness, not just the justification, but the very faith through which we receive it. The faith that appears in the text as the instrument of our salvation is itself explicitly called a gift.

This is not negotiable. It is not a point of Reformed theology. It is the clear teaching of Scripture.

The Mechanism of Deception

When someone says "I chose God" or "I decided to believe," they may be speaking from genuine spiritual experience. But they are making a theological claim they cannot support. They are attributing the gift to themselves. And because the gift is what saves them, they are removing grace from the equation. What replaces grace? Works. What is works? The thing that cannot save.

Faith as a Work

Here is where the argument becomes devastating:

If faith is something you generate—if it is the product of your decision, your effort, your choice—then faith is a work. It is something you do. And the moment faith becomes a work, it cannot save, because Scripture explicitly states:

You who are trying to be justified by the law have been alienated from Christ; you have fallen away from grace. (Galatians 5:4)
Galatians 5:4

To "fall from grace" is to exchange grace for works. And if your faith is your work—something you accomplished, something you chose, something you generated—then you have fallen from grace. And if you have fallen from grace, nothing you have accomplished, chosen, or generated can save you.

This is not an inference. This is direct logical consequence of two scriptural premises:

  1. Premise One: Faith that is not grace cannot save (Galatians 5:4).
  2. Premise Two: If faith is your work, it is not grace.
  3. Conclusion: Therefore, if faith is your work, it cannot save.

To reject the doctrines of grace is to accept the conclusion that your faith cannot save you. Yet many who reject these doctrines sincerely believe they are saved by their faith. What they have done, whether they realize it or not, is embrace a doctrine that contradicts their own assurance.

Imagine a man drowning in the sea. A rescuer throws him a rope. The man grabs it and is pulled to safety. Later, someone asks: "Did the rope save you?"

He answers: "No, I grasped the rope. I pulled myself in. It was my effort that mattered."

But this is nonsense. The rope was already in his hand before he seized it. The tension on the rope preceded his grip. He was saved the moment the rescuer acted. His grasping was the response to grace already given—not the source of his salvation.

What Scripture Actually Teaches

The doctrines of grace do not emerge from Reformed speculation. They emerge from the plain testimony of Scripture when Scripture is allowed to speak clearly:

For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe on him, but also to suffer for him... (Philippians 1:29)
Philippians 1:29

Notice: belief itself is something "granted" to you. It is given as a gift. Not something you discovered on your own. Not something you achieved through your own searching. Something granted—awarded to you by the sovereign gift-giver.

No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws them... (John 6:44)
John 6:44

Not "unless they try really hard." Not "unless they make a decision." Not "unless they exercise their will." Unless they are drawn. The Greek word is helkuo—to drag, to pull forcefully toward. It is not a gentle invitation that can be easily refused. It is the action of God overcoming resistance and bringing someone to Christ.

And as many as had been appointed to eternal life believed. (Acts 13:48)
Acts 13:48

The order is crucial: appointment precedes faith. Not "as many as believed were appointed" but "as many as were appointed believed." Election is the cause; faith is the effect. The decision God made determined the faith that person would have.

And the Lord's servant must not be quarrelsome but kind to everyone, able to teach, patiently enduring evil, correcting his opponents with gentleness. God may perhaps grant them repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth. (2 Timothy 2:25–26)
2 Timothy 2:25–26

Repentance itself—the turning from sin—is something God "grants." It is not something the sinner generates independently. It is a gift God may or may not grant.

The Subtlety of the Deception

Here is what makes this lie so dangerous: it feels true to lived experience.

When you come to faith, you do choose. You do decide. You do repent. Your will is involved. Your mind wrestles with the truth. Your heart makes a decision. You experience this as something you are doing.

But experience is not the measure of causality. Just because you consciously choose does not mean you generated the ability to choose. Just because you exercise your will does not mean your will is independent of God's action. Just because faith feels like your decision does not mean it is your work in the theological sense that matters.

Consider: when you see a sunset, you see it. Your eyes open. Your mind perceives. You exercise the faculty of sight. But you did not create light. You did not originate photons. You did not establish the laws of optics. You are a receiver of something that was already acting on you before your eyes opened.

So it is with faith. You believe. You choose. You receive. But the gift was already prepared. The Father was already drawing you. The Spirit was already working. Your believing is your genuine response to God's prior grace—not the source of that grace.

The Theological Equation That Cannot Be Avoided

If faith is my achievement, then I have something to boast in. But Scripture says no one may boast. Therefore, faith cannot be my achievement. It must be God's gift. Any other conclusion contradicts Scripture and implicitly claims what Scripture forbids.

The Elect Will Not Rest in Self-Righteousness

Now we come to the pastoral reality that separates the elect from those who are not:

Scripture teaches that the elect—those whom God chose before the foundation of the world—will eventually submit to this truth. Not because they arrived at it through logic. Not because they defeated their resistance through effort. But because the Shepherd will not rest until His sheep hear His voice and follow.

My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they shall never perish; no one will snatch them out of my hand. (John 10:27–28)
John 10:27–28

The doctrines of grace are not optional theology for the elect to debate indefinitely. They are the irresistible truth that the Spirit of God presses upon the soul of God's people until they submit.

This can take years. It can involve profound struggle. The elect may resist fiercely—may build intellectual fortifications against this truth, may recruit other Christians to defend their autonomy, may construct entire theological systems to avoid it. But the Shepherd is relentless. And His sheep, though they wander, will not be lost.

Consider the life of Aaron Forman, the founder of this site. He understood the doctrines of grace. He spent a year intoxicated by their beauty. Then he ran. For over a decade, he fled from God—not because he had never believed, but because he believed too well and the implications terrified him. He lived as a secular unbeliever. He tried to make God forsake him. He rebelled with every weapon at his disposal.

And God would not let him go.

By his own account, his heart failed. His spine shattered. He was broken, bedridden, bankrupt. And in that brokenness, the Shepherd returned. Not with judgment. Not with condemnation. With tenderness. With the whisper that He had never let Aaron go—not through the rebellion, not through the exile, not through the decade of running. The election was secure. The grace was irresistible.

This is what the Holy Spirit does with His elect: He will not let them rest in self-righteousness. He will shatter every tower of autonomy they build. He will pursue them across deserts of doubt and through valleys of despair, until they finally bow their heads and acknowledge: It was grace. It was always grace.

The elect will eventually understand that grace alone saved them, faith alone received it, and even the faith was not theirs to claim. The Shepherd will not accept any other conclusion.

The Alarming Pattern

But what of those who persistently, actively, hostilely reject this truth?

Scripture offers a sobering word:

"You do not believe because you are not my sheep. My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me." (John 10:26–27)
John 10:26–27

There is a reason some people reject the doctrines of grace. And that reason is not merely intellectual. It is spiritual. It is that they are not the Shepherd's sheep.

This does not mean that every person who currently struggles with these truths is unregenerate. The Spirit often brings His people through seasons of resistance. But the Scripture is clear: those who belong to God will eventually come to understand that they belong to God—that He chose them, not they chose Him.

To be confronted with the doctrines of grace and to actively, persistently, hostilely reject them—to insist on your autonomy, to defend your right to claim credit for your salvation, to maintain that your decision was the deciding factor—this can be a sign of something terrifying. It can be the sign that you are not His sheep.

And if you are not His sheep, then you are not safe in the Shepherd's hand. And if you are not safe in His hand, then the question of whether you can save yourself becomes eternally significant.

The Pattern That Matters

Someone who hasn't yet understood the doctrines of grace is in a different position than someone who has been confronted with them repeatedly and has chosen to remain hostile. The first may be the elect in their season of growth. The second may be revealing something about their spiritual state that deserves attention.

The Door Remains Open

But let there be no misunderstanding: this article does not come with judgment. It comes with tenderness.

For the Struggling Soul

If you have resisted the doctrines of grace, if you have insisted on your autonomy, if you have claimed credit for your faith—know this: the door to mercy remains open. The Spirit of God is more patient than any human words can express. He does not grow weary in His pursuit of His sheep. And if you are His sheep, He will bring you home, regardless of how far you have wandered or how fiercely you have resisted.

The doctrines of grace are not a declaration of judgment on you. They are an invitation to rest. They are a call to surrender the exhausting work of maintaining your own salvation and to believe, instead, in a God who will not release what He has claimed.

If the Spirit is stirring your heart as you read this—if there is a sense, however faint, that you are not the author of your own faith—listen to Him. Not to these words, but to Him. Let Him press this truth deeper into your soul. Let Him show you that your salvation was secured not by your decision but by His. And find in that revelation not fear, but freedom.

The Truth That Sets Free

There is one final word that must be spoken:

The doctrines of grace are not burdensome. They are liberating.

To believe that you saved yourself is to carry an impossible weight. You must prove that you chose correctly. You must maintain that choice through every temptation, every doubt, every failure. You must ensure that your decision sticks. You must be the guarantor of your own salvation. And if you ever slip, if you ever doubt, if you ever fall—then what? Have you lost what you saved yourself into? Can the works that saved you be undone by a moment of weakness?

But to believe that God saved you is to be set free.

If God chose you, then your status does not depend on your performance. If grace saved you, then no failure of yours can undo what grace has done. If the faith by which you were saved is His gift to you, then the object of your trust is not your spiritual strength but His infinite power. You are not the guarantor of your salvation. The God who chose you is.

This is why the elect eventually submit to these truths. Not because they are forced. But because they discover, in the midst of their own weakness and failure, that they have been held all along by hands far stronger than their own.

And I am convinced that he who began a good work in you will carry it on to completion until the day of Christ Jesus. (Philippians 1:6)
Philippians 1:6

This is not your responsibility. It never was. And when you finally believe that—when you finally stop trying to save yourself and let yourself be saved—then you will know the truth. And the truth will set you free.
