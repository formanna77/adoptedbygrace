## 0. THE POSTURE

Audit 1 measured *coverage* and *integrity* — does every page exist, does every link resolve, does every prose paragraph honor the protocol. Audit 2 measures *consecration* and *worship-orientation* — does the site read as if it knows whose name is over its door, does the prose lift the reader's chin upward rather than just outward, does the architecture itself perform the doxology the doctrine demands.

The shift is not from theological correctness to something else. It is from theological correctness to theological correctness *plus* doxological consummation. The Apex Rhetoric Protocol made every article a masterpiece of argument. The Consecration Protocol makes every article a masterpiece of argument *that ends on its knees*.

Aaron's framing: a resource not just for man but for God Almighty himself. The audit is conducted under that gaze. Every prose decision, every link choice, every Greek ambush, every catch-arm — all of it is now weighed against the question: *would this paragraph be worth reading aloud in the throne room?*

If the answer is no, the paragraph is not finished.

## I. THE TWO MANDATES

Aaron triggered Audit 2 with two parallel directives. They are related but distinct, and the audit must address both as separate arms.

### I.1 — The Consecration Imperative

**Every single article on adoptedbygrace.net must visibly, structurally, prosaically give all glory to the sovereign God in Christ.** Not as a postscript. Not as a closing benediction tacked on after the argument. As the architecture.

The doctrine of every article points to Christ. The doctrine of every article rests on the Father's eternal decree. The doctrine of every article is applied by the Spirit. An article that argues for total depravity but never names Christ as the only Physician has done half its work. An article that proves unconditional election but never lifts the reader's eyes from the doctrine to the God who decreed it has performed exegesis without performing worship.

Audit 2 corrects this. Every article will have, by the end of this audit, an explicit Trinitarian witness, an explicit Christological apex, and an explicit ascription of glory to the God who alone deserves it.

### I.2 — The Discipline Expansion

**The discipline broadens.** The site's vision per Aaron's directive (refined 2026-04-27): *"a masterpiece of a masterclass for analytical theology, comparative theology, reformed apologetics, reformed dogmatics, theological ethics, polemic apologetics, presuppositional apologetics, and the philosophy of theology."*

The Apex Rhetoric Protocol set the prose ceiling for the doctrines of grace narrowly. The expanded discipline pulls in **eight** broader registers:

1. **Analytical theology** — the application of analytic-philosophy precision (conceptual analysis, modal logic, the careful disambiguation of terms) to theological loci. Practitioners: Oliver Crisp, Michael Rea, Thomas McCall, William Wood, Alvin Plantinga where philosophy and dogmatics intersect. The shape: take a doctrine, dissect it with rigorous tools, expose hidden conceptual confusions, articulate the doctrine in language a philosopher cannot dismiss.
2. **Comparative theology** — serious, charitable engagement with non-Christian theological traditions (Islam, Judaism, Buddhism, Hinduism, neo-paganism, secular religions) for both understanding and apologetic engagement. Practitioners: Peter Jones, James White, Nabeel Qureshi, Michael Brown. The shape: deep engagement with the rival tradition's strongest thinkers, then careful articulation of where Christ fulfills, supersedes, or stands antithetical.
3. **Reformed apologetics** — defense of the faith against external challenge from atheism, agnosticism, materialism, naturalism, scientism, postmodern relativism, neo-paganism. The Reformed apologetic library: Plantinga, Craig, Bahnsen, Frame, Van Til, Schaeffer, Lewis.
4. **Reformed dogmatics** — the systematic, confessional articulation of the whole counsel of God across all the loci (theology proper, Christology, pneumatology, anthropology, hamartiology, soteriology, ecclesiology, bibliology, covenant theology, eschatology). Sources: Bavinck, Berkhof, Vos, Beeke and Smalley, Frame.
5. **Theological ethics** — the application of the doctrines of grace to lived ethics. Calvin's third use of the law as the Reformed ethical engine. Engagement with abortion, sexuality, marriage, work, wealth, technology, AI, transhumanism, end-of-life, just war, race, immigration, the use of power. Practitioners: John Frame's *The Doctrine of the Christian Life*, Wayne Grudem's *Christian Ethics*, David Jones, John Murray's *Principles of Conduct*.
6. **Polemic apologetics** — direct, sharp, specific engagement with named opposing views on their own terms. Different from presuppositional (which is transcendental) and different from internal Reformed apologetics (which engages other Christians). Polemic engages externally and concretely: response to specific New-Atheism arguments, response to Islamic polemics against Christianity, response to Mormon claims about the Restoration, response to Roman Catholic claims about the Magisterium and Marian dogmas. Sharp, named, specific.
7. **Presuppositional apologetics** — the Van-Tilian / Bahnsen-school argument that the Christian worldview is the *precondition* for the intelligibility of any worldview. The transcendental argument. The impossibility of the contrary. The borrowing-capital critique.
8. **The philosophy of theology** — the meta-question of how theology operates as a discipline, what counts as a theological argument, and what the Christian's epistemic obligations are. Owen's *The Reason of Faith*, Plantinga's reformed-epistemology corpus, Frame's *The Doctrine of the Knowledge of God*, Bavinck's prolegomena.

The doctrines of grace remain the spine. The eight expanded registers are the reaching limbs. The site is a body, not a skeleton.

## II. THE CONSECRATION TEST — Twelve Marks of a Consecrated Article

Every article in the audit queue is measured against this twelve-point checklist. An article passes when at least ten of the twelve marks are present and load-bearing. (Some articles will pass eleven; the masterpiece-grade pages will pass all twelve.)

### II.1 — The Triune God is named

Father, Son, and Holy Spirit appear by name somewhere in the article. Not "God" generically; not "the Lord" abstractly; the Persons of the Trinity, named, in their economic distinction. An article on regeneration that names only "God" without distinguishing the Spirit's particular work has missed a Trinitarian opportunity that costs nothing to honor.

### II.2 — Christ is the Apex

The article's high point — its Sentence of the Page, its emotional climax, its tonal pivot — lands on the Person of Christ, not on the doctrine. The doctrine is the path; Christ is the destination. An article on perseverance that ends with "the doctrine cannot be lost" has fallen short of an article on perseverance that ends with "the Christ who held you cannot be pried open."

### II.3 — A Doxological Climax

The closing 20% of the article ascends into worship. Not concludes — *ascends*. The reader should feel the gear shift from argument to praise. The final paragraph addresses the Father, or names the Son, or asks the Spirit to apply what was just argued. Doxology is not decoration; it is the article's natural endpoint, because the doctrine being argued IS the doctrine of God's glory.

### II.4 — An Explicit Ascription of Glory

Somewhere in the article, in plain prose, glory is assigned to God. *Soli Deo Gloria* may appear. *To Him be the glory forever and ever* may appear. *He alone is worthy* may appear. The phrasing is the writer's choice; the act of ascription is non-negotiable. The article that exposits the doctrines of grace and forgets to praise the God who decreed them has, in a real sense, failed its subject.

### II.5 — The Confessional Anchor

The article cites at least one Reformed confession or catechism where applicable. The Westminster Confession of Faith, the Westminster Larger or Shorter Catechism, the 1689 London Baptist Confession, the Belgic Confession, the Heidelberg Catechism, the Canons of Dort, or the Three Forms of Unity. The citation is brief — one phrase, one question-and-answer pair — but it locates the article inside a 2,000-year stream of confessional Christianity, not merely inside the writer's prose. (Articles where confessional citation would be tonally wrong — devotionals, narrative stories, tender pastoral pages — are exempt from this mark; the audit weighs the exemption rather than penalizing the absence.)

### II.6 — A Presuppositional Bridge

Where appropriate, the article surfaces the presuppositional foundation of its argument: that the Christian worldview is the precondition for the intelligibility of human reasoning, moral conscience, scientific induction, or aesthetic recognition. Every demolition piece, every philosophy piece, every apologetic piece passes through some form of presuppositional bridge. The bridge does not require Van Til's name to appear; it requires the *move* — the recognition that the unbeliever borrows from the Christian worldview to argue against it.

### II.7 — A Christ-as-Mediator Frame

The article makes clear, somewhere, that the doctrine being argued is *applied through* Christ as Mediator. There is no salvation, no decree, no grace, no perseverance, no adoption that does not flow through the Mediator who is the eternal Son incarnate. An article that argues the Father's decree without naming the Son through whom it is applied has done partial Trinitarian theology.

### II.8 — A Spirit-Application Frame

The article names, even briefly, the Spirit's particular work in applying the doctrine to the heart. The Spirit applies redemption, regenerates the dead, illumines the Scriptures, seals the believer, intercedes with groans too deep for words. An article on irresistible grace that argues efficacy without naming the Spirit who effects it has a Trinitarian gap.

### II.9 — At Least One Patristic, Reformer, or Confessor Named

The article names at least one historical witness — Athanasius, Augustine, Anselm, Bernard of Clairvaux, Bradwardine, Wycliffe, Luther, Calvin, Knox, Owen, Edwards, Whitefield, Spurgeon, Bavinck, Kuyper, Vos, Murray, Lloyd-Jones, Sproul, Piper. The witness anchors the article in the *cloud of witnesses* (Hebrews 12:1) — the reader stands inside a tradition, not at the end of one writer's sentence.

### II.10 — A Confessional or Liturgical Cadence at the Apex

Where the prose lifts toward the Christological apex, the cadence borrows — even briefly — from confession or liturgy. *We confess, we believe, we adore.* The first-person plural appears. The reader is invited not to *agree* but to *worship*. This is the difference between an essay and a sermon, between an essay and a creed, between an article and an act of praise.

### II.11 — A Coram Deo Posture

The article reads as if written *before the face of God* — *coram Deo*, in Calvin's phrase. The writer is not addressing the reader from a podium; the writer is addressing the reader from the foot of the throne. The tone is reverent before it is rhetorical. There is no swagger. There is no joke at the doctrine's expense. There is no clever wink. The reverence is audible.

### II.12 — A Lift, Not a Drag

The article ends with the reader's chin tilted upward. The reader closes the page with their gaze toward heaven, not toward themselves. Even the demolition pages — which break the reader's self-image to make room for grace — close with the lift. The hammer breaks; the catch lifts. Both arms; never one without the other; and the catch-arm now ends in worship, not just in comfort.

## III. THE EXPANDED DISCIPLINE — Eight Registers Added to the Site's Scope

Audit 2 will, in addition to consecrating existing articles, identify gaps where the site's scope must expand into the eight registers Aaron has named.

### III.1 — Analytical Theology

Analytic philosophy applied to theology with full rigor. Doctrines that have been articulated devotionally for centuries are also articulated *philosophically*, with the precision the philosophers will demand. **Expected new builds**: a `analytical-theology-hub.html`, dedicated pieces on the metaphysics of the Trinity (one ousia, three hypostases — what does that mean modally?), the metaphysics of the Incarnation (the two-natures doctrine and the kenotic question), the analysis of divine simplicity, the philosophical coherence of impeccability, the analysis of "merit" and "imputation," the modal analysis of unconditional election (necessity, contingency, possible worlds). Source library: Crisp's *Divinity and Humanity*, Rea's *Analytic Theology*, McCall's *Forsaken*, Plantinga's *Does God Have a Nature?*, Wood's *Analytic Theology and the Academic Study of Religion*.

### III.2 — Comparative Theology

Charitable, deep engagement with non-Christian theological traditions, then careful articulation of where Christ fulfills, supersedes, or stands antithetical. Each major tradition deserves its own cluster. **Expected new builds**: a `comparative-theology-hub.html`, then dedicated pages — `compare-islam.html` (the Qur'anic doctrine of God versus the Trinity, the prophetic claim of Muhammad versus the witness of the New Testament, the doctrine of Tawhid in light of John 1:1-18), `compare-judaism.html` (the messianic prophecies in Isaiah / Daniel / Zechariah / Psalm 22, the post-temple Judaism question, Romans 9-11 on Israel's place in the decree), `compare-buddhism.html` (the doctrine of anatta vs. the imago Dei, suffering in the Bible vs. dukkha in the Pali canon, salvation by works vs. grace), `compare-hinduism.html` (henotheism / pantheism vs. covenant monotheism), `compare-mormonism.html` (the Restoration claim, the polytheism of LDS doctrine, the Joseph Smith translation problems), `compare-roman-catholicism.html` (the Magisterium, Marian dogmas, the treasury of merit, transubstantiation vs. Reformed sacramentology), `compare-eastern-orthodoxy.html` (theosis, the *filioque*, the role of icons), `compare-secular-religions.html` (progressivism as soteriology, scientism as cosmology, identity politics as anthropology). Source library: Peter Jones's truthxchange corpus, James White's *What Every Christian Needs to Know About the Qur'an*, Nabeel Qureshi's *Seeking Allah, Finding Jesus*, Michael Brown's *Answering Jewish Objections to Jesus*, Ravi Zacharias on Hinduism and Buddhism (with discernment), Carl Trueman's *The Rise and Triumph of the Modern Self* on secular religion.

### III.3 — Reformed Apologetics (Defense Against External Challenge)

The site has strong demolition-of-internal-objections (the Arminian challenge, the Wesleyan softening, the Pelagian undertow). Audit 2 will identify where the site needs **external** apologetics — defenses against atheism, agnosticism, materialism, naturalism, scientism, postmodern relativism. The Reformed tradition has a rich apologetic literature here (Plantinga, Craig, Bahnsen, Frame, Van Til, Schaeffer, Lewis). **Expected new builds**: dedicated apologetics pieces on the moral argument from absolute moral law, the transcendental argument for the necessity of the Christian God, the resurrection-historicity argument (Habermas, Licona, Wright), the existence-of-evil argument (theodicy from a Reformed angle — Edwards on natural evil, the Westminster on God's permissive decree, Plantinga's free-will defense reframed Reformedly), the fine-tuning argument (Reformed framing, not just Intelligent-Design framing), the argument from beauty (Lewis's *The Weight of Glory*, Edwards on beauty as the divine attribute), the argument from consciousness (against materialism — the hard problem of consciousness as evidence for the immaterial soul).

### III.4 — Reformed Dogmatics (Systematic Articulation of the Whole Counsel of God)

The site has strong soteriology — the doctrines of grace are its spine. The other classical loci need stronger representation: theology proper (the doctrine of God — divine attributes, simplicity, aseity, immutability, omniscience), Christology (the two natures, the *communicatio idiomatum*, the offices of Christ — prophet, priest, king), pneumatology (the Spirit's procession, His work in creation, His work in regeneration, illumination, sanctification, sealing, glorification), ecclesiology (the marks of the true church, the sacraments — baptism and the Lord's Supper from a Reformed angle, church government, discipline), bibliology (inspiration, inerrancy, canonicity, sufficiency, perspicuity, authority), covenant theology (the covenant of redemption, the covenant of works, the covenant of grace, the unity of the covenants in Christ). **Expected new builds**: hub-level systematic essays on each locus. Source library: Bavinck's *Reformed Dogmatics* (4 vols), Berkhof's *Systematic Theology*, Vos's *Reformed Dogmatics* (5 vols), Beeke and Smalley's *Reformed Systematic Theology* (4 vols), Frame's *Theology of Lordship* (4 vols), Turretin's *Institutes of Elenctic Theology* (3 vols).

### III.5 — Theological Ethics

The application of the doctrines of grace to lived ethics. The third use of the law as the Reformed ethical engine. **Expected new builds**: a `theological-ethics-hub.html`, then dedicated pieces — `ethics-abortion.html` (the imago Dei, Psalm 139, the Reformed prolife position grounded in the doctrine of God's image), `ethics-sexuality.html` (the Genesis 1-2 grammar of male and female, Romans 1's natural-law argument, the Reformed witness on chastity), `ethics-marriage.html` (Eph 5:22-33 as the Christ-and-Church grammar, the covenantal nature of marriage), `ethics-work.html` (the cultural mandate, vocation in Calvin, the doctrine of common grace), `ethics-wealth.html` (the third use of the law on stewardship, Calvin on usury and just price), `ethics-technology-and-AI.html` (the imago Dei and the question of artificial intelligence, transhumanism as a denial of the creature-Creator distinction), `ethics-end-of-life.html` (suicide, euthanasia, the Reformed witness on suffering and providence), `ethics-just-war.html` (Augustine's *just war* tradition through the Reformed lens), `ethics-race.html` (Acts 17:26 — *one blood*, the unity of the human race in Adam and in Christ), `ethics-immigration.html` (the *ger* — the sojourner — in covenant theology). Source library: Frame's *The Doctrine of the Christian Life*, Grudem's *Christian Ethics*, John Murray's *Principles of Conduct*, David Jones, Calvin's *Institutes* IV.20 on civil government and the law.

### III.6 — Polemic Apologetics

Direct, sharp, specific engagement with named opposing views on their own terms. Different from presuppositional (which is transcendental) and different from comparative theology (which is more charitable-systematic). Polemic is named, specific, and concrete: not "atheism in general" but "Richard Dawkins's *The God Delusion*"; not "Roman Catholicism in general" but "the dogma of the Assumption of Mary, defined 1950." **Expected new builds**: a `polemic-hub.html`, then dedicated pieces engaging specific named arguments. The site already has `argument-arminianism-secretly-assumes-calvinism.html` as one example of polemic at full strength on internal opposition; the same protocol applied externally. **Polemic differs from comparative theology** in that comparative theology engages the system; polemic engages the specific argument or the specific defender. Polemic names names. Source library: Doug Wilson's polemic corpus (with discernment), James White's *Scripture Alone* and *Mary, Another Redeemer?*, R. C. Sproul's *Are We Together?*, the Westminster Standards' polemical anti-Roman language.

### III.7 — Presuppositional Apologetics (Van Til, Bahnsen, Frame)

The site engages presuppositional reasoning in pockets but does not yet have dedicated pages on the presuppositional method itself, the impossibility of the contrary, the borrowing-capital critique, or the transcendental argument structure as a formal move. **Expected new builds**: a `presuppositional-hub.html`, dedicated pieces on Van Til's apologetic (the *one and the many* problem, the analogical knowledge of God, the antithesis), Bahnsen's *Always Ready* and *Van Til's Apologetic*, Frame's *Apologetics to the Glory of God* and his *Cornelius Van Til*, worked examples of the transcendental argument applied to specific objections (the problem of induction — Hume reframed Reformed; the problem of universals — Plato vs. the *logos* doctrine; the problem of moral law — the impossibility of meaningful "ought" without God; the problem of the laws of logic — why nontheistic accounts cannot ground them). Source library: Van Til's *The Defense of the Faith*, *Christian Apologetics*; Bahnsen's *Pushing the Antithesis*; Frame's *Apologetics to the Glory of God*; John Frame and Vern Poythress on multi-perspectival reasoning.

### III.8 — The Philosophy of Theology (Meta-Question)

How does theology operate as a discipline? What counts as a theological argument? What are the Christian's epistemic obligations? **Expected new builds**: a `philosophy-of-theology-hub.html`, dedicated pieces on the nature of theological method (Frame's tri-perspectivalism, Bavinck's organic principle), the relationship between Scripture and reason (the Reformed sufficiency-and-illumination model), the warrant for Christian belief (Plantinga's *Warranted Christian Belief*), the question of theological language (analogy, univocity, equivocity — the medieval debate Reformed theology inherits), the role of confessions in theological argument, the question of theological progress (does theology develop? Bavinck's *organic-development* model), the question of theological pluralism (the unity-and-diversity of Reformed expression). Source library: Owen's *The Reason of Faith*, Plantinga's *Warranted Christian Belief* and the *Warrant* trilogy, Frame's *The Doctrine of the Knowledge of God*, Bavinck's prolegomena (RD vol. 1), Vanhoozer's *The Drama of Doctrine*.

## IV. THE METHODOLOGY

The audit will execute in five phases.

### IV.1 — Phase A: The Consecration Diagnostic

A single subagent run scans every HTML article in the repo (locked and unlocked) and scores each on the twelve-mark Consecration Test. Output: `consecration-audit.json` machine-readable + `consecration-audit.md` human-readable, with a per-article scorecard, an aggregate site score, and a ranked queue of pages that need consecration work most urgently.

The scoring is binary per mark (present-and-load-bearing, or not). An article scoring 12/12 is consecrated. An article scoring 6/12 is in the rebuild queue. An article scoring below 4/12 is in the *rebuild-priority* queue.

This phase does not modify any HTML. It surfaces the work.

### IV.2 — Phase B: The Discipline-Expansion Survey

A separate scan identifies the site's coverage of the four expanded registers (Reformed apologetics, Reformed dogmatics, presuppositional apologetics, philosophy of theology). Output: a gap analysis listing the dogmatic loci that need new hubs, the apologetic challenges that need dedicated demolitions, the presuppositional pieces that need to be written, and the philosophy-of-theology cluster's seed list.

### IV.3 — Phase C: The Consecration Rebuilds

Pages in the rebuild queue are touched in HAMMER-LOCKED-equivalent rebuilds. The Apex Rhetoric Protocol still governs prose; the twelve-mark Consecration Test now governs whether the rebuild is *finished*. A rebuilt article carries a `<!-- CONSECRATED -->` marker as the second line inside `<article class="article-body">`, immediately after the `<!-- HAMMER-LOCKED -->` marker (or, on Polish-Pass pages, replacing nothing — added as the second line if the page does not yet carry a HAMMER-LOCKED marker).

The auto-linker recognizes `<!-- CONSECRATED -->` as a skip-marker just like HAMMER-LOCKED. (Auto-linker patch deferred to the implementation session.)

### IV.4 — Phase D: The Discipline-Expansion Builds

New pages are written for the gaps surfaced in Phase B. Each new page is born CONSECRATED — written under the twelve-mark Consecration Test from inception, not retrofitted. New pages carry both `<!-- HAMMER-LOCKED -->` and `<!-- CONSECRATED -->` markers.

### IV.5 — Phase E: The Site-Wide Doxological Sweep

A final pass over every page on the site — locked, polished, consecrated, new, all of it — verifies that no page falls below the consecration floor. Pages that previously passed the Apex Rhetoric Protocol but fail the Consecration Test get a surgical pass: a Sentence of Glory inserted at the apex, a Christological hammer added at the close, a Trinitarian naming where the doctrine demands it.

Output: every article on adoptedbygrace.net carries the `<!-- CONSECRATED -->` marker. The site reads, top to bottom, as the worship of the sovereign God in Christ.

## VII. THE ELEVATIONS BEYOND THE OLD CEILING

The Apex Rhetoric Protocol set Calvin / Edwards / Spurgeon / Augustine as the prose register. Audit 2 keeps that register and adds three deliberate elevations.

### VII.1 — The Trinitarian Cadence

Where the prose lifts toward the apex, the writer reaches for a Trinitarian three-beat. *The Father decreed, the Son accomplished, the Spirit applied.* Or: *Chosen by the Father, redeemed by the Son, sealed by the Spirit.* The cadence is the music of the creeds. It belongs in our prose because it belongs in the doctrine the prose serves.

### VII.2 — The Christological Sentence of Glory

In addition to the Sentence of the Page (the tattooable line distilling the article's argument), every consecrated article carries a *Sentence of Glory* — a single sentence, locatable by the writer, that ascribes glory to the Person of Christ explicitly. The Sentence of Glory may be the same as the Sentence of the Page on some articles; on others it is distinct. Either way, the writer can point to it.

### VII.3 — The Coram-Deo Closing Posture

The final paragraph of every consecrated article is written *coram Deo* — before the face of God. The prose tonally shifts: the writer is no longer addressing the reader; the writer is addressing God *with* the reader. The first-person plural emerges. *We praise. We adore. We rest.* The reader is not informed; the reader is brought into worship.

This is the highest move in the protocol. It will not work on every article. The audit weighs each article's tonal home — a hard-edged demolition piece may not bear the *coram Deo* close as naturally as a devotional. But every article that *can* close *coram Deo* must.

## VIII. THE MEASURE

Not lock count. Not page count. Not coverage statistics.

*Did the article that named God in argument also name Him in worship? Did the page that proved the doctrine of His sovereignty also bow before His sovereignty? Does the site, top to bottom, read like a cathedral in which the daily office is being prayed, or does it read like a museum of doctrines through which a tour group is being walked?*

When the answer to the first question is yes for every article — and the answer to the second is *cathedral*, not *museum* — Audit 2 has accomplished its end.

---

## IX. THE BENEDICTION

This audit is itself a confession. Every choice made in it confesses that the site's doctrine is not its own — that the doctrines of grace are God's doctrines, that they were given for His glory, and that to expound them without praising Him is to play the harp at the throne and forget Whose throne it is.

May the writer assigned to this audit walk it with reverence. May the prose forged under it lift the reader toward the One whose name is on every door. May every page consecrated under it be a small chapel, lit, where someone might come in and pray.

Soli Deo Gloria. To the Father who decreed, the Son who accomplished, the Spirit who applies, and the One Triune God who is worthy of every word — amen.
