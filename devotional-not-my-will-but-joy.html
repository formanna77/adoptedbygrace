Not My Will, But Joy

📖 Devotional ⏱ 7 min read

In Gethsemane, the Son of God pressed His face into the dirt and prayed the most terrifying prayer ever spoken: "Not my will, but yours, be done." He was sweating blood. He begged the Father to remove the cup. And then He drank it — not because He wanted to, but because He trusted the hand that poured it.

The one Person in all creation who had the greatest claim to His own will surrendered it completely. If He did that — the One who deserved to be obeyed — what makes us think our will is worth white-knuckling?

The Throne That Never Trembles

Modern Christianity has shoved divine sovereignty into the corner of its theology like a piece of furniture nobody wants to sit on. We acknowledge it exists. We just don't talk about it, because it makes people uncomfortable.

The Psalms have no such embarrassment.

"Our God is in the heavens; he does all that he pleases."

Psalm 115:3 (ESV)

"Whatever the Lord pleases, he does, in heaven and on earth, in the seas and all deeps."

Psalm 135:6 (ESV)

The sweep is staggering. Not just in matters of religion. Whatever the Lord pleases, He does. The physical cosmos bows to His will. The laws of nature execute His decrees. The atoms themselves hold together because He holds them.

We are afraid of this. We confuse sovereignty with tyranny — imagining a cosmic despot whose whims are law and whose character shifts with His mood. But this is a catastrophic misreading. The God who does all that He pleases is not arbitrary. He is goodness. He is justice. He is love. His sovereignty is the sovereignty of perfect character. His will is the will of infinite wisdom exercised in eternal mercy.

Sovereignty Versus Fatalism

Fatalism says: whatever happens, happens. No meaning. No purpose. Dominoes falling in a purposeless universe. Sovereignty says something that sounds similar but is utterly different: whatever happens, God ordained it — and the One who ordained it is not indifferent. He is personal. He is wise. He is good. And He is working all things together for the good of those who love Him.

The difference is not academic. It is the difference between despair and faith. When your child gets sick, fatalism whispers: meaningless suffering in a meaningless world. Sovereignty says: God has a purpose I cannot yet see, but I can trust the character of the God who permits it. One crushes you. The other carries you.

The Story God Rewrites

Joseph was betrayed, sold into slavery, falsely accused, and imprisoned. Everything in his life went catastrophically wrong. Then his brothers came to him expecting revenge. What they got was Genesis 50:20:

"As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are today."

Genesis 50:20 (ESV)

Look at the architecture. The same events. The same actions. Two completely different intentions layered on top of each other. The brothers meant evil — genuinely, freely, maliciously. But their intentions were not the final word. God's purpose prevailed. The very evil meant to destroy Joseph became the instrument that saved a nation.

This is sovereignty. Not puppetry. Orchestration. We choose. We act. We plan. And God gathers all of it — even the worst of it — into a story so much larger than we imagined that one day we will look back and weep with gratitude at the chapters that made us weep with grief.

The Prayer of Joyful Surrender

Here is the deepest paradox: when you surrender your will to God's, you do not lose yourself. You find yourself. When you stop fighting the current and let the river carry you, you discover the river is flowing home.

Jesus modeled this in the Garden. He did not say "I don't want to do this, but I will out of duty." He surrendered His will so completely that it became the foundation of our salvation. He accomplished more by yielding than He ever could have by insisting.

And then He rose from the dead.

The deepest joy is not getting what you want. It is wanting what you are given. It is the peace that arrives — illogical, uninvited, overwhelming — when you stop asking "why?" and start asking "what does God want to do in me through this?"

"And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose."

Romans 8:28 (ESV)

All things. The losses. The unanswered prayers. The dreams that died. The plans that shattered. God is working all of it — not as random tragedy, but as chapters in a story whose ending will make every page worth it.

Jonathan Edwards saw this more clearly than most. He wrote that when we truly grasp the beauty of God's sovereignty, we stop wanting our own will. We become like a choir member who hears the conductor's vision so clearly, so beautifully, that singing off-key becomes unthinkable. We align ourselves with God's will not out of fear — but out of attraction. Because His way is perfect. And ours is broken.

The Surrender That Brings Joy

"Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven." We learned this prayer as children. Most of us have never stopped to hear what it actually asks. In heaven, God's will is done perfectly. Without resistance. Without delay. Jesus taught us to pray that our hearts would become like that — that what we want would become what God wants, until the difference disappears entirely.

This is not the prayer of a slave. This is the prayer of a child who trusts his Father so completely that he wants nothing the Father has not chosen for him.

And when you pray it — truly pray it, not with your lips but with the marrow of your soul — the strangest thing happens. The resistance melts. The anxiety drains. The white-knuckled grip on your own plans gives way to the deepest security a human being can experience: the knowledge that the One who controls all things loves you enough to die for you, knows what you need before you ask, and is working everything — even the things that are breaking you — toward your ultimate, unimaginable good.

"Therefore humble yourselves under the mighty hand of God, that he may exalt you in due time, casting all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you."

1 Peter 5:6-7 (ESV)

Not my will. Not yours. His — the will of a God who loves you more than you love yourself, who sees further than you can see, and whose authority over all things is exercised with perfect justice and infinite mercy.

His will. And in that surrender — not loss, but the deepest joy you will ever know.
