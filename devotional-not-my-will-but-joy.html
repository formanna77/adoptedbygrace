Not My Will, But Joy

📖 Devotional ⏱ 7 min read

In Gethsemane, the Son of God pressed His face into the dirt and prayed the most terrifying prayer ever spoken: "Not my will, but yours, be done." He was sweating blood. He begged the Father to remove the cup. And then He drank it — not because He wanted to, but because He trusted the hand that poured it.

The one Person in all creation who had the greatest claim to His own will surrendered it completely. If He did that — the One who deserved to be obeyed — what makes us think our will is worth white-knuckling?

The Throne That Never Trembles

Modern Christianity has shoved divine sovereignty into the corner of its theology like a piece of furniture nobody wants to sit on. We acknowledge it exists. We just don't talk about it, because it makes people uncomfortable.

The Psalms have no such embarrassment.

"Our God is in the heavens; he does all that he pleases."

Psalm 115:3 (NIV)

"Whatever the Lord pleases, he does, in heaven and on earth, in the seas and all deeps."

Psalm 135:6 (NIV)

The sweep is staggering. Not just in matters of religion. Whatever the Lord pleases, He does. The physical cosmos bows to His will. The laws of nature execute His decrees. The atoms themselves hold together because He holds them.

We are afraid of this. We confuse sovereignty with tyranny — imagining a cosmic despot whose whims are law and whose character shifts with His mood. Psychology explains why: the human psyche recoils from any reality that strips it of the illusion of control. But this is a catastrophic misreading. The God who does all that He pleases is not arbitrary. He is goodness. He is justice. He is love. His sovereignty is the sovereignty of perfect character. His will is the will of infinite wisdom exercised in eternal mercy.

Sovereignty Versus Fatalism

Fatalism says: whatever happens, happens. No meaning. No purpose. Dominoes falling in a purposeless universe. Sovereignty says something that sounds similar but is utterly different: whatever happens, God ordained it — and the One who ordained it is not indifferent. He is personal. He is wise. He is good. And He is working all things together for the good of those who love Him. This is the heart of God's election — not arbitrary choice, but purposeful love.

The difference is not academic. It is the difference between despair and faith. When your child gets sick, fatalism whispers: meaningless suffering in a meaningless world. Sovereignty says: God has a purpose I cannot yet see, but I can trust the character of the God who permits it. One crushes you. The other carries you. For those in the midst of that crushing, there is a place to bring your pain.

The Story God Rewrites

Joseph was betrayed, sold into slavery, falsely accused, and imprisoned. Everything in his life went catastrophically wrong. Then his brothers came to him expecting revenge. What they got was Genesis 50:20:

"You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good to accomplish what is now being done, the saving of many lives."

Genesis 50:20 (NIV)

Look at the architecture. The same events. The same actions. Two completely different intentions layered on top of each other. The brothers meant evil — genuinely, freely, maliciously. But their intentions were not the final word. God's purpose prevailed. The very evil meant to destroy Joseph became the instrument that saved a nation.

This is sovereignty. Not puppetry. Orchestration. We choose. We act. We plan. And God gathers all of it — even the worst of it — into a story so much larger than we imagined that one day we will look back and weep with gratitude at the chapters that made us weep with grief.

The Prayer of Joyful Surrender

Here is the deepest paradox: when you surrender your will to God's, you do not lose yourself. You find yourself. When you stop fighting the current and let the river carry you, you discover the river is flowing home. This surrender is not weakness — it is the deepest rest a soul can know.

Jesus modeled this in the Garden. He did not say "I don't want to do this, but I will out of duty." He surrendered His will so completely that it became the foundation of our salvation. He accomplished more by yielding than He ever could have by insisting.

And then He rose from the dead.

The deepest joy is not getting what you want. It is wanting what you are given. It is the peace that arrives — illogical, uninvited, overwhelming — when you stop asking "why?" and start asking "what does God want to do in me through this?"

"And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose."

Romans 8:28 (NIV)

All things. The losses. The unanswered prayers. The dreams that died. The plans that shattered. God is working all of it — not as random tragedy, but as chapters in a story whose ending will make every page worth it.

Jonathan Edwards saw this more clearly than most. He wrote that when we truly grasp the beauty of God's sovereignty, we stop wanting our own will. We become like a choir member who hears the conductor's vision so clearly, so beautifully, that singing off-key becomes unthinkable. We align ourselves with God's will not out of fear — but out of attraction. Because His way is perfect. And ours is broken.

The Surrender That Brings Joy

"Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven." We learned this prayer as children. Most of us have never stopped to hear what it actually asks. In heaven, God's will is done perfectly. Without resistance. Without delay. Jesus taught us to pray that our hearts would become like that — that what we want would become what God wants, until the difference disappears entirely.

This is not the prayer of a slave. This is the prayer of a child who trusts his Father so completely that he wants nothing the Father has not chosen for him.

And when you pray it — truly pray it, not with your lips but with the marrow of your soul — the strangest thing happens. The resistance melts. The anxiety drains. The white-knuckled grip on your own plans gives way to the deepest security a human being can experience: the knowledge that the One who controls all things loves you enough to die for you, knows what you need before you ask, and is working everything — even the things that are breaking you — toward your ultimate, unimaginable good. Like gravity pulling you toward home, His will draws you toward the good you could never find on your own. That is the promise that will never break.

"Therefore humble yourselves under the mighty hand of God, that he may exalt you in due time, casting all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you."

1 Peter 5:6-7 (NIV)

Not my will. Not yours. His — the will of a God who loves you more than you love yourself, who sees further than you can see, and whose authority over all things is exercised with perfect justice and infinite mercy.

His will. And in that surrender — not loss, but the deepest joy you will ever know.

The Surrender Underneath the Surrender

There is one more layer here, and it is the layer most Christians never reach. They surrender their circumstances to God — their job, their marriage, their health, their plans — and they call that surrender. And it is. But it is not the deepest one.

The deepest surrender is not the surrender of your circumstances. It is the surrender of your choice. It is the surrender of the belief that your will was ever free in the first place. Because as long as you secretly believe that you are the captain of your own willing — that the steering wheel of your soul belongs to you, and that surrender is something you generously offer God from the resources of your own autonomous self — you have not yet handed over the deepest thing. You have only handed over what was on the surface. The hand on the wheel is still yours, and you are politely letting Him direct it.

But Scripture says something so much more devastating, and so much more freeing, than that: "It is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose" (Philippians 2:13). The willing itself is His. The acting itself is His. The very surrender you are offering Him at this moment is something He is producing in you. You are not handing Him a gift you generated. You are watching Him work in your own soul and calling it your contribution. This is not a put-down. This is the most liberating sentence in the universe. Because if even your willingness to surrender is His gift, then there is no part of your salvation left for you to fail at. The whole apparatus is His. The choosing is His. The willing is His. The surrendering is His. You are not the source of any of it. You are the riverbed through which the river of His grace is running.

And the moment you see this, the question of "am I surrendering enough?" disappears. Because the question always assumed you were the active party. The question assumed there was a quota of surrender you had to produce out of the resources of your own will, and the anxiety came from never being sure whether you had hit the quota. But you were never the producer. You were the recipient. The surrender that the Spirit is working in you right now is enough — not because the amount is high, but because the source is infinite. He is the one surrendering through you. He is the one bending your will toward His. He is the one making the prayer "Your will be done" rise from a heart that, left to its own resources, would have prayed only "my will be done" until the day it died.

Which means the joy of surrender is not the joy of accomplishing a difficult spiritual task. It is the joy of finally noticing that the task was being done for you the whole time. You did not climb to surrender. You were carried there. And the same God who carried you to the surrender will carry you through the consequences of it. There is no part of this you have to do alone. There is no part of this you ever did alone, even when you thought you were the one walking. The footprints in the sand were always His.
