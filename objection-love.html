Objection Answered

How Can a Loving God Choose Some and Not Others?

The objection that confuses love with indifference—and why election is the most loving thing God has ever done.

The Objection Stated

A truly loving God would save everyone. Selective love isn't love—it's favoritism. If God has the power to save all and chooses not to, that's not love, it's cruelty. A God who picks and chooses whom to love and whom to abandon is the opposite of the loving Father Jesus described. Love doesn't discriminate. Love includes everyone. Therefore, predestination contradicts the very nature of God's love.

The Category Confusion

This objection sounds devastating until you realize something crucial: the objector is asking about love's extent when Scripture is answering about love's nature. These are completely different questions, and conflating them destroys the argument.

Here is the signature move of this site: All love discriminates.

Ask a parent: "Do you love all children equally?" They will look at you like you've lost your mind. Of course they don't. They love their children—their particular children—with a fierce, irrational, sacrificial love that towers infinitely above their love for other children. That selectivity is not the weakness of their love. It's the proof that they love at all. A parent who loves all children equally loves their own children not at all.

Ask a spouse: "Do you love your spouse the way you love everyone else?" No. A spouse who loves their partner the way they love strangers hasn't married them—they've done them a profound disservice. Covenant love is exclusive. It's jealous. It's devoted. It's selective. That's what makes it love.

Ask a friend: "Do you care about all people equally?" Of course not. We have best friends, close friends, acquaintances. Some people matter to us in ways others don't. Some we'd take a bullet for. Others we smile at politely. That discrimination is not a character flaw—it's the nature of real, embodied, human love.

Love that cannot choose is not love. It's cosmic sentimentality. It's the abstract affection of a god who loves "humanity" while never stepping into the real, particular pain of an actual human being.

So when you ask, "How can a loving God choose some and not others?"—you've already answered your own question. He chooses because He loves. Election is love expressed in its highest form. (And if you think about it, the objection to election is really just the complaint that God loves like a father instead of like a vending machine.)

What Scripture Actually Says About God's Love

Scripture is clear and unrelenting about this: God's love is electing, particular, and discriminating.

"For God so loved the world that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life." — John 3:16 (ESV) John 3:16

Wait—doesn't this say God loved the "world"? Yes. But read it in context. John uses "world" (κόσμος, kosmos) in a specific way throughout his gospel. Sometimes it means creation. Sometimes it means the global community. Always it means what God accomplished: the redemption of His people from every nation, tribe, and tongue. John's use of "world" does not negate his equally clear doctrines of grace (John 6:37, 10:27-29, 17:2-6). Jesus loves the world He came to save, not indiscriminately, but with the particular love of a shepherd for his flock.

"Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her..." — Ephesians 5:25 (ESV) Ephesians 5:25

This is the key passage. Christ's love is directed at someone specific: the church. Not humanity in general. Not everyone theoretically. The church. His particular, chosen, redeemed bride. He didn't die for an abstract principle. He died for a specific people. That's what the language means. That's what the cross proves.

"As it is written, 'Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated.'" — Romans 9:13 (ESV) Romans 9:13

This verse demolishes the idea that God's love is universal and unconditional. God explicitly states that He loved Jacob and hated Esau—before either was born, before either could do good or evil. This is divine love as election. God sets His love on some. This is not accident. It's the heart of His nature.

"The Lord set his love on you and chose you, not because you were more numerous than any other people, but because the Lord loves you..." — Deuteronomy 7:7-8 (ESV) Deuteronomy 7:7-8

God's love for Israel was uncaused by anything in them. It wasn't earned. It wasn't merited. It was freely chosen. This is the pattern of divine love: it chooses. It discriminates. It sets its affection on a people and clings to them forever.

"In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins." — 1 John 4:10 (ESV) 1 John 4:10

Love that must be earned is not love—it's transaction. Real love initiates. And God's love initiated. He loved us before we could love Him. Before we could respond. Before we were even born. That love chose us.

"The Lord has appeared to him from afar. 'I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued my faithfulness to you.'" — Jeremiah 31:3 (ESV) Jeremiah 31:3

Everlasting love. Not newly discovered when you finally chose Him back. Not created by your response. Love that existed before time, that transcends time, that will exist after time. This is electing love. This is the love that chose you before the foundation of the world.

The Greek Word That Changes Everything: Agape

In Ephesians 5:25, Paul uses the word ἀγαπάω (agapaō) to describe Christ's love for the church. This is not a vague, sentimental affection. This is the Greek word for self-sacrificial, covenant love. It's love that gives everything. Love that cannot be reversed.

Here's what's crucial: this love has an object. It's directed at someone specific. You don't say "Christ loved ἀγαπάω humanity" the way you might say "the sun shines on everyone." Agape is too particular, too fierce, too costly for that kind of universality. You say "Christ loved the church" because that's what agape means: particular, chosen, irrevocable love for a specific beloved.

Compare this to how John uses kosmos (world). Kosmos is the system, the fallen order, the realm that opposes God. When Scripture says God loves the world (John 3:16), it means He loves the redemptive outcome: His people called out of the world, transformed from it. It never means He loves sin, or the flesh, or the powers arrayed against Him.

The doctrines of grace are simply the claim that God's agape—His particular, self-sacrificial, electing love—is directed at His church. That is not weakness. That is perfection.

The Devastating Question

Ask yourself this: If God loved everyone equally and identically, why would anyone feel personally loved by God at all?

When your mother says, "I love you," those words move you because you know she doesn't say them to everyone the same way. If she loved all seven billion people with the identical love she gives you, her words would be meaningless. Not because she is limited, but because love that cannot distinguish is affection that cannot touch. The objector wants a God who loves everyone the same. But that God — a God who plays no favorites, makes no covenants, and chooses no one in particular — is a God whose love means nothing in particular to anyone. That's not a more loving God. That's a less personal one.

Seven Arguments From Scripture and Reason

Argument 1

All Love Is Selective

Love by definition discriminates. It chooses one person or group as the object of particular affection. A parent loves their child. A spouse loves their partner. A friend loves their friend. That's what love is. It's not a vague benevolence toward all humanity equally. It's a radical, preferential commitment to specific people.

God is love (1 John 4:8). His love, therefore, must be expressed the way love is expressed: through choice. Through preference. Through covenant. Election is not a departure from God's love. It's love in its truest, most beautiful form.

Argument 2

Universal Love Doesn't Solve the Problem

The objector claims that a loving God would save everyone. But here's the problem: even if God loves all people equally, the fact remains that not all people are saved. So the objector still has hell. They still have people separated from God forever.

In the Arminian system, God loves everyone equally, but only those who choose Him are saved. So salvation depends on the creature, not the Creator. The creature takes credit. God tried His best but couldn't overcome human rebellion. Which is more comforting—a God who chose you before the foundation of the world and will never lose you? Or a God who loves you equally with everyone else but might not be able to save you because it depends on your will, not His?

The objection doesn't eliminate the problem. It just shifts the blame.

Argument 3

The Cross Proves Selective Love

Jesus didn't die as a general gesture toward humanity. He died for specific people: His sheep (John 10:11, 15). He died for the church, His bride, whom He loves and for whom He gave Himself (Ephesians 5:25-26). He didn't pray for the world. In His high priestly prayer, He said: "I am not praying for the world but for those whom you have given me" (John 17:9).

The cross was not universal in its efficacy. It was particular. It was intentional. It was effective for those whom the Father gave the Son. That's why it accomplished something. That's why sin was actually paid for, not just offered as an option. The cross is the ultimate proof of selective love.

Argument 4

Love Initiated, Not Earned

Real love is not contingent on the beloved's response. A mother loves her infant not because the infant has chosen to love her back, but because she is a mother. A father doesn't love his son conditionally, waiting to see if the son will reciprocate. He loves him unconditionally, before the son can understand the concept of love.

God's love works the same way. "We love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19). His love is not a response to our belief. His love is the cause of our belief. His choosing us is not dependent on our choosing Him back. He loved us when we were dead in sin (Ephesians 2:4-5). He initiated. He chose. He acted. That's what real love does.

Argument 5

Election Makes Love Personal

"God loves everyone" is as warm as "the sun shines on everyone." It's objectively true but profoundly impersonal. It doesn't comfort you. It doesn't move you. It doesn't change you.

But election—the truth that before the foundation of the world, God set His eternal love on you, specifically, by name—that's the most intimate reality conceivable. That's the love that makes you weep. That's the truth that transforms you. That's the gospel that rescues you.

The fact that God loves you not because you are worthy, not because you chose Him first, not because you could somehow earn it—but because He chose to set His love on you before you even existed—that is the love that saves. That is the love that holds you when everything else falls apart. That's the love that never lets go.

Argument 6

The Parent Analogy

No parent says, "I love all children equally, including yours and everyone else's the same way." That's not love. That's indifference wearing love's costume. Real parents love their own children with a ferocity, particularity, and devotion that is incomparable to how they love other people's children.

Scripture uses this metaphor constantly. God is Father. The elect are His children (Ephesians 1:5: "he predestined us for adoption as sons through Jesus Christ"). A father's love for his sons is selective. It's particular. It's covenantal. And it's the closest thing on earth to the infinite love of God for His chosen people.

Argument 7

Sovereignty and Love Are Not Enemies

The objector imagines that God's sovereignty (His absolute power to choose) somehow contradicts His love. But they work together perfectly. God's sovereignty enables His love. Because He is God, He can choose whom He will love. Because He is omnipotent, He can keep them forever. Because He is eternally constant, His love never wavers.

If God were not sovereign—if His will depended on ours, if His love could be reversed by our rebellion—then His love would be weak, contingent, and ultimately untrustworthy. But because He is sovereign, His love is absolute. He chose you, and nothing—not your sin, not your doubt, not your rebellion—can separate you from that love (Romans 8:38-39).

Historical Witness

Augustine, On the Predestination of the Saints

"What does predestination mean but foreknowledge and purposefulness respecting the kindness of God? For predestination is the foreknowledge and preparation of the benefits of God by which those who were to be liberated are most certainly liberated."

Charles Spurgeon, Sermon on Divine Love

"Election is a precious truth, for it teaches us that God is not merely powerful, but that He is love. When a man is entirely dependent upon God, he loves to hear that God has from the beginning chosen his people, that He did not leave them to chance."

Jonathan Edwards, Charity and Its Fruits

"There is no greater instance of love in all the world than the love of God in electing and predestinating His people. For all other love is in response to something in the beloved. But this love creates the loveliness it loves."

J.I. Packer, Knowing God, Ch. 19

"The grace of God is love freely shown towards guilty sinners, contrary to their merit and indeed in defiance of their demerit. It is God showing goodness to persons who deserve only severity, and had no reason to expect anything but severity."

Thomas Goodwin (1600–1680), The Objects and Acts of Justifying Faith

"God did not choose us because we were lovely. He loved us, and that made us lovely."

Five Objections Answered

1. "But 1 John 4:8 says 'God is love'—so He must love everyone equally."

No. Being love by nature doesn't mean loving everyone in the same way or with the same outcome. A parent is a parent, but they don't parent all children equally—they parent their own children with particular devotion. God's nature is love, but His love is expressed as particular, electing love for His covenant people. This is consistent with His nature. It's an expression of it.

2. "But John 3:16 says God loved the world—that sounds universal."

In John's usage, the "world" is not every individual person. It's the cosmic redemptive reality: the fact that God's salvation extends to people from every nation, tribe, language, and people group. Jesus loved the world He came to save, which includes people from every corner of creation—but He came specifically to save His sheep (John 10:14-15), not to offer a vague opportunity to those who might choose Him.

3. "But Ezekiel 18:23 says God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked."

Correct. But this is about God's character, not about election. God does not delight in death. But He is not unjust in judging sin. And the fact that God doesn't delight in judgment doesn't mean He doesn't execute judgment perfectly. God's sovereignty and God's character are not in tension. He can predestine people to judgment while taking no pleasure in it, just as a judge can sentence a criminal to prison without taking pleasure in the sentence, even though it's just.

4. "This makes God arbitrary and capricious—like He's just picking and choosing for no reason."

No. Arbitrariness means acting without a reason. But God has reasons for everything He does—reasons that flow from His wisdom, justice, mercy, and eternal purposes. His choice to elect is not arbitrary; it's a manifestation of His sovereign will. We may not understand His reasons completely, but that doesn't make Him capricious. It means His thoughts are higher than our thoughts (Isaiah 55:8-9).

5. "But Jesus taught us to love our enemies—shouldn't God do the same?"

God does love His enemies—but not in the way believers love enemies. Jesus commands us to pray for those who persecute us (Matthew 5:44), to overcome evil with good. But God's love for His elect is of a different order entirely. It's covenantal. It's irrevocable. It's the love of a Father for His children. God doesn't love the reprobate the way He loves the elect, and He doesn't command us to either. He commands us to pray for our enemies that they might repent and be saved—but He does not promise that all will be.

The Devotional Truth

All of this—the exegesis, the arguments, the historical witness—it all collapses into one staggering reality: God chose you.

Not because you were lovable. You weren't. None of us were. We were dead in sin, enslaved to it, actively hostile to God. There was nothing in you that deserved to be chosen.

Not because you chose Him first. You didn't. You came to faith because He drew you, because He gave you a new heart, because He opened your eyes to see the beauty of Christ. Your response was an effect of His choice, not the cause of it.

But because before the foundation of the world was laid, before you drew a single breath, before you could do anything good or bad—God set His eternal love on you. By name. Specifically. Irrevocably.

That's the love that pursued Aaron Forman through rebellion and exile. That's the love that didn't release him even when he tried to make God forsake him. That's the love that met him on Christmas Day 2024, broken and bedridden, and whispered: "I never let you go."

That's the love that will hold you. That's the love that chose you. That's the love that will never, ever let you go.

Love chose you. Before you could choose yourself. Before you could believe. Before you could do anything but exist—God loved you into being and chose you for Himself.

The Final Word

"For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord." — Romans 8:38-39 (ESV) Romans 8:38-39

This is not the promise of universal love. This is the promise of particular, electing, unbreakable love. Nothing will separate you from the love of God. Why? Because that love chose you, and what God chooses, no power in heaven or earth can snatch away.

He Will Never Give Up on You

The love that chose you before time is the love that holds you through all of it.
