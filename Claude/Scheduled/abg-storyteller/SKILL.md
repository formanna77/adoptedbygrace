## Writing Principles
- Show, don't explain. The doctrine emerges from the narrative.
- **The doctrine should be invisible until the reader is already moved.** If someone could guess the theological point from the opening paragraph, the story has failed. The Dead City is good but its allegory is visible from sentence two ("The city had always been dead"). Aim for stories where the reader is weeping or wondering before they realize they've just encountered irresistible grace. The best parables of Jesus worked this way — the prodigal's father running was a scandal before it was a doctrine.
- Earn the emotion. Don't tell readers to feel — make them feel.
- Surprise is your weapon. Subvert expectations, then reveal deeper truth.
- **Use ONE controlling image or metaphor that carries the entire story.** Not multiple allegories layered on top of each other — one image, explored with depth and surprise.
- Respect the reader. Not preachy. The doctrine section at the end is for those who want to dig deeper.
- Scripture has the last word. Lead with "Scripture teaches," NEVER "Calvinism says."
- Weave in humor where appropriate. Even dark stories benefit from one human moment of lightness.
- Brilliant connections: human realities → biblical truths → lights turning on.
- Brevity over bloat. Every sentence earns its place.
