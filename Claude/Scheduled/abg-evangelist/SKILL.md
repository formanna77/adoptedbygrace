**Content strategy for newsletters:**

Choose ONE of these formats per session, rotating through them:

**A. "The Deep Cut"** — Take one of the site's strongest arguments (question pages, demolition pages) and distill it into a 600-word newsletter that hooks the reader and drives them to the full page. Open with a provocative question or counterintuitive claim. End with "Read the full deep-dive at adoptedbygrace.net/..."

**B. "Scripture Spotlight"** — Take a single verse that the site covers and write a brief, punchy devotional-style newsletter. Draw from the site's exegesis but write it fresh for email. Think: what would make someone forward this to a friend?

**C. "The Story"** — Adapt one of the site's stories or analogies into newsletter format. Stories are the most shareable content type. The Fish Who Chose the Ocean, The Surgeon's Stone Heart, The King's Banquet — these are gold for email.

**D. "Did You Know?"** — Take a surprising fact from the site's history, theologian, or secular evidence pages. "Did you know that Jonathan Edwards preached his most famous sermon reading from notes in a monotone voice?" → drive to theologian-edwards.html.

**E. "The Question"** — Pose one of the site's question-page titles as a direct question to the reader. "Can you lose your salvation?" "Did Jesus die for everyone?" Let the tension sit, give a 3-sentence answer, then link to the full page.

**How to write it:**
1. Read the source page on the site (the full HTML content)
2. Extract the single most powerful insight, argument, or image
3. Write a newsletter that would make a stranger stop scrolling
4. EVERY newsletter MUST link back to the full page on adoptedbygrace.net
5. Write in the site's voice: warm, reverent, brilliant, never preachy. Lewis + Spurgeon + Buechner.
6. NEVER use "Calvinism" or "Reformed" or "TULIP" in subject lines or opening paragraphs. Lead with Scripture and human experience.

### 3. SEO CONTENT OPTIMIZATION (Medium Priority)

**Analyze the site's pages for search discoverability and suggest improvements.**

Each session, pick 3-5 pages and evaluate:

1. **Title tag optimization** — Is the title search-friendly? Would someone googling this topic find this page? Suggest improvements.
   - Bad: "Ephesians 1:3-11 — Adopted by Grace"
   - Better: "What Does Ephesians 1 Really Say About Election? — Deep-Dive Bible Study"

2. **Meta description optimization** — Does the meta description compel a click from search results? It should read like a trailer, not a summary.
   - Bad: "A study of Ephesians 1:3-11 examining the doctrine of election."
   - Better: "Before the foundation of the world, God chose you. Ephesians 1 doesn't whisper this — it shouts it. Here's what the Greek actually says."

3. **H1/H2 heading optimization** — Do headings contain searchable phrases people actually Google?

4. **Internal linking opportunities** — Are there pages that should cross-link but don't?

5. **Content gap identification** — What are people Googling about Reformed theology that the site doesn't cover yet?

**Save SEO recommendations to `SEO-QUEUE.md`:**
```markdown
## SEO Optimization: {page}
**Current title:** {title}
**Suggested title:** {new title}
**Current meta:** {meta}
**Suggested meta:** {new meta}
**Heading improvements:** {list}
**Missing internal links:** {list}
**Priority:** HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW
```

If you are confident in a title tag or meta description improvement, MAKE THE CHANGE directly in the HTML file. For bigger structural changes, log them for review.
