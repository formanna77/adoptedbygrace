You Just Heard About Election and It Broke Your Brain

Welcome. This will make sense. We will build the scaffolding together — one truth on top of another, no jargon, no embarrassment, until the thing that broke your brain becomes the thing that holds it together.

Someone said "God chooses who gets saved" and the ground shifted under your feet. Maybe it was a friend at coffee. Maybe it was a sermon you walked into not expecting a hammer. Maybe it was a passage in Romans you had read a hundred times and never noticed, and then noticed all at once. Maybe it made you angry. Maybe it terrified you. Maybe — and this is the part that scares you most — it rang true in a place underneath your arguments where you could not quite reach down to argue with it.

You are in the right place, and we are glad you came. Slow your shoulders. Breathe out. The doctrines of grace are not strange visitors to the Christian faith. They are the bones underneath the body that has been holding you up since the day you first believed. You did not know they were there because nobody had named them for you. Today is the day they get named. By the time you reach the end of this path, the doctrine that just shook you will not be standing over you with a club. It will be the floor you are standing on.

The truth that just broke your brain is the truth that has been holding it together since before you were born.

Three Things to Hear Before You Begin

One: you are not the first person who has felt this. Every honest believer who has ever encountered the doctrines of grace has felt some version of what you are feeling — the vertigo, the protest, the half-formed objection, the slow turning toward Scripture to see whether the unsettling thing is actually there. Augustine felt it. Luther felt it. Spurgeon felt it. Edwards felt it. Whitefield felt it. The men whose books shaped Western Christianity for fifteen centuries each had a moment that looked exactly like the moment you are having. Their advice, from across the centuries, is the same: do not run from the question; let Scripture answer it.

Two: this is not a logic puzzle to be solved before bed. It is a building to be entered, room by room. The sequence below was designed to keep you from tripping. Step One is not Step Five for a reason. Each card builds on the one before it; skip ahead and the floor will not yet be there to hold you. Take it slow. Take a week between cards if you need to. Go back. Reread. Pray over it. The Spirit who gave you the faith you already have is the same Spirit who is going to walk you through these rooms, and He is not in a hurry.

Three: the goal is not Calvinism. The goal is the God of the Bible. The doctrines of grace are not a denomination you are about to join. They are the names — the careful, biblical names — for what Scripture has been saying about salvation since Genesis. You are not converting from one Christian tribe to another. You are seeing, more clearly, the gospel you already trusted. The map is not the country. The doctrines are not the love. They are the language we use to describe a love that was holding you before language existed.

What "It Broke My Brain" Actually Means

Locate the breakage. It is rarely the doctrine itself that does the damage. It is what the doctrine implies about you. Let the implications surface, slowly. If God chose me, then I did not choose myself. If I did not choose myself, then the version of my testimony I have been telling — the one in which I am the protagonist, the spiritual self-starter, the one who took the decisive step — is not quite the version Scripture tells. If Scripture tells a different version, the protagonist of my own salvation is no longer me. That last sentence is the place the brain breaks. Not because the new protagonist is unsafe — He is, in fact, the only safe protagonist there has ever been. The brain breaks because the old protagonist has been getting credit for thirty years and is about to be told the credit was a mistake. Not a mortal sin. Not damnable. A mistake. And the mistake had been comfortable.

Sit with that for a moment longer than is comfortable. The discomfort is the gospel arriving on time. The flesh wants the credit. The Spirit, who loves you too much to let the flesh keep it, is gently — patiently, over weeks or years — relocating the credit to the only Person who has ever been able to bear its weight. Christ saved you. Not your decision, not your sincerity, not your insight, not your willingness, not your altar call, not your tradition. Christ. And every implication of that single noun is going to need to land in your imagination, slowly, over the rest of your life. The cards below are the first ten landings.

When the Path Feels Long

Some readers walk all ten cards in a weekend, sleep on it, and wake up unmistakably home. Some readers walk Card One and stop. Some readers walk three cards, run, come back six months later, and walk three more. There is no schedule. The Spirit who has been at work in your life since before you knew His Name is not measuring this against the calendar of any other reader, and He is not embarrassed by your slow pace. He has never given up on a soul mid-walk, and He is not going to start with you.

If a card raises a hard question, do not hide from it. Bring it. The doctrines of grace can survive every honest question that has ever been asked of them, and they have been asked harder ones than any you will think of. If a card lands as comfort and you find yourself crying at the kitchen table, do not apologize for the tears. The tears are usually your body letting go of weight you have been carrying for so long you forgot it was weight. Let it go. Faith was a gift to begin with; the carrying of it was never your job.

Three short truths to send you into the path with. One: you cannot lose what was never yours to hold. The salvation that broke your brain is not a fragile object you have to grip with white-knuckled fear. It is a Hand that has always been gripping you. Two: the doctrines of grace are not the property of any one tradition. They are the inheritance of every believer who has ever read Scripture and let it speak. You are not joining a tribe. You are arriving at a homeland that has been yours since before time. Three: the God who has been kind enough to break your brain in this small way is the same God who, on the day He calls you home, will be kind enough to let you see the whole architecture from above. You are not going to be embarrassed by what you discover. You are going to weep with relief.

So begin Card One. Read slowly. Pray as you go. Argue out loud with the screen if you have to; the screen does not mind. And when the tenth card lands, come back to this page and look at the path you walked, and notice that the hand that walked you was His. It was His all along.

Welcome home. He held you through the whole walk.
