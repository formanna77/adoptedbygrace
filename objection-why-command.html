Objections Answered Objection #2 · Sovereignty, Commands, and the Means of Grace

If God Predestined Everything, Why Does He Command Us to Do Anything?

If God already decided who would be saved, why does He command everyone to repent? It's the question that makes people walk away from sovereignty—and when answered honestly, it makes sovereignty inescapable. Scripture shows that God's decrees don't bypass commands. They accomplish themselves through them. And that changes everything about prayer, evangelism, and obedience.

The Objection Stated

You prayed last night. You meant it. And then this thought crept in: If God already decided everything, why did I bother?

That thought has a name. And this page exists to kill it.

If God has already predestined who will be saved and who won't, why does He command us to repent and believe? Why preach the gospel? Why pray? Why evangelize? If everything is already determined, human activity is meaningless. God is just going through the motions. Predestination makes all commands, all preaching, all prayer completely pointless.

The objection carries real emotional weight. It feels like a logical slam-dunk. How can you square an absolute divine decree with genuine human responsibility? How can a command mean anything if the outcome is already settled?

But let's name what's really happening beneath the logic. If you're the one asking this question, there's a good chance you're not actually concerned about the philosophical coherence of God's decrees. You're afraid. Afraid that if God has already decided everything, then your prayers are whispered into a void. Afraid your evangelism is theater. Afraid that a sovereign God has turned you into a prop in a play you didn't audition for. And underneath all of that — if you're honest — afraid of a God you can't negotiate with. A God who doesn't need your permission.

That fear is real. This page is for you. And the answer Scripture gives is not what you expect — it doesn't just defend God's sovereignty. It reveals that sovereignty is the very thing that makes your prayers powerful, your evangelism unstoppable, and your obedience meaningful.

The answer also illuminates why total depravity is the bedrock upon which every other truth stands, why the thing we resist most is often the truth we need most, and ultimately why even the faith to believe is itself a gift from God — a truth that reframes everything about the relationship between divine command and human response.

What This Question Is Really Asking

Before we answer the logic, we need to name the feeling. Because this objection almost never starts as a philosophical puzzle. It starts as a pit in your stomach.

You're lying in bed after a conversation about sovereignty, and the thought hits: If God has already decided, then nothing I do changes anything. My prayers are pointless. My choices are theater. I'm a puppet. And behind that thought is something even more primal — something most people won't say out loud: If I can't influence the outcome, then I don't matter.

That is the real fear. Not that commands are logically incoherent with decrees. The real fear is that you are irrelevant. That the universe doesn't need your participation. That a sovereign God has turned you into a prop.

And here is where the answer begins to dismantle the fear from the inside: you are not a prop. You are the means. God didn't decree the end and then tolerate your existence. He decreed the end through your existence. Through your prayers. Through your conversations. Through your obedience. You are not a bystander watching God's plan unfold. You are the instrument through which He unfolds it. The decree doesn't erase your significance. It establishes it. You matter precisely because God ordained that you would.

The person who truly grasps this stops feeling like a puppet and starts feeling like a knight — sent on a mission by a King who has already won the war, and who chose you to carry the victory banner into the cities that don't know it yet. That is the difference between fatalism and sovereignty. Fatalism says nothing matters. Sovereignty says everything matters — because the King decreed that it would.

If you're wrestling with this distinction — between feeling controlled and feeling commissioned — you are closer to the truth than you realize. The very fact that you care whether your actions matter is itself evidence that God is working in you. A puppet doesn't lose sleep over whether it's a puppet. A person the Spirit is drawing does. And here is the irony that should stop you cold: the objection itself is a prisoner's dilemma — you think you're defending your freedom, but you're actually defending the prison. The person who insists "My actions must be autonomous to matter" has confused independence with significance. A child's first steps matter immensely — not because the child walks independently, but because the Father's hands are underneath the whole time.

The Hidden Assumption

The objection rests on a hidden assumption: If God decrees the END, then He does not also decree the MEANS.

But Scripture teaches something far different. God ordains the destination and the road. He decrees not just the final outcome, but all the steps that lead to it. The decree and the means are not in tension — the means are part of the decree.

Illustration

A father tells his toddler to walk toward him. The toddler cannot walk. The child's legs are too weak, his balance too uncertain. Yet the father commands: "Walk to me." Is the command pointless? Not at all. The father's hands are underneath, supporting every step, bearing the weight. The command and the holding are simultaneous. The certainty that the child will reach the father doesn't make the command arbitrary. It makes it possible. This is how God works with the elect—He commands what He secures, and secures what He commands.

The Objector's Own Logic Refutes Them: Imagine someone argues: "If a university has predetermined who will be admitted, then studying for the entrance exam is pointless." Would anyone accept this? Of course not. The predetermined admission works through the normal means. The decree secures the means, not bypasses them. Yet this is precisely what the objection claims about God. It treats divine sovereignty as if it operates against causation rather than through it. The irony is devastating: the objector has adopted a framework that makes sense in no other domain of reality.

Here's another devastating question the objection cannot answer: If commands prove ability, then can you be perfect? Jesus commands it in Matthew 5:48—"Be perfect, as your Father in heaven is perfect." Does that command prove you have the capacity? Of course not. You are finite, fallen, and unable. Yet the command stands. It reveals God's standard. It exposes your incapacity. And that exposure is precisely what drives you to the God who alone can complete what He has begun in you.

This is not just a theological principle — even secular philosophy acknowledges that a determined outcome doesn't nullify the means that produce it.

God decrees that His elect will be saved. That decree is certain and irreversible. But God also decrees that they will be saved through the preaching of the gospel, through prayer, through the means of grace. The decree doesn't bypass the means. It secures the means.

Here's where it gets uncomfortably funny. The objector who says "If God predestined everything, why pray?" is the same person who eats breakfast even though God already knows whether they'll die of hunger. They go to the doctor even though God already knows when they'll die. They set their alarm even though God already knows when they'll wake up. In literally every other area of life, they understand that a determined outcome doesn't eliminate the means. They only discover this "problem" when it touches sovereignty. That tells you the objection isn't philosophical. It's personal — the flesh demands credit for its contribution. It's the same pattern that makes us pay an enormous psychological price to avoid the implications of grace.

Or consider it from the other side. No parent has ever said: "I knew my child would learn to walk eventually, so I stopped encouraging them." No farmer has ever said: "The weather is determined by forces beyond my control, so planting seeds is pointless." No surgeon has ever said: "The patient's recovery was determined by their biology, so my scalpel was irrelevant." The objection applies a standard to God that no one applies to anything else in the universe. It is — and there's no polite way to say this — the theological equivalent of refusing to steer your car because GPS already knows where you're going.

This is the truth of the ordained connection between ends and means — a bedrock principle in Reformed theology and a clear teaching of Scripture. To understand this fully, explore how God's decrees work across all creation: God doesn't decree results in isolation. He ordains the entire chain that produces them.

Scripture: God Decrees Ends AND Means

The Bible does not leave us guessing. In passage after passage, we see God accomplishing His predetermined purposes precisely through human action, command, and obedience. The two are not in competition — they are woven together.

1. Acts 27:22–31 — The Shipwreck

Acts 27:22-24 (NIV)
"Yet now I urge you to take heart, for there will be no loss of life among you, but only the ship. For there stood by me this night an angel of the God to whom I belong and whom I worship, saying, 'Do not be afraid, Paul; you must stand before Caesar. And behold, God has granted you all those who sail with you.'"

God gave Paul an absolute promise: no one will die in the shipwreck. The outcome is divinely decreed and certain. Yet watch what happens next:

Acts 27:31 (NIV)
"Paul said to the centurion and the soldiers, 'Unless these men stay in the ship, you cannot be saved.'"

Paul says something remarkable: Unless the men stay in the ship, you cannot be saved. The means (staying in the ship) is absolutely necessary — yet the outcome (everyone's safety) is absolutely certain. God ordained both. The certainty of the end guarantees the reality of the means, not the reverse.

Did You Know? Charles Spurgeon — the most popular preacher in Victorian England — was once asked how he reconciled God's sovereignty with human responsibility in his preaching. His answer was disarming: "I never reconcile friends." The point was profound: sovereignty and responsibility are not enemies that need reconciliation. They are friends who work together. Spurgeon preached to 10 million people in his lifetime and endured fierce criticism for his belief in election. Yet he was more zealous in evangelism than his critics. Why? Because he understood what the objection misses: God's decree doesn't diminish the means. It guarantees them. He knew that when he preached, God's elect would hear. His confidence was unshakeable.

2. Acts 18:9–11 — Corinth

Acts 18:9-11 (NIV)
"And the Lord said to Paul one night in a vision, 'Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you, and no one will attack you to harm you, for I have many in this city who are my people.' And he settled there a year and six months, teaching the word of God among them."

God tells Paul: "I have many in this city who are my people" — these people are already God's elect. Their salvation is decreed and certain. What does Paul do? He stays and preaches for eighteen months. Why? Because God had predetermined both who would be saved and that preaching would be the means by which they were saved. The election didn't make Paul's preaching unnecessary. It made his preaching powerful.

If the idea that God chose people before they heard the gospel sounds too radical, consider the evidence: Scripture says it over sixty times.

3. Romans 10:14–17 — The Chain of Salvation

Romans 10:14-17 (NIV)
"How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching? And how are they to preach unless they are sent?... So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ."

Paul lays out a chain of causation. God's decree (that certain people will be saved) flows through a series of human means: someone must be sent, someone must preach, the word must be heard, faith must come through that hearing. Election is not the end of this chain — it is the power source that runs through it. The means are not made pointless by the decree. They are made efficacious by it.

4. 2 Timothy 2:10 — Paul's Labor

2 Timothy 2:10 (NIV)
"Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they also may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus with eternal glory."

Paul endures hardship and suffering specifically because God has elect people. His labor is not rendered meaningless by election. Rather, election is the reason for his labor. He knows his preaching will reach the elect. He knows his suffering will contribute to their salvation. Election is not the enemy of missions — it is the foundation of it.

"The decree doesn't make commands pointless. It makes them powerful."

5. Philippians 2:12–13 — Command and Divine Work

Philippians 2:12-13 (NIV)
"Therefore, my beloved, as you have always obeyed, so now, not only as in my presence but much more in my absence, work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose."

Here is the truth of means in one verse. Paul commands believers: "Work out your own salvation." But simultaneously, Paul says God is the one doing the working — working in them "both to will and to work." The command does not compete with divine sovereignty. The divine work does not eliminate human responsibility. They operate together. God's decree to work in you is the means by which the command to work finds its power.

6. Ezekiel 37:1–10 — The Valley of Dry Bones (The Scripture Knockout)

Ezekiel 37:4-6 (NIV)
"Then he said to me, 'Prophesy over these bones, and say to them, O dry bones, hear the word of the LORD. Thus says the Lord God to these bones: Behold, I will cause breath to enter you, and you shall live. And I will lay sinews upon you, and will cause flesh to come upon you, and cover you with skin, and put breath in you, and you shall live.'"

This passage demolishes the objection. Ezekiel is commanded to prophesy to dead bones. Dead bones cannot hear. They cannot respond. They have zero ability to obey. Yet Ezekiel obeys and prophesies—and God raises them from the dead. The command did not prove the bones' capacity. The command did not cause the resurrection. But it was the ordained means through which God accomplished it. God decreed the resurrection and the command. The command to preach did not diminish God's sovereign power. It demonstrated it. This is the biblical answer: God commands what only He can do, and that command is the very mechanism by which He does it.

You Think You're "Not That Bad." Here's Why.

Ezekiel prophesied to dead bones — and the fact that this doesn't alarm you tells you everything about the depth of the problem. The bones in that valley didn't know they were dead. They couldn't. Death is not a condition you diagnose from the inside. And this is precisely where the objection about commands reveals something the objector never intended to expose: the reason you think commands prove ability is that you don't believe you're as dead as Scripture says you are.

The reason most people reject total depravity is not that they've examined the evidence and found it lacking. It's that they've never actually looked — because the flesh will not willingly look in a mirror that shows it what it really is. This is what philosophers call self-deception at the structural level — not a conscious lie, but a blindness so complete that the blind person is convinced they can see.

Here is what "dead in sin" looks like when you stop hiding behind the metaphor and start looking at your actual life:

You have never once loved God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength. Not for a single second. Not on your best day. The greatest commandment — the one Jesus said matters most — you have violated every moment of your existence. And you don't feel the weight of that because you've quietly decided that God doesn't really expect all. You've negotiated the standard down to something you can manage. That negotiation is itself a sin.

You prefer sermons that make you feel good over sermons that make you feel convicted. You choose churches based on comfort, not truth. You gravitate toward teachers who affirm you and avoid the ones who challenge you. Your "spiritual life" is carefully curated to avoid the parts of God that make you uncomfortable — His sovereignty, His wrath, His absolute right to do as He pleases with what is His.

And here is the part no one says out loud: you think you're "not that bad" because you've only ever compared yourself to other sinners. You've never seriously compared yourself to God's actual standard. The standard is not "better than average." The standard is the holiness of God Himself — the being before whom angels hide their faces and mountains melt like wax. Measured against that, your best righteousness is what Isaiah called it: filthy rags. Not a B-minus. Filthy rags.

When that gap becomes real to you — when you stop measuring yourself against your neighbor and start measuring yourself against the God who is holy, holy, holy — then the question is no longer "Why would God need to choose me?" The question becomes: "How could a God this holy choose someone this fallen?" And the answer is the most beautiful word in any language: grace.

If the objection insists that commands prove human ability, then Ezekiel 37 is the graveyard of that objection.

Seven Reasons God Commands What He Has Decreed

If God has decreed the outcome, why issue commands at all? Scripture gives us seven profound reasons:

1

Commands Are the Means of the Decree

God uses human obedience as the instrument through which His purposes are accomplished. When God commands "Repent and believe," He is ordaining the mechanism by which the elect come to salvation. The command is not peripheral to the decree — it is central. This is why responsibility and predestination are not opposites but partners.

2

Commands Reveal God's Moral Will

God's commands show us what is right, what God delights in, what is holy and good. Commands reveal God's character. They matter whether anyone obeys them or not. "Be holy, for I am holy" reveals God's nature even apart from whether every creature obeys.

3

Commands Expose Human Inability

The law reveals that we cannot obey perfectly (Rom. 3:20, 7:7–13). Commands serve to show us our desperate need for grace. This is the "first use of the law" — to drive us to despair of ourselves and flee to Christ. This is how total depravity becomes the doorway to irresistible grace.

4

Commands Are the Occasion for Grace

When God commands "Repent and believe," He gives the very repentance and faith He commands (Acts 5:31, 11:18; Phil. 1:29). The command is the vehicle through which the gift arrives. The command occasions the grace. This is the crown jewel of the gospel: faith itself is not something you generate — it is something you receive. And if faith is received, then the command to believe is not a demand for self-generated performance. It is the doorway through which God delivers His own gift. The person who claims they produced their own faith has deceived themselves about the nature of the gift.

5

Commands Structure the Redeemed Life

Even after salvation, God's commands guide the believer's sanctification. This is the "third use of the law" — the law as a rule of life for those already saved by grace. Commands tell us how to live in light of who we are in Christ.

6

Commands Establish Moral Accountability

If God never commanded, He would have no basis for judgment. The command establishes the standard. Failure to meet it demonstrates guilt. God's grace rescues the elect from that guilt — but the command is what establishes their culpability in the first place.

7

Prayer Is Ordained as a Means

God ordains the answer and ordains that we pray for it. "You do not have, because you do not ask" (James 4:2). Prayer is not informing God of something He doesn't know. It is the means He has ordained for accomplishing His purposes. This is why evangelism flowing from sovereignty is not tentative but unstoppable.

Commands, in other words, are not a backup plan for a universe God can't control. They are the very mechanism by which God displays His sovereignty, accomplishes His purposes, and demonstrates His glory.

Read those seven reasons again — slowly. Now ask yourself which one you instinctively resist the most. That resistance is diagnostic. If you bristle at Reason 3 (commands expose inability), it's because you still believe you can obey on your own. If you resist Reason 4 (commands are the occasion for grace), it's because you're still holding onto the idea that your faith was self-generated. If Reason 7 (prayer as ordained means) makes you uneasy, it's because you've confused sovereignty with fatalism — and somewhere deep down, you're afraid that if God is truly in control, your voice doesn't reach Him. But notice what your resistance reveals: the objection was never about logic. It was about control. You don't want God to be sovereign through your actions. You want your actions to be sovereign apart from God. And that is the very problem of merit that the gospel exists to dismantle. The flesh doesn't object to commands. It objects to grace — because grace means the credit belongs entirely to Someone Else.

The Command That Reveals Everything

There is one command in Scripture that, when you understand how God works through means, cracks the entire debate wide open.

"Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved" (Acts 16:31).

A straightforward command. Believe. But here's the question the objector never asks: Where does the ability to obey that command come from?

If God commands you to believe, and you do believe — was that belief self-generated? Did you reach into the depths of your dead, sin-enslaved heart and produce saving faith from your own resources? Or was the faith itself given to you by the same God who issued the command?

Scripture is unambiguous. "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8). "For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him" (Philippians 1:29). The Greek word translated "granted" is echaristhē — from charis, grace. Your belief was graced to you. It was a gift. And this changes everything about how you understand the relationship between God's commands and human response.

This is the crown jewel hiding inside the objection. The command to believe is not evidence that you can believe on your own. It is the means through which God gives you the belief. He commands, and in the commanding, He supplies. He says "Walk!" to the paralytic — and the paralytic walks, not because the command proved his legs worked, but because the command carried the power to make them work. He says "Come forth!" to Lazarus — and Lazarus comes, not because dead men can hear, but because the voice of God is the thing that makes dead men live.

So when someone asks "If God predestined everything, why command us to believe?" — the answer is staggering: because the command is the delivery vehicle for the gift. The preaching of "Believe!" is not information about what you should do. It is the Holy Spirit's instrument for creating what only He can create. The command to believe and the gift of faith are not in competition. The command is the means. The faith is the miracle. And both were ordained before you took your first breath.

"He chose you before you could disappoint Him. Which means your failures can't surprise Him either. The same God who commanded you to believe is the God who gave you the faith to do it."

And here is where the infinite regress of choice becomes inescapable: if you "chose" to believe, what caused you to make that choice? Your desire? What caused the desire? Your nature? What made your nature receptive? Trace the chain back far enough and you will always arrive at the same place: an uncaused Cause who acted first. The trilemma of self-salvation has only one resolution — a God who began what you could not begin for yourself.

The Language Test — A Truth Every Parent Already Knows

Here is something that should stop you cold. Every parent on earth already understands the relationship between divine commands and human response. They just don't realize it.

A mother speaks to her newborn: "Say mama." The infant cannot speak. It has no language. It has no capacity to obey the command. Its vocal cords are untrained, its brain has not yet formed the neural pathways for speech, its understanding of the word "mama" is precisely zero. The infant is, in every meaningful sense, unable to comply.

And yet the mother speaks. She commands. She says "mama" a thousand times — in the kitchen, at bedtime, during bath time — not because she believes the infant can already speak, but because her speaking is the means by which the infant learns to speak. The command does not prove the child's ability. The command creates the ability. The mother's voice is not testing the infant's capacity. It is building it.

No parent has ever said, "My child learned to speak because they chose to — I just provided the environment." Every parent knows instinctively that their speaking came first, that the child's response was caused by the parent's initiative, that the miracle of language acquisition is something that happened to the child, not something the child accomplished independently. The child did not bootstrap itself into language. The parent's voice entered the child's world and literally restructured the child's brain — creating capacities that did not exist before the speaking began.

Now apply this to God.

God speaks to the spiritually dead: "Believe." The sinner cannot believe. Their nature is hostile to God, their spiritual faculties are dead, their understanding of saving truth is precisely zero in any way that could produce genuine faith. They are, in every meaningful sense, unable to comply. And yet God speaks. He commands. He says "Believe" — through preaching, through Scripture, through the witness of His people — not because He believes the sinner can already believe, but because His speaking is the means by which the sinner comes to faith.

The command to believe does not prove human ability. It creates it. God's Word carries the power to produce what it demands — just as a mother's voice carries the power to create language in a child who has none. And here is where the analogy becomes devastating: no one credits the infant for learning to speak. We credit the parent. We credit the environment. We credit the biological design that made acquisition possible. The infant's "contribution" — babbling, imitating, eventually forming words — was entirely caused by forces outside itself. The child did not decide to acquire language. Language happened to the child.

If you understand this about language, you already understand what Scripture teaches about faith. You just haven't applied it yet. The same God who designed infants to acquire language through parental speech designed sinners to acquire faith through His Word. In both cases, the command precedes the capacity. In both cases, the speaking creates the response. In both cases, the "recipient" cannot take credit for what was given to them by another.

"A mother does not wait for her infant to develop language before she speaks to it. She speaks, and her speaking creates the capacity. God does not wait for sinners to develop faith before He commands them to believe. He commands, and His commanding carries the faith within it."

The objector who says "If God predestined who would believe, why command belief?" might as well ask, "If the mother knows the infant can't speak, why talk to it?" The answer is the same in both cases: because the command is the delivery mechanism for the gift. The speaking is not separate from the ability it produces. It IS the means.

Election: The Engine of Evangelism

Far from killing evangelism, election is the very thing that makes evangelism possible and assured.

Why Election Empowers Missions

  • Without election, no one could be saved. Total depravity means no one would freely choose God apart from divine grace. We are dead in sin, hostile to God, unwilling to come to Him (John 6:44; Rom. 3:10–18). The only hope for anyone's salvation is God's election — His prior choice to save them. This is why commands to believe are not contradictory—they are the very mechanism by which God's grace reaches us.
  • With election, we KNOW some will respond. Because God has chosen certain people and will open their hearts, we can preach with confidence. "I have many in this city who are my people" (Acts 18:10). We don't preach into a void. We preach to a harvest already planted.
  • Election explains Paul's strategy. Paul kept preaching even in hostile cities because he knew God had elect people there. He wasn't hoping against hope. He was laboring in faith, knowing his efforts would reach the very people God had chosen.

History Confirms This

The great missionaries of history have been, almost without exception, convinced of God's sovereignty:

  • William Carey (1761–1834) — The "father of modern missions," a committed Calvinist. He did not believe election made mission pointless. He believed it made mission powerful.
  • Adoniram Judson (1788–1850) — Missionary to Burma, committed to God's sovereignty even in the face of seemingly impossible obstacles.
  • David Brainerd (1718–1747) — Missionary to Native Americans, a devout believer in God's election.
  • Hudson Taylor (1832–1905) — Founder of China Inland Mission, a Calvinist who preached with unshakeable confidence that God would save His elect.
  • John Paton (1824–1907) — Missionary to the New Hebrides, unafraid to preach Christ because he trusted in God's sovereign choice.
If God had painted a yellow stripe on the backs of the elect, I'd go around lifting up shirt tails to find them. But since He hasn't, I preach the gospel to every creature.
— Charles Spurgeon

Spurgeon grasped the issue perfectly. We don't know who the elect are. So we preach to everyone. And we preach with confidence because we know that wherever God has chosen someone, our preaching will reach them. Election is not an excuse for laziness. It is a spur to faithful labor.

Think of it this way. A doctor who has a treatment that might work administers it tentatively, apologetically, managing expectations. A doctor who has a treatment that will work administers it with urgency and confidence — not less effort but more, because the certainty of the cure compels action, not passivity. The Arminian evangelist is the tentative doctor: "I have a gospel that could save you, if you cooperate." The Reformed evangelist is the confident one: "I have a gospel that will save everyone it was designed to save — and I don't know if you're one of them, so I'm preaching to you with everything I have." Which doctor works harder? The one who knows the treatment is certain. Every time. This is why the psychological cost of rejecting grace includes the loss of missionary confidence — the person who denies election preaches from hope, but the person who affirms it preaches from certainty.

A Deeper Observation: Notice something remarkable—the very people most vocal about this objection often have the least developed prayer life and the least vigorous evangelism. Why? Because they unconsciously sense that if God is truly sovereign, their own effort doesn't matter. But that's precisely the problem they came to defend against. Their objection conceals a deeper fear: if I don't control my salvation, then I'm not in control. And a person can't rest in grace until they stop trying to secure themselves. This is why understanding why we resist God's sovereignty is as important as understanding the truth itself. It's the pathway to freedom. Learn why the mind naturally resists this truth.

The Cloud of Witnesses

The greatest theological minds across centuries have understood this truth. The consistency is stunning—from Augustine in the 5th century to modern thinkers, the best minds in Christian history have all arrived at the same conclusion: sovereignty and means work together, not against each other.

Augustine (354–430)

"God's decrees are certain, but they operate through secondary causes. God does not override human activity; He accomplishes His purposes precisely through human action. The certainty of the decree does not make the action unnecessary—it makes the action real."

Jonathan Edwards (1703–1758)

"God governs the world by persuading and inciting men to do that which He decrees. He does not act independent of the means. He ordains both the decree and the human action through which it comes to pass. In no way does His sovereignty diminish human responsibility."

John Calvin (1509–1564)

"The preaching of the gospel is the appointed means by which God calls His elect. God does not save men apart from the Word. He saves them through it. The decree does not make the means unnecessary—it makes them essential."

William Carey (1761–1834)

"I have now fully proved, by the experience of near forty years, that the sovereignty of God is perfectly consistent with the most ardent exertions for the salvation of men. The decree is not an obstacle to missions. It is the very foundation of my confidence."

Charles Spurgeon (1834–1892)

"It is a great thing to believe that God is in earnest with us, that He has purposes, and that He will accomplish them. When I know that God has chosen me, I am stirred to the greatest possible diligence in preaching the gospel. I preach all the harder because I know God's elect will hear my voice."

J.I. Packer (Modern Era)

"When you believe that God is sovereign, that He has a plan, that He has chosen His people, it is not that you do less in evangelism. It is that you do it with greater assurance. You know that when you preach Christ, God's purposes will be accomplished."

"The decree doesn't compete with the command. The decree accomplishes itself through the command. This is not a paradox—it is compatibilism. God ordains the end and the means. He commands us because He has already predetermined that His people will respond. This is not confusion. This is the foundation upon which apostolic boldness stands."

The Question That Ends the Debate

Here is the question no one who raises this objection has ever honestly answered:

If commands prove the ability to obey, then why can't you be perfect?

Jesus commands perfection: "Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (Matthew 5:48). God commands total love: "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind" (Matthew 22:37). If God's commands prove human ability, then you have the capacity to be sinlessly perfect right now. Today. This moment.

Do you? Has anyone in the history of the human race obeyed those commands perfectly? Then either God issues commands that prove nothing about human ability — in which case your entire objection collapses — or God has been mocking humanity for six thousand years with demands He knows we can't meet. Those are your only two options. Pick one.

The objector always picks Door One when it comes to the command to be perfect. They know they can't be perfect. They understand that God's standard reveals something about God, not something about human capacity. But when it comes to the command to believe, suddenly they switch frameworks. Suddenly commands prove ability. Suddenly "believe" means you can believe on your own. The inconsistency is breathtaking — and it reveals that the objection is not about logic at all. It is about protecting the one contribution the flesh refuses to surrender: the belief that you, at the decisive moment, did something God couldn't do without you.

This is the bootstrap paradox of Arminian theology: you cannot pull yourself into existence, and you cannot pull yourself into spiritual life. The command to believe is not proof that you have bootstrapping capability. It is the voice of the God who creates what He commands — who says "Let there be light" and light happens, who says "Lazarus, come forth" and a dead man walks, who says "Believe" and grants the very faith He demands.

"The command is not the test of your ability. It is the vehicle of His power. He doesn't command the dead to rise because they can. He commands them because He can."

A Pastoral Word

If you are reading this because you feel paralyzed — because you've heard about God's sovereignty and your instinct is to give up on prayer, evangelism, or trying — let me be direct: you are listening to the flesh, not Scripture.

The lie whispers, "If God is sovereign, why try?" But this entire page shows you the answer: God is sovereign through your trying. He works through your prayers. He saves through your witness. You are not a passenger in God's plan. You are a participant in it. Your obedience matters. Your prayers move the hand of God. Your testimony changes eternal destinies. This is not burden. This is purpose. This is what it means to be chosen.

And there is a deeper comfort here too. If you've been paralyzed by self-doubt — wondering whether your faith is real, whether you truly chose God, whether you could ever measure up — here is the mercy: you don't have to. God chose you. He secured your faith. He will carry you through to the end. Your job is not to hold onto salvation. Your job is to rest in the grip of the God who chose you before you were broken. If that sounds too good to be true, you may be experiencing the imposter syndrome that grace itself dissolves.

Think about what it means that God commanded you to come — and then carried you there. He didn't stand at a distance and shout instructions to a paralyzed person. He spoke, and in the speaking, He moved your legs. The command and the power arrived together, in the same breath, from the same mouth, aimed at the same heart — yours. That's not the behavior of a God who is indifferent to your participation. That's the behavior of a Father who wants His child close enough to hear His voice. Surrendering to this truth is not losing your freedom. It is finding it for the first time.

The Truth: Commands as the Very Substance of God's Decree

God has decreed the salvation of His elect. This decree is certain, unchangeable, and glorious. But—and here is where most people go spiritually numb—God has not decreed it apart from anything. He has not decreed it in some ethereal realm disconnected from reality. He has decreed it through the preaching of the gospel, through prayer, through human obedience to His commands. The decree and the means are not in tension. They are one.

The objection assumes a false either/or: either God's decree is absolute or human commands matter. Scripture teaches a glorious both/and: God's decree is absolute precisely because it secures the means. The decree doesn't bypass commands—it accomplishes itself through commands. The decree doesn't make prayer pointless—it makes prayer the very mechanism by which His will comes to pass on earth as it is in heaven.

This changes everything. It means every command you obey is not wasted effort. It is the very substance of God's decree taking shape in real time. Every prayer you pray is not futile—it is part of the causal chain God ordained before time existed. Every gospel conversation is not meaningless—it is the hammer and chisel God uses to carve His purposes into history.

So preach. Pray. Command. Evangelize. Obey. Not because you must inform God of your desires or convince Him to act. But because the outcome is already certain—God has ordained it—and the means are already real—God has ordained them too. This is not a burden. This is freedom. This is the gospel.

Think about what this means for you personally. The prayer you prayed last night? God ordained it before the stars existed — and He ordained the answer too. The conversation about Jesus you had with your coworker? That wasn't random. That was a link in an eternal chain forged before the foundation of the world. You are not shouting into a void. You are participating in a rescue operation that was planned before time began — and you were chosen to be part of it. Not because you volunteered. Because He chose you for it.

The person who grasps this stops praying less and starts praying with fire. Because now they know every prayer lands. Every act of obedience matters. Every word of witness is a thread in a tapestry God designed before the first star ignited. You are not playing a game God is watching — you are a move God is making.

If you want to wrestle with this at the deepest level, Romans 9 is the place where God Himself confronts this very objection directly — and nowhere else does Scripture press the question of God's sovereign choice more relentlessly, more beautifully, or with more pastoral tenderness toward the struggling soul. And if you want to understand why this truth feels threatening rather than liberating, the answer lies in the gap between what your heart rejects and what your mind already knows.
