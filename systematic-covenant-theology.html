Systematic Theology

One Plan, One People, One Mediator

God has never had two plans of salvation. From Eden to Calvary, one covenant of grace runs through all of redemptive history, sealed in the blood of one Mediator.

TL;DR: Most people read the Bible like a library — sixty-six disconnected books. But it is a single contract. Before time began, the Trinity agreed on a plan: the Father would choose a people, the Son would redeem them, the Spirit would bring them to life. That agreement — the Covenant of Redemption — is the foundation beneath every page of Scripture. Adam broke the Covenant of Works. Christ fulfilled it as the second Adam. And the Covenant of Grace, first whispered in Genesis 3:15, runs like a golden thread through Abraham, Moses, David, and the prophets until it is sealed forever in Christ's blood. One plan. One people. One Mediator. Your salvation was not Plan B.

Not a Library — A Contract

Most people read the Bible like a library. Genesis over here. Revelation over there. Paul doing his thing. Moses doing his. Somewhere in the middle, Jesus shows up and changes the subject.

But what if it is not a library at all? What if it is a contract — a single, unbreakable agreement that God made with Himself before time began, and every page of Scripture is a different clause in the same document? What if Abraham and David and Paul and you are not characters in separate stories but beneficiaries of the same will — a will signed in blood before the foundation of the world and irrevocable because the One who signed it cannot lie, cannot fail, and cannot die?

That is covenant theology. And it changes everything about how you read your Bible — and how you understand why you are saved.

The Agreement Before Time

Before God spoke a word to Adam, the Godhead made an eternal agreement regarding the redemption of the elect. Theologians call it the Pactum Salutis — the Covenant of Redemption. The Father chose a people and gave them to the Son. The Son agreed to take on human flesh, keep the law perfectly as their representative, and bear the penalty of their sin in His death. The Spirit agreed to apply the redemption Christ purchased — to regenerate, unite to Christ, and conform to His image every soul the Father chose.

This is not speculation. Jesus prays about it openly: "Father, I want those you have given me to be with me where I am, and to see my glory, the glory you have given me because you loved me before the creation of the world" (John 17:24). The Father gave. The Son received. The Spirit applies. Your salvation was planned in a Trinitarian council before the stars existed. It was never contingent on your decision — it was settled in eternity by persons who cannot fail.

The Covenant That Broke — and the One Who Kept It

In Eden, God established a covenant with Adam as the federal head of the human race. The terms were simple: obey and live, disobey and die. Hosea 6:7 calls it what it was: "Like Adam, they have broken the covenant." Adam broke it. And because he represented all humanity, his failure was ours. "Sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and in this way death came to all people, because all sinned" (Romans 5:12).

This is the Covenant of Works, and every human being ever born has entered the world under its broken verdict: guilty, condemned, dead. Not because you personally ate the fruit but because your representative did — and federal headship means his failure counts as yours, just as surely as a treaty signed by a king binds every citizen of the realm.

But here is where the contract metaphor becomes breathtaking. The Covenant of Works required perfect obedience. Adam could not provide it. Neither can you — not for a single hour, not on your best day. You have never loved God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength for even one unbroken moment. The standard is the holiness of God Himself, and measured against that, your finest righteousness is what Isaiah called it: filthy rags.

So God sent a second Adam. Christ entered the Covenant of Works on your behalf, kept every clause perfectly, and then bore the penalty you owed for breaking it. His perfect obedience is credited to your account. His death pays your debt. This is justification — not a legal fiction but a covenantal transfer: the second Adam's record replaces the first Adam's, and God declares you righteous on the basis of a righteousness that is not your own.

"For just as through the disobedience of the one man the many were made sinners, so also through the obedience of the one man the many will be made righteous."

ROMANS 5:19

One Thread Through Every Page

The Covenant of Grace — God's promise to save His people through a Mediator — did not begin at Calvary. It began in Genesis 3:15, the moment sin entered the world: "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and hers; he will crush your head, and you will strike his heel." The Seed of the woman would come. Redemption was promised before the exile from Eden was complete.

From there, every covenant in Scripture is a progressive unveiling of the same promise. God narrows the line to Abraham: from your seed, all nations will be blessed — and Paul tells us that "seed" is singular, pointing to Christ (Galatians 3:16). He gives the law through Moses — not as a second plan of salvation, but as a tutor: "The law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we might be justified by faith" (Galatians 3:24). He promises David an eternal throne — fulfilled not in Solomon but in the Son of David who reigns forever. He promises through Jeremiah a new covenant: "I will put my law in their minds and write it on their hearts" (Jeremiah 31:33) — not a replacement of grace but its final, internal, permanent administration.

One thread. One promise. One Mediator. The entire Old Testament is a forward motion toward Christ, which is why Jesus could walk two disciples through "Moses and all the Prophets" and explain "what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself" (Luke 24:27). He was not adding Himself to the story. He was revealing that He had been the story all along.

Why This Changes Everything About Your Salvation

If the Bible is a library, then you are responsible for finding the right book at the right time and making the right choice. If the Bible is a contract, then your salvation was written into the terms before you were born — and the only One whose faithfulness matters is the One who signed it.

Covenant theology does not merely reorganize your theology. It relocates the foundation of your assurance. Your election was not God's reaction to your foreseen faith — it was His decision, made in the Covenant of Redemption, before time began. Your justification is not a reward for your cooperation — it is a covenantal transfer accomplished by the second Adam on your behalf. Your perseverance is not your responsibility to maintain — it is God's promise to keep, because He is the guarantor of the covenant, not you.

And your faith? If the entire covenant flows from God — if the Father chose, the Son redeemed, and the Spirit applies — then even your ability to believe is a gift within the covenant, not your contribution to it. To claim that your faith is the one thing you brought to the table is to misunderstand the contract entirely. You did not sign this covenant. You were named in it — by Someone who does not forget, does not fail, and does not let go.

"Now may the God of peace, who through the blood of the eternal covenant brought back from the dead our Lord Jesus, that great Shepherd of the sheep, equip you with everything good for doing his will."

HEBREWS 13:20-21

The blood of the eternal covenant. Not a new plan. The plan — finally and fully sealed in the God-man who walked out of the grave. For the full order of salvation this covenant produces, see how every step belongs to God. For the glory of the Mediator at the center of every covenant, see who Jesus actually is. And if what you have read here feels less like theology and more like the ground shifting beneath your feet — there is Someone who will not let you fall.
