Printable · The Effectual Call

The Voice That Wakes the Dead

The general invitation goes out to all. The particular summons raises the dead. Romans 8:30, 1 Corinthians 1:24, John 6:44, John 11:43 — the call no corpse can refuse, distilled to one printable page.

Designed to print on one US Letter or A4 page. Use your browser's Print function (Ctrl/Cmd + P). Tuck it into the back of a Bible at John 6. Hand it to a friend who still believes their faith began with their decision.

Print This Page →

1. The Two Calls

Scripture knows two calls, and the difference between them is the difference between every funeral that ends in mourning and every funeral that ends in resurrection. The first is the general call — the invitation that goes out to every ear that hears the gospel preached, the offer that floods the marketplace, the door knocked upon the house of every man and woman alive. It is universal, sincere, and entirely insufficient to raise a corpse. The second is the effectual call — the particular summons that does what the general call cannot. The first call invites. The second call awakens. The first goes through the air. The second goes through the grave. Romans 8:30 names the second when it says, "And those he predestined, he also called." That call never returns empty.

2. The Lazarus Summons

"Lazarus, come out!" Four days dead. The body already turning. The sisters already past hope. And Jesus stands at the mouth of the tomb and does not invite — He commands. He does not whisper, if you would like to live, please consider the option. He shouts. He uses the man's name. And the verb that follows in John 11:44 is one of the most stunning in Scripture: the dead man came out. Lazarus did not contribute fifty percent to his resurrection. Lazarus did not contribute one percent to his resurrection. Lazarus contributed the corpse. Christ contributed the voice, the power, the breath, the eyelids that opened, the lungs that filled, the legs that obeyed. The general call goes out into a graveyard. The effectual call names a grave and the grave gives up its dead.

3. The Greek — καλέω (kaleō)

The verb behind every "called" in the New Testament's salvation passages is καλέω (kaleō) — to call, to summon, to name. It is not the verb of an open invitation. It is the verb of a sovereign address. When Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 1:24 of "those whom God has called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God," he is not describing a generic offer; he is describing a particular naming. The same verse, two clauses earlier, has just said to those who are perishing the message of the cross is foolishness. Same gospel. Same words preached. Two reactions. Why? Because in one ear the words are sound; in the other ear the words are summons. The difference is not in the volume. The difference is in the call.

4. The Chain — Romans 8:30

"And those he predestined, he also called; those he called, he also justified; those he justified, he also glorified." Read the verbs. Predestined. Called. Justified. Glorified. All four are aorist; from heaven's vantage every one is a closed event. And note what is missing — there is no leak. There is no slippage. There is no number that enters at predestined and a smaller number that emerges at glorified. The chain of Romans 8:30 has no link that fails, because the same Hand that forged the first link forges every link after it. The called are called all the way home. If you are reading this with a heart that is breaking under the weight of the gospel, that breaking is the call.

5. The Distinguishing Mark — John 6:44 and 6:65

Jesus said it in a sentence the church has spent two thousand years trying to dilute: "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws them." And then, in case anyone missed it, He said it again sixteen verses later: "This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless the Father has enabled them." The word for draws is ἑλκύσῃ (helkysē) — the same verb the Gospel of John uses for dragging a net of fish onto the shore. It is not persuasion. It is not enticement. It is traction. The fish do not vote on whether to be drawn. They are drawn because the One pulling the net has the strength the catch does not. The dead do not draw themselves. The drawn do not draw themselves. The Father draws. And every fish in the net is in the net because the net was thrown precisely to gather them.

6. The Steel-Man — "But What About the Free Offer?"

Hear the objection at full strength: If only the called are saved, why preach to all? Why does Paul plead and weep? Why does Jesus stand on a hillside and say "come to me, all you who are weary"? The free offer is real. The general call is sincere. The pleading is honest. Granted, every word of it. The general call is real. The pleading is sincere. God means it when He says, turn, turn — for why will you die? But the same Bible that tells us the call goes out to all tells us the call comes home only when the Spirit does the calling. The two are not in competition; they are in cooperation. We preach to every ear. The Spirit raises the corpses He chooses to raise. The general call is the lifeguard's shout from the shore. The effectual call is the lifeguard going into the water.

7. The Catch — Where the Hammer Becomes a Hand

If you came, He called. If your heart is loosening as you read these words, the loosening is His doing. You did not contrive your willingness; your willingness was the first sign of His work. Even the faith you reach with was a faith given to your hand by the same Hand that called your name out of the dark. So rest. The voice that woke you is the voice that holds you, and the One who began the work in you is the same One who will never give up. You were rescued without a say in the rescue, because the corpse had no say to give. The summons came. The grave answered. The Hands that called you are the Hands that hold you, and they have been at work on you since before the foundation of the world.
