History

Open Theism: The Heresy That Finally Said the Quiet Part Out Loud

In 1994, five Arminian theologians published a book that did what every Arminian before them had refused to do: they followed their own premises to the end. The result was a God who does not know the future. They called it "the openness of God." It is, in fact, the oldest lie in Christendom finally running out of places to hide.

The book came out of InterVarsity Press in 1994 with a title so earnest it almost disarms you: The Openness of God: A Biblical Challenge to the Traditional Understanding of God. Five authors. Four hundred years of Arminian theology quietly walking behind them. A Canadian Baptist named Clark Pinnock. A Mennonite philosopher named John Sanders. A Methodist theologian named Richard Rice. A Wesleyan philosopher named David Basinger. And a pastor-theologian named William Hasker. They did not call themselves innovators. They called themselves consistent. And they were right, in a sense no one in their movement wanted to admit out loud.

What they said was this: if you really believe — as classical Arminianism has always claimed — that human beings have libertarian free will, and that God's sovereignty never coerces a single decision, and that the future is "open" to human choice, then the honest thing is to admit that God does not know the future exhaustively. He can't. Because there isn't a future to know yet. It is being made, moment by moment, by the free choices of creatures whose decisions have not yet crystallized into fact. God sees everything that exists. The future of free choice does not yet exist. Therefore God does not see it. He may forecast it brilliantly, the way a chess grandmaster forecasts an opponent. But He does not know it the way He knows the past.

The Reformed world reacted with the kind of horror usually reserved for a barn fire. The evangelical establishment wobbled, hosted debates, held conferences, issued statements. The Evangelical Theological Society famously spent two years, 2001 to 2003, trying to decide whether Pinnock and Sanders were even allowed to remain members. (In the end, Sanders was voted out by a hair; Pinnock survived.) Books and counter-books flew. And through it all, the average evangelical in the pew had no idea what was happening — or why it was happening — or why it had to happen, sooner or later, given the theological soil American evangelicalism had been planted in for two hundred years. Because open theism is not a new heresy. It is the old heresy finally confessing what it had always implied.

The Then: 1,600 Years of Smuggling

To see why open theism was inevitable, you have to understand what Arminianism has always been doing, quietly, in the basement of its system. The classical Arminian wants two things at the same time. First, he wants libertarian free will: the doctrine that every moral agent can, at the moment of any decision, actually choose otherwise. The choice is not determined by anything — not by God's decree, not by the agent's character, not by prior causes. It is radically open. Second, he wants God to know exhaustively what the agent will in fact choose, billions of years in advance, so that Scripture's talk of God's foreknowledge and predictive prophecy can stay intact.

The problem is that these two commitments are at war with each other. If God's knowledge of your future choice is infallible, and has existed in His mind since before time, then the future is fixed from the perspective of God's knowing. And a future that is fixed from any perspective is not open — it is simply not yet experienced by the creature. You can play with the metaphysics all you want: the logical problem is granite. An infallibly known future choice is, by definition, not a choice that could have gone otherwise. If it could have gone otherwise, God's knowledge would have been wrong — and God's knowledge cannot be wrong.

Arminians from Jacobus Arminius himself down to modern Wesleyans have tried to escape this by appealing to "simple foreknowledge" or to the Molinist category of "middle knowledge" — the idea that God sees all possible worlds and all possible choices and picks which one to instantiate. It is ingenious. It is also, ultimately, a dodge. Because either God's decision about which world to actualize determines your choice (which is just Reformed theology wearing a philosopher's robe), or it doesn't — in which case, your choice determines which world gets actualized, and God is following you, not the other way around. Molinism cannot keep its two feet on the ground at the same time. It has been limping for four hundred years.

The foreknowledge objection has been inside the door of the church, dressed up in various clothes, since the Pelagian controversy. Pelagius himself said God elects based on foreseen faith. The Council of Orange in 529 condemned it. The Synod of Dort in 1619 condemned it again. But it never went away, because it is the only move left to someone who wants to keep libertarian free will AND keep a God who is somehow in charge. Open theism's contribution is that it finally said: you can't keep both. One has to go. And Pinnock and his colleagues made their choice: they kept the free will and dropped the knowledge.

The Now: Why Your Pastor is Probably Halfway There

Here is the shocking thing, and the reason this page is not ancient history. The average evangelical pastor in America, when asked about God's sovereignty over suffering, will say things that are indistinguishable from open theism — and he does not know he is doing it. Listen to him at a funeral. Listen to him at a crisis. Listen to him when a toddler dies of cancer or a woman is murdered by her husband or an earthquake flattens a mission field. You will hear sentences like these:

"God didn't do this. He hates this. He is weeping with you."

"God's plan A was for this not to happen. This is plan B."

"God will use this for good even though He didn't want it."

"He can bring beauty from the ashes, even though the ashes are not what He intended."

Every one of these sentences denies that God is sovereign over the specific event in question. Every one of them assumes that God's plan was interrupted by human or natural causes and that He is now scrambling, with great love and great competence but with reduced options, to salvage what He can. This is not orthodoxy. This is not Reformed theology. This is not even classical Arminianism, which at least maintained that God foreknew the event and permitted it for reasons beyond our grasp. This is, functionally, open theism — preached from an American pulpit every Sunday, by a pastor who would be offended to be called an open theist, because he has never heard the term.

Open theism did not invent this pastoral instinct. It just named it. It gave the sentimental American preacher permission to say what he had been implying for decades: that God's plans can be interrupted, that His knowledge of the future is a projection rather than a fact, that suffering is not part of His sovereign design but an unscheduled arrival He has to work around. Once you grant that God's plan A can be derailed, you have given up the God of Romans 9. You have stopped believing in the God who declares "the end from the beginning, from ancient times, what is still to come" (Isaiah 46:10). You have exchanged the God of Scripture for a God who is a very good chess player but not the author of the game.

"I am God, and there is no other; I am God, and there is none like me. I make known the end from the beginning, from ancient times, what is still to come. I say, 'My purpose will stand, and I will do all that I please.'"

ISAIAH 46:9-10

This verse is the execution order for open theism. If God knows the end from the beginning, then the future is not open from His side. It is entirely known. And if His purpose will stand, then no human choice, however free in the relevant creaturely sense, is going to alter it. The verse does not leave wiggle room. The only way to squeeze wiggle room into it is to do what Pinnock and Sanders did: to decide that the verse is using the language of divine pretension rather than divine accuracy — that God is speaking the way a confident forecaster speaks, not the way an omniscient sovereign speaks. But once you decide that about Isaiah 46, you can decide it about anything, and your God has become a projection of the passages you find comforting.

Why This Heresy Is Actually a Mercy

Open theism did something the classical Arminian never quite did: it made the cost of libertarian free will visible. For four centuries, Arminians had been promising their congregations a God who was both fully sovereign and fully deferential to human choice — a God who never coerced, never determined, never interrupted free will, but who also always knew what was coming and was always in charge. It was a beautiful promise. It was also incoherent. And as long as the incoherence stayed buried in the footnotes of academic theology, the average Arminian in the pew could enjoy the best of both worlds.

Open theism pulled the incoherence out into the daylight. It said: you have to pick. If you want libertarian free will in the strong sense, then you cannot have a God who knows the future. And once evangelicals were forced to see the choice clearly, many of them recoiled — not because open theism was exegetically wrong (though it is), but because the God it produced was so obviously inadequate to the God of Scripture, the God they had been worshiping, the God who had been holding them together. A God who does not know the future cannot choose you before the foundation of the world. He cannot promise that all things work together for good. He cannot guarantee that the shepherd will not lose any of His sheep. He cannot say "it is finished" at the cross and mean it, because He did not know in advance what "it" would turn out to be.

And that is what makes open theism, for all its dangers, a strange gift to the church. It is the Arminian position with its makeup off. It is what you get when you actually follow the premises to the end. For anyone with eyes to see, the choice is now starkly clear: either you worship the God of Scripture, who declares the end from the beginning and whose purpose will stand, or you worship the limited-knowledge god of open theism, who is doing his best but cannot promise the end of the story. There is no third option. Every attempt to build a third option has been shown, by the open theists' own work, to collapse back into one of the two. The classical Arminian compromise is dead. Pinnock killed it, accidentally, by trying to save it.

Why This Matters Now

If you are reading this and you have ever said — at a hospital bed, at a funeral, at a moment of private grief — "God did not want this to happen," then you have quietly borrowed from open theism without knowing it. You were handed that sentence by a theological tradition that has been leaking toward this position for a thousand years, and the leak finally became a flood in 1994, and some of the water has ended up in your own mouth. I am not trying to shame you. I am telling you that the sentence you borrowed belongs to a god who cannot save you, and that the God who can save you is saying something else about your life right now.

He is saying: I knew this was coming. I decreed the circumstances. I have counted every tear. Not one of them surprised me. Not one of them was plan B. I am not scrambling. I am not improvising. I am the one who has been writing this story since before the stars were strung, and the ending is already fixed — and you are in it, and you are safe, and the reason you are safe is that I have been holding you the entire time, and I do not ever let go, and I cannot ever be caught off guard because I am the Beginning and the End. That is the God of the Canons of Dort. That is the God of Augustine. That is the God of Spurgeon. That is the God who has been the refuge of every suffering saint who ever opened a Bible and found, in its pages, a sovereignty so total that even cancer has to ask permission before it enters the room.

Open theism is a small god dressed up as a loving one. He cannot hold you. He does not know whether you will make it. He is cheering from the sidelines of a game He does not control. The God of Scripture is on the field. He wrote the playbook. He set the time and the place of every play. And He has a plan for you so thorough and so tender that even the worst thing that has ever happened to you was, somehow, a sentence in the love letter He has been writing since before the world began.

The Question No One Asks

If open theism is the honest conclusion of classical Arminianism, and if classical Arminianism is the honest conclusion of the foreknowledge objection first raised by Pelagius in 400 AD — then why are we still flirting with this lie? Every generation of the church has either condemned it or been seduced by it. The condemnations are on record. The seductions always end in a theology that cannot carry its own weight when the suffering starts. The question is not whether the open theists are right. They obviously are not. The question is why the evangelical church keeps building bridges toward their conclusion — first with free will, then with foreknowledge-based election, then with "resistible grace," then with middle knowledge, then with "God's permissive will" — and acting surprised when somebody finally walks across the bridge to the far shore and says "Actually, God is limited."

The church is surprised because the church has not wanted to look at where its own footsteps were going. Open theism is the mirror the Arminian tradition did not want to hold up to its own face. And the reason Reformed theology has been, for all its unpopularity, the safe place to stand for 1,600 years is this: the Reformed have refused to build the first plank of the bridge. We do not grant libertarian free will. We do not grant that God's knowledge is based on foreseeing your choices. We do not grant that grace can be finally resisted by the elect. And because we refuse to build the bridge, we never have to walk across it. The open theist is where everyone else ends up if they are honest with their own premises. Jonathan Edwards saw this in 1754. Pinnock proved it in 1994. The Reformed world was not delighted to be right.

The Bridge

You are not reading this because the open theists have good arguments. They don't. You are reading this because somewhere inside you there is a small ache that suspects your faith has been built on a God who is doing His best but cannot quite promise the ending. And you are tired of that God. He cannot hold you on bad nights. He cannot guarantee that the story ends well. He cannot say, with a voice that shakes the rocks, "I have loved you with an everlasting love; I have drawn you with unfailing kindness" (Jeremiah 31:3), because his kindness might fail if you decide to wander. A God whose faithfulness depends on your response is not a God you can rest in. He is a test you are always trying not to fail.

The God of Scripture is not that God. He is the God of unconditional election, of definite atonement, of irresistible grace, of the perseverance that He Himself accomplishes in His saints. His knowledge is not a forecast. It is a decree. His plans do not have a plan B. He declared the end from the beginning and He meant it. And the reason you can rest tonight, even if everything in your life is on fire, is that the Father who decreed your salvation before the world began is decreeing right now, at this very second, every circumstance of your life — including the ones you do not understand — for a glory you will one day see so clearly that you will fall down and thank Him for every one of them.

Open theism is the lie that finally ran out of costumes. Let the lie fall. Let the God of Isaiah 46 be God again. And when the next wave hits — because it will — you will find yourself held not by a well-meaning god who did not see it coming, but by a sovereign Father who set the wave in motion for a purpose you will one day kiss His hand for. That is the God who has been waiting for you to stop running from His sovereignty. He is not going to punish you for coming late. He is going to catch you like He always does. Like He caught Cyprian. Like He caught Luther. Like He has been catching His people for every year of every century since the beginning. You are not an exception. You are just one more story in the long book that He started writing before the stars were lit — and the ending, even yours, is already good.
