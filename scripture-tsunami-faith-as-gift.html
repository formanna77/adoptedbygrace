Scripture Tsunami: Faith Is a Gift

From Genesis to Revelation, Scripture speaks with one voice: saving faith originates in God, not in you. Here is the evidence — all of it.

By Aaron Forman · January 1, 2026

The Truth: Over 40 passages across the entire biblical canon teach that faith is a gift from God, not a human achievement. You can explain away one verse. You can reinterpret two. But when Moses, David, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Jesus, Luke, Paul, Peter, James, and John all say the same thing — at some point the weight of testimony becomes undeniable. This is that point.

Stand on the Shore

Before the verses come, a picture. Stand on a long, flat coastline — sand so pale it is almost white, the tide pulled out farther than you have ever seen it pulled. A seashell in the wet sand. A gull lifting. The wind with that particular salt-and-iron smell that means something is coming. One verse reaches you first. Deuteronomy 29:4 — a ripple, ankle-high, warm, gone before you have time to step back. You decide it was nothing. Then another. Ezekiel 36:26 — to your knees, colder, and this one stays a moment before it pulls back. You tell yourself the first two were anomalies. Then John 6:44. Romans 8:29-30. Ephesians 2:8-9. And now, out on the horizon, the line darkens. A wall. Dark green, quiet, moving at the speed of a thought. Forty verses deep. Fifteen centuries wide. Written on three continents, in three languages, by men who never met — and every single one of them is saying the same thing, and it is all traveling toward you, and there is nowhere to stand that the water will not reach.

That is this page. What follows is not an argument. It is a shoreline.

The Old Testament: God Gives the Heart

Deuteronomy 29:4 — "But to this day the LORD has not given you a heart to understand or eyes to see or ears to hear." Spiritual perception is something the LORD gives. Without it, even those who witnessed the miracles of the Exodus remained blind.

Deuteronomy 30:6 — "The LORD your God will circumcise your hearts and the hearts of your descendants, so that you may love him with all your heart and with all your soul, and live." God circumcises the heart. The result is love for God. You do not circumcise your own heart.

1 Kings 18:37 — Elijah prays that God would reveal "that you have turned their hearts back." Repentance is a work of God, not the achievement of the repenter.

Psalm 65:4 — "Blessed are those you choose and bring near to live in your courts!" Two actions: God chooses, God brings near. Neither is performed by the person being blessed.

Proverbs 21:1 — "In the LORD's hand the king's heart is a stream of water that he channels toward all who please him." If God directs the hearts of kings, He directs all hearts.

Jeremiah 24:7 — "I will give them a heart to know me." Knowing God begins with a gift. The return to God is the result of the gift, not the cause of it.

Ezekiel 36:26-27 — "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit in you; I will remove from you your heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit in you and move you to follow my decrees." Four first-person divine actions. Zero human contributions. The stony heart does not soften itself. It is replaced.

The Gospels: Jesus Teaches It Plainly

Matthew 11:25-27 — "I praise you, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, because you have hidden these things from the wise and learned, and revealed them to little children. Yes, Father, for this is what you were pleased to do." Jesus attributes both the hiding and the revealing to the Father's will.

Matthew 16:17 — "Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by flesh and blood, but by my Father in heaven." Peter's confession did not originate in Peter. The Father revealed it.

John 6:37 — "All those the Father gives me will come to me." The Father gives people to the Son. They will come. The giving precedes the coming.

John 6:44 — "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws them." Not "no one will" — "no one can." Coming to Christ is an inability without divine drawing.

John 10:26-27 — "You do not believe because you are not my sheep." The order is devastating: being a sheep comes first, believing comes second. Sheephood is the cause of faith, not the result.

John 15:16 — "You did not choose me, but I chose you." Christ could not be more explicit.

John 1:12-13 — Those who received Him "Yet to all who did receive him, to those who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God—children born not of natural descent, nor of human decision or a husband's will, but born of God." The believing is real. The origin of the believing is divine.

John 3:8 — "The wind blows wherever it pleases... So it is with everyone born of the Spirit." Regeneration is like wind — you do not control it.

Acts: The Early Church Understood It

Acts 13:48 — "All who were appointed for eternal life believed." Those appointed believed. Not: those who believed were then appointed. Appointment precedes and produces belief.

Acts 16:14 — "The Lord opened her heart to respond to Paul's message." Lydia did not open her own heart. The Lord opened it.

Acts 11:18 — "So then, even to Gentiles God has granted repentance that leads to life." Repentance is granted. Not achieved. Granted.

The Epistles: Paul, Peter, James, and John Hammer It Home

Ephesians 2:8-9 — "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God — not by works, so that no one can boast." The entire package — grace, salvation, and the faith through which it comes — is a gift. If faith were your contribution, you could boast.

Philippians 1:29 — "For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him." Believing has been granted. The Greek echaristhē makes faith a grace gift.

Philippians 2:13 — "For it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose." Not just the doing — the willing itself is God's work.

Romans 9:16 — "It does not, therefore, depend on human desire or effort, but on God's mercy." Paul eliminates both internal disposition and external effort. Only divine mercy remains.

Romans 8:29-30 — "For those God foreknew he also predestined... called... justified... glorified." Every link is forged by God. The human does not appear as the subject of a single verb.

1 Corinthians 4:7 — "What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?" The question that ends all boasting. If faith is received, it is a gift. If it is a gift, you cannot take credit.

2 Corinthians 4:6 — "For God, who said, 'Let light shine out of darkness,' made his light shine in our hearts." Conversion is creation — the same sovereign word that made the universe.

2 Timothy 2:25 — "...in the hope that God will grant them repentance leading them to a knowledge of the truth." Repentance is something God grants, at His discretion.

Hebrews 12:2 — "...fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith." Jesus is the archēgos — founder, originator — of faith. He starts it. He perfects it.

James 1:18 — "He chose to give us birth through the word of truth." Regeneration is by God's will, not ours. James — the epistle of works — affirms that spiritual life begins with divine initiative.

1 John 5:1 — "Everyone who believes that Jesus is the Christ is born of God." The Greek tense is decisive: "believes" (present active) and "has been born" (perfect passive). The being-born-of-God is completed before the believing.

1 Peter 1:3 — "In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope." God actively regenerated us. Not: we decided to be born again. He caused it.

Revelation 13:8 — "...the Lamb who was slain from the creation of the world." Names written in the Book of Life before you existed. Before you could believe. Before you could choose.

The Cumulative Weight

Forty verses. Zero exceptions. At what point does the weight of testimony become something other than coincidence?

If even one of these verses means what it plainly says, the Arminian framework collapses. But it is not one verse. It is forty. What theology requires you to explain away forty passages to survive?

The biblical authors did not hold a committee meeting to coordinate their testimony. They wrote across fifteen centuries, three continents, and multiple languages — and they all said the same thing. Conspiracy theorists have worked with less.

Notice What Your Mind Just Did

As you read the list, something moved in you. Probably more than once. Somewhere around verse twelve or fifteen or twenty-seven, a small mental posture adjusted itself — the posture of the reader who needs these verses to mean something other than what they say. Maybe it was a sentence that began in your head with "But surely that one means —" Maybe it was the urge to read the next verse less carefully so the weight of the first one had time to fade. Maybe it was a quiet, almost imperceptible bargaining: "Thirty-eight of them could be gifts. But mine was still mine."

Watch that reflex. Name it. That reflex is not your intellect protecting Scripture from a heresy. That reflex is your interior architect protecting a building — a very old, very tired, very small building with your name on the door — from a tide that is about to flatten it. You did not build that building on purpose. You inherited it. You were born inside it. Its central load-bearing wall is this: I am the author of what happens to me. And every verse on this page is a sledgehammer aimed at that wall. Which is why you cannot read forty of them and remain neutral. Nobody can. You will either walk out of this page still defending the wall — and you will call it discernment, you will call it careful exegesis, you will call it "balance" — or you will let the wall go and discover, for the first time, that what you thought was a house was actually a cage, and the tide that just broke it open was the kindness of a God who refused to leave you in it.

The reflex that wants to rescue thirty-eight of these verses from meaning what they say is not a theological reflex. It is autobiographical. It is the part of you that still wants a single square inch of the salvation story to have your handwriting on it somewhere.

The Tide Comes In

Stop for a moment. If your faith came from you — if you generated it, activated it, chose it — then you have something to boast about. Paul says you do not. One of you is wrong.

Go back to the shoreline. The tide is no longer on the horizon. It is at your feet. Forty verses deep. It is over your knees now, your waist, your chest. And here is the thing you did not expect. You thought you were going to drown. You thought if the wall fell — if the architect of your self-trust was finally silenced — you would disappear beneath the water. Instead, you discover something Lazarus discovered and Jonah discovered and Job discovered and Aaron Forman discovered on Christmas Day 2024: the tide that breaks your building was carrying a Man across it all along. And when the water closes over your head — the head that no longer has a wall to hide behind — you find you are breathing. Not because you are strong. Because the water is Him. And the hand around your wrist is the hand of the One who wrote your name in His book before the foundation of the world, and He is not letting go. He has not been letting go since Deuteronomy. He has not been letting go since before there were continents for the verses to be written on.

And the God who gave you faith will never let you go.
