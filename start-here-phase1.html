Phase 1 — Curiosity

What Is Reformed Theology?

Before we open a single page of Scripture, let's lay our cards on the table. What do these Christians actually believe about God and salvation — and why does it set their souls ablaze?

The Word "Reformed" — What Does It Mean?

When we say "Reformed theology," we're not talking about a denomination or a club with a secret handshake. We're talking about a set of convictions about who God is and how He saves — convictions that Christians have held since the ink dried on the apostles' letters, that were recovered with earth-shaking force during the Protestant Reformation, and that continue to shape millions of believers across the globe today.

The name comes from the Latin reformata — "formed again." The Reformers believed the church had drifted dangerously from what the Bible actually teaches about salvation, and they called Christians back to the text. Not to novel ideas or clever innovations — to ancient truths that had been buried under centuries of human tradition.

At its heart, this theology makes one audacious, terrifying, glorious claim:

The Central Claim
Salvation is entirely the work of God — from first to last.
God chooses who will be saved. God sends His Son to accomplish their redemption. God sends His Spirit to apply that redemption to their hearts. God preserves them to the very end. At no point — not one — does the outcome depend on human effort, human decision, or human worthiness. It depends on grace. Unmerited. Unconditional. Unstoppable. Grace.

That's the claim. It is staggering. It is offensive to human pride. And the only question that matters is this: does the Bible teach it? That's what this entire journey is about. Not what feels comfortable — but what is true.

Where Did These Ideas Come From?

The short answer: from the Bible. But the historical answer paints a picture that will take your breath away — because these truths have been shaking the world for two thousand years.

The Apostle Paul wrote the clearest explanations of sovereign grace in Romans 8–9, Ephesians 1–2, and his letters to Timothy and Titus. The early church father Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD) developed these themes in a titanic battle against the monk Pelagius, who taught that humans could choose God by the strength of their own will. Augustine insisted — as Paul insisted before him — that fallen humanity is dead in sin and needs God to act first. Dead men don't make decisions.

A thousand years later, the church had largely buried this emphasis under an avalanche of human works, purchased indulgences, and institutional rituals. Then came a German monk named Martin Luther, who in 1517 nailed 95 theses to a church door in Wittenberg — and the world cracked open.

Luther was followed by John Calvin, whose Institutes of the Christian Religion became the most influential articulation of grace-centered theology in history. Calvin didn't invent these doctrines. He simply organized what Scripture had been saying all along — and said it so clearly that the church could no longer look away.

Ephesians 2:8–9
"For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."

The movement continued through the Puritans, through Jonathan Edwards and George Whitefield during the Great Awakening, through Charles Spurgeon who preached to tens of thousands in Victorian London. Notice something: these were not dusty academics hiding in library corners. They were the most passionate evangelists, the most powerful preachers, and the most luminous minds in the entire history of Christianity. Sovereign grace didn't kill their evangelism — it fueled it.

The Big Question: Who Is in Charge of Salvation?

Every theological system answers this question one way or another — and your answer will reshape everything: your prayers, your worship, your peace, your view of death. Here is where the fork in the road appears:

The Popular View (Arminianism)
God offers salvation to everyone equally
Humans must choose to accept it by free will
God's plan depends on human decision
You can lose your salvation by choosing to walk away
God hopes you'll choose Him
The Reformed View
God chooses specific people before the foundation of the world
He sends the Spirit to make them willing and able to believe
God's plan depends on God's decision
Those God saves, He keeps — not one is lost
God accomplishes what He purposes

We know which view is more popular. But popular doesn't mean biblical. There was a time when the whole world believed the sun orbited the earth — and they were all wrong. The question has never been what feels right or what the majority thinks. The question is — and has always been — what does the text actually say?

That's exactly what we'll investigate in the phases ahead. Bring your Bible. Bring your skepticism. We welcome both.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

You might be thinking: "This is interesting historically, but does it actually matter when I'm lying awake at 2 AM?" The answer is profoundly, achingly, life-alteringly yes. Here's why:

If salvation depends on you, then your assurance depends on the stability of your own heart — a heart the Bible itself calls "deceitful above all things" (Jeremiah 17:9). You will spend your life haunted by whispers: Did I believe hard enough? Was I sincere enough? What if I stumble too far? Will I hold on until the end? That is the quiet torment of millions of sincere believers right now.

If salvation depends on God, then your assurance rests on the character of the One who is the same yesterday, today, and forever — the One whose promises have never failed, whose power has never waned, and who says with terrifying finality:

John 10:28–29
"I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand."

This is not cold, academic theology. This is the end of fear. This is the beginning of a peace so deep it can withstand anything life throws at you — because it was never based on your performance in the first place. It was based on His.

What's Ahead

In the next four phases, we're going to do something dangerously simple: we're going to open the Bible and read it. Carefully. Honestly. Without forcing it to say what we want it to say. We'll walk through the five truths about grace, examine the most staggering passages on salvation, face the hardest objections head-on, and arrive at a place where the glory of God in salvation becomes the most beautiful, most terrifying, most liberating thing you have ever encountered.

This is not about winning a theological argument. It's about something infinitely more dangerous: meeting the God who chose you before the world began. Fair warning — that meeting tends to rearrange everything.

Romans 11:33, 36
"Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways! … For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be glory forever. Amen."

Reflection Questions

  1. When you think about salvation, who is ultimately in charge — God or man? What has led you to that view?
  2. Have you ever felt insecure about your salvation? What would it mean for your daily life if that security rested entirely on God's faithfulness rather than your own?
  3. Are you willing to let the Bible change your mind — even if what it teaches is different from what you've always heard?
