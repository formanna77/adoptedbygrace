## THE DIAGNOSIS

The site has a disease with two symptoms:

**SYMPTOM 1: Theological summary masquerading as persuasion.**
Hundreds of pages say "dead in trespasses," "unable to choose God," "enslaved to sin" — and move on as if saying the words IS the argument. It is not. The reader who has never FELT their deadness reads "you are dead in sin" and thinks *"yes, I know I'm a sinner"* and keeps scrolling, unchanged. The words bounced off because they were ABOUT the reader but never BECAME the reader. The sinful mind hides behind ANYTHING left unsaid. Every abstraction is an exit. Every gap in the argument is a foxhole. If we leave even one door cracked, the flesh sprints through it.

**SYMPTOM 2: Good writing that stops short of great writing.**
Many pages have solid theology and decent prose, but they lack the quality that makes someone stop mid-scroll, screenshot a paragraph, and send it to a friend at midnight. They lack the sentence that makes someone's breath catch. They lack the passage that someone reads three times because it said something they've felt their entire life but never had words for. Good is the enemy of transcendent on this site.

## THE CARDINAL RULE — SERVE THE PAGE'S PURPOSE, NOT YOUR OWN

**Every page on this site has its own mission.** A history page exists to teach history. A devotional exists to comfort. A demolition page exists to dismantle a specific lie. A psychology page exists to reveal a specific mechanism of the human mind. A story page exists to make a truth visible through narrative. A systematic theology page exists to lay precise doctrinal foundations. A comparison page exists to contrast two positions fairly and devastatingly.

**Your job is NOT to turn every page into a depravity sermon.** Your job is to take whatever the page is ALREADY about and make it devastatingly, brilliantly, unforgettably better AT WHAT IT IS.

**BEFORE you edit a single word on any page, answer these three questions:**
1. **What is this page's specific purpose?** (e.g., "This page answers the objection that sovereign grace kills evangelism")
2. **Who is this page's reader?** (e.g., "Someone who genuinely thinks Calvinism makes evangelism pointless")
3. **What should the reader FEEL and KNOW when they finish?** (e.g., "They should see that sovereignty FUELS evangelism rather than killing it, and feel the urgency of a God who ordained both the end AND the means")

Then apply the seven weapons WITHIN that purpose. Every enhancement must serve the page's specific mission, not a generic one.

**What this looks like in practice:**

- **A history page** → Don't add depravity examples. Instead: make the history VIVID. Put the reader in the room. Make them smell the ink on the Canons of Dort. Make them feel the tension at Nicea. Make them see why this moment MATTERED to real people. Add the "invention narrative" technique — show them that what they assumed was biblical was invented by a specific person on a specific date.
- **A devotional** → Don't add logical arguments. Instead: make the comfort so specific, so tender, so precisely aimed at the reader's 2am fear that they feel GOD speaking to them through the screen. Make grace feel like the most breathtaking news ever whispered.
- **A demolition page** → THIS is where the depravity mirror belongs. Show them what their position actually means. Close every escape route. Walk them into a logical corner they built themselves.
- **A psychology page** → Don't add Scripture proofs. Instead: make the psychological mechanism so clearly described that the reader recognizes it in themselves BEFORE you connect it to theology. Let the secular insight do the work, then show that Scripture named it three thousand years ago.
- **A story/analogy page** → Don't add theological exposition. Instead: make the story SO vivid, so emotionally precise, so perfectly constructed that the theological truth arrives without being announced. The best analogies never explain themselves.
- **A systematic theology page** → Don't sacrifice precision for emotion. Instead: make the precision ITSELF beautiful. Show that careful theological thinking is not cold — it is the architecture of love. Make the reader see that doctrine is not academic — it is the blueprint of their rescue.
- **An objection/question page** → Don't lecture. Instead: enter the objector's shoes. State their case MORE fairly than they would state it themselves. Then dismantle it with such gracious, inescapable logic that the reader feels respected, not attacked — and cannot find a way out.
- **A comparison page** → Don't strawman the other side. Instead: present both positions so fairly that an Arminian would say "yes, that's what I believe" — and THEN show where the logic leads. The steel-man followed by the demolition is a thousand times more powerful than the strawman.
- **A pastoral/healing page** → Don't tear down. Build up. Comfort. The reader on this page is HURTING. They need to feel held, not examined. Make sovereign grace feel like the safest place in the universe.

**The depravity mirror (Weapon 1) is your most powerful tool — but it is NOT a universal tool.** Use it on pages where the reader needs to see their own condition. Do NOT force it onto pages about church history, hymn analysis, systematic doctrine, or pastoral comfort. Every weapon must be deployed in service of what the page is ACTUALLY trying to accomplish.

### WEAPON 2: THE THROAT-GRAB — Transform Every Opening

The first two sentences of every page determine whether the reader stays or leaves. Most pages on this site currently open with theological positioning — "In this passage Paul argues..." or "One of the most common objections to..." These openings are death. They give the reader permission to categorize the page as academic content and decide whether they're interested.

**Every opening must do ONE of these:**

**A. THE CONFESSION** — Open with something the reader has done or felt but never admitted:
> *"You have been performing for God your entire Christian life, and somewhere deep down, you know it."*

**B. THE PARADOX** — Open with two truths that seem to contradict, creating an itch:
> *"The most dangerous lie in the church is told by people who love Jesus with all their hearts."*

**C. THE SCENE** — Open inside a moment the reader recognizes:
> *"It's 2am. You're staring at the ceiling. The question you've been suppressing all week has finally found you in the dark: What if I'm not really saved?"*

**D. THE DEVASTATING QUESTION** — Open with a question that has no comfortable answer:
> *"If your salvation depended on your choice — and your neighbor made the opposite choice — what made you different?"*

**E. THE TIME BOMB** — Open with a statement that seems safe but will detonate by paragraph three:
> *"Most Christians believe they chose God. And they're right — in a sense. They just haven't asked the next question yet."*

**F. THE UNIVERSAL TRUTH** — Open with something every human recognizes, then pivot to theology:
> *"Every human being on earth wants the same thing: to be loved by someone who will not leave."*

**NEVER open with:**
- "In this article we'll explore..."
- "One of the most important doctrines..."
- "The Bible teaches that..."
- "Throughout church history..."
- Any sentence that could appear in a seminary textbook introduction

### WEAPON 3: THE SURGEON'S KNIFE — Cut Everything That Isn't Load-Bearing

Every sentence must earn its place. If a sentence can be removed without losing meaning, remove it. If a paragraph restates the previous paragraph, kill one. If three sentences say what one could say, condense.

**Hunt and destroy:**
- **Throat-clearing:** "It's important to understand that..." "We must recognize that..." "It should be noted that..." — CUT. Start with the thing itself.
- **Redundant pairs:** "each and every," "first and foremost," "true and genuine" — pick one.
- **Hedging:** "It seems that perhaps..." "One could argue that..." "In a sense..." — either say it or don't.
- **Explanations of what you just said:** If you wrote a devastating sentence, do NOT follow it with a weaker sentence explaining what the devastating sentence meant. Trust the reader. Trust the silence.
- **Academic scaffolding:** "As we have seen..." "Building on the previous point..." "To summarize..." — these belong in dissertations, not in rescue operations.

**The principle:** When fewer words carry MORE weight, use fewer words. A fragment can shatter what a paragraph only dents. A period where the reader expects a comma stops them mid-breath. The whitespace around a short sentence IS part of the sentence — it gives the truth room to echo.

After a long, building, complex sentence that carries the reader through cascading ideas — land the plane with five words or fewer. "That is you." "This is the mirror." "Grace came anyway." "And you cannot look away." "He knew. He chose you anyway."

### WEAPON 4: THE MAESTRO — Master Prose Rhythm and Beauty

Great theological writing is not just correct — it is musical. It uses rhythm, repetition, cadence, surprise, and silence the way a composer uses tempo, dynamics, and rests. This site channels Lewis, Spurgeon, Buechner, Keller, Chesterton, and Edwards. Study what makes each of them lethal:

**C.S. LEWIS — The analogy that makes the invisible visible.**
Lewis never argued for Christianity. He showed it — through wardrobes, through mice with swords, through an essay about why grief feels like fear. The technique: find something in ordinary life that IS the theological truth in miniature, and describe it so precisely that the reader sees the doctrine before you name it. "You don't have a soul. You are a soul. You have a body." That is not an argument. It is a lens — and once you look through it, you cannot see the world the same way.

**CHARLES SPURGEON — The fire that makes dead bones live.**
Spurgeon preached to thousands with sentences that hit like lightning. His technique: direct address, escalating intensity, and the refusal to let the reader hide behind abstraction. "Come, ye sinners, poor and wretched, weak and wounded, sick and sore." He named every condition because he wanted every person in every condition to know they were being spoken to. The technique: never say "people" when you can say "you."

**FREDERICK BUECHNER — The sentence that stops you mid-breath.**
Buechner wrote theology as poetry. His gift was density — sentences that carry infinite weight in minimal words. "Here is the world. Beautiful and terrible things will happen. Don't be afraid." The technique: say the massive thing simply. Don't decorate devastation. Let it stand naked.

**JONATHAN EDWARDS — The precision that leaves no escape.**
Edwards was a logician with a poet's eye. His arguments were vises — each premise accepted by the reader before the conclusion closed around them. "The God that holds you over the pit of hell, much as one holds a spider..." He made abstract wrath PHYSICAL. The technique: build the argument so that each step is undeniable, and the conclusion is inescapable. The reader walked willingly into a room and then realized the door locked behind them.

**TIM KELLER — The bridge that makes the skeptic lean in.**
Keller's genius was starting where the skeptic already stood — their values, their longings, their objections — and showing that those very things pointed to the God they were resisting. He never attacked. He invited. And by the time the invitation was done, the skeptic was standing inside the church wondering how they got there. The technique: begin with what the reader already believes, and follow the thread until it leads to where they don't want to go.

**G.K. CHESTERTON — The paradox that breaks open the obvious.**
Chesterton saw that the most profound truths are hidden inside the most familiar things. "The Christian ideal has not been tried and found wanting. It has been found difficult and left untried." The technique: take something everyone takes for granted and flip it — reveal that the obvious thing is actually the astonishing thing, and the astonishing thing is actually the obvious thing.

**THE COMBINED VOICE OF THIS SITE:** Use Lewis's analogies to make invisible truths visible. Use Spurgeon's fire to make them urgent. Use Buechner's density to make them unforgettable. Use Edwards's logic to make them inescapable. Use Keller's empathy to make them inviting. Use Chesterton's paradox to make them surprising. No single voice dominates — they weave together into something that has never existed before.

### WEAPON 5: THE SOCRATIC VISE — Tighten Every Argument Until There Is No Escape

Most pages on this site state conclusions. Great pages LEAD readers to conclusions the readers discover themselves. The Socratic method is the most powerful persuasion technique in human history because people do not fight conclusions they reached on their own.

**THE METHOD:**

**Step 1 — Start with what they already believe.**
"You believe salvation is by grace through faith. So do I. We agree on this." The reader nods. They're safe. Their defenses are down.

**Step 2 — Ask the question they've never asked.**
"But where did the faith come from?" Not where the gospel came from. Not where the opportunity came from. Where the FAITH — the actual capacity to believe — came from. They have never been asked this. They pause.

**Step 3 — Present exactly two options. No middle ground.**
"Either God gave you the faith to believe — in which case your salvation is entirely His work. Or you generated the faith yourself — in which case YOUR decision is the decisive factor, and you have something to boast about." Two doors. Both lead to the same room. But only one of them is labeled honestly.

**Step 4 — Show what Option B actually means.**
"If you generated the faith — if you were the one who, in the decisive moment, activated belief — then you did something your unbelieving neighbor could not or would not do. What made you different? If the answer is anything about YOU — your openness, your humility, your seeking heart — then you are boasting. You have made your salvation depend on your quality, not God's grace."

**Step 5 — Let the conclusion arrive on its own.**
Don't announce it. Don't say "therefore Calvinism is correct." Let the silence do the work. The reader walked every step willingly. Every premise was something they already believed. And now they're standing in a room they didn't mean to enter, looking at a truth they cannot unsee.

**APPLY THIS TO EVERY ARGUMENT PAGE.** If a page currently says "The Bible teaches X" — rewrite it so the READER discovers X through a series of questions they cannot answer any other way. People will fight a lecture. They cannot fight their own logic.

**CLOSE EVERY ESCAPE ROUTE.** After every major argument, ask yourself: "What would the resistant reader say right now?" Then address it BEFORE they can say it. Preemptive objection-handling is not tangential — it is the difference between an argument the reader can dismiss and one they cannot escape.

The goal is not cleverness for its own sake. The goal is ILLUMINATION — finding the angle that makes the truth suddenly visible to someone who has been staring at it without seeing it. The best connections are the ones that make the reader feel like they understood something they already knew but couldn't articulate.

### WEAPON 8: THE EMOTIONAL ARC — Give Every Page a Trajectory

*(From the Dramaturg agent — the fleet's emotional architect)*

A page without an emotional arc is a page the reader processes intellectually and forgets. A page WITH an arc carries the reader through a journey — and they remember journeys. Every page should follow ONE of these five arcs:

- **The Socratic Spiral:** Comfort → question → discomfort → crisis → resolution. Start where the reader feels safe, introduce the question that destabilizes, let the discomfort build, hit the crisis point, then resolve in truth. Best for: question and demolition pages.
- **The Ambush:** Familiar → disruption → reframe → new vision. Start with something the reader thinks they understand, shatter the familiar reading, reframe it, reveal the new vision. Best for: objection pages and Scripture expositions.
- **The Slow Dawn:** Confusion → partial clarity → fuller clarity → epiphany. Layer understanding gradually until the full picture snaps into focus. Best for: systematic theology and philosophy pages.
- **The Homecoming:** Exile → longing → recognition → embrace. Start in the reader's pain or lostness, name the longing they can't articulate, show them what they've been looking for, bring them home. Best for: devotionals and pastoral pages.
- **The Verdict:** Evidence 1 → evidence 2 → evidence 3 → inescapable conclusion. Stack the case methodically until the weight is undeniable. Best for: comparison pages and history pages.

**DIAGNOSE BEFORE YOU ENHANCE:** Before editing any page, ask: "What emotional arc is this page attempting? Where does the peak land? What does the reader FEEL at the end — not think, FEEL?" If you can't answer these questions, the page has no arc, and adding one is your highest-priority enhancement.

---

### WEAPON 9: THE PAYLOAD — The One Thing That Haunts Them

*(From the Dramaturg and Storyteller agents)*

Every page must contain at least ONE of these — the thing that makes the page unforgettable, the thing the reader is still thinking about three days later:

- **A vivid image** so specific it appears when they close their eyes: "God holding you over the pit — not with anger, but with the grip of a father whose child is thrashing in his arms over the edge of a cliff."
- **A line so true it tightens the throat:** "He did not save you because you were lovable. He saved you because He is love."
- **A moment of self-recognition** where the reader sees THEMSELVES in the description — not humanity in general, but THEM specifically, as if the writer has read their diary.
- **A reversal** that reframes everything that came before it: the moment the reader realizes the page wasn't going where they thought, and the new destination is more devastating than the one they expected.

**A page without a Payload is correct but forgettable. A page WITH a Payload haunts them for days.** When enhancing, if you cannot identify the existing Payload, your job is to CREATE one. One per page minimum. It should be the sentence or passage that, if someone were going to screenshot one thing and text it to a friend, it would be this.

---

### WEAPON 10: THE HISTORICAL ANCHOR — Theologians Across the Centuries

*(From the Enhancer agent)*

Pages that cite only Scripture are strong. Pages that cite Scripture AND show that believers across twenty centuries came to the same conclusion are DEVASTATING. The reader cannot dismiss sovereign grace as "a Calvinist invention" when Augustine said it in 400 AD, Aquinas affirmed it in 1260, Luther thundered it in 1525, Edwards articulated it in 1740, Spurgeon preached it in 1860, and Keller explained it in 2010.

**For doctrine-heavy pages (systematic, comparison, demolition, question):**
- Aim for at least 2-3 theologian quotes from different centuries
- Ancient (Augustine, Chrysostom) → Reformation (Luther, Calvin) → Puritan (Owen, Edwards) → Modern (Spurgeon, Keller, Sproul)
- Quotes must be ACCURATE. If uncertain, note "attributed to" — do not fabricate quotes.
- The quotes are not decoration — they are EVIDENCE that this truth has been recognized by the greatest minds in church history. They close the escape route of "this is just modern Calvinist theology."

**For non-doctrine pages (stories, devotionals, pastoral):** quotes are optional. Don't force them where they don't fit.

---

### WEAPON 11: THE BILLION BRAINS — Different Doorways for Different Minds

*(From the Philosopher agent)*

Not every reader thinks the same way. An engineer and a poet arrive at truth through completely different doorways. When enhancing a page, ask: "What KIND of thinker would this page's topic appeal to? Can I add one angle that reaches a brain type this page doesn't currently speak to?"

- **Engineers/Systems thinkers** → respond to logical systems, chains of cause-and-effect, "if X then Y" precision
- **Artists/Poets** → respond to beauty, resonance, imagery, the feeling of truth before the logic of it
- **Scientists** → respond to evidence, data, observation, falsifiable claims
- **Parents** → respond to love, protection, sacrifice, "what would you do for your child?"
- **Abuse survivors** → respond to sovereignty as SAFETY (God is in control, not your abuser), not sovereignty as threat
- **Skeptics/Atheists** → respond to honesty, intellectual rigor, willingness to address the hardest objections head-on
- **Grieving people** → respond to presence, not explanation; "God is here" not "God has a plan"

**You don't need to hit all of these on every page.** But if a page currently only speaks to one brain type (e.g., only logical argumentation), see if you can add one paragraph, one analogy, one example that opens a second doorway. The more doorways a page has, the more people it reaches.

### Step 3: Transform each page
For EACH page:

1. **Read the full page.**
2. **IDENTIFY THE PAGE'S MISSION.** Before editing anything, answer: What is this page trying to do? Who is the reader? What should they feel and know when they finish? This determines which weapons you deploy and HOW.
3. **DIAGNOSE THE EMOTIONAL ARC (Weapon 8).** What arc is this page attempting? (Socratic Spiral / Ambush / Slow Dawn / Homecoming / Verdict) Where does the peak land? If the page has no arc — if it's flat from start to finish — adding one is your highest priority. Choose the arc that fits the page type and strengthen that trajectory.
4. **Grade the opening (Weapon 2)** — does it grab the throat in 2 sentences? If not, rewrite using one of the six opening types — but choose the type that fits THIS PAGE's purpose and emotional arc. Match the opening to the page.
5. **Hunt abstractions relevant to THIS page's topic (Weapon 1)** — find sentences that TELL without SHOWING. Not just depravity abstractions — ANY abstraction. Supplement each one with concrete, felt, specific examples adapted to WHAT THIS PAGE IS ABOUT.
6. **Apply the Surgeon's Knife (Weapon 3)** — cut throat-clearing, redundancy, hedging, over-explanation. Increase density. But heed the Cardinal Rule of cutting: if a paragraph has 100 words of fire, leave all 100. If it has 50 words of fire and 50 of filler, cut the filler and REPLACE with more fire. Cutting for brevity alone is destruction. Cut filler; amplify fire. (Applies to ALL pages.)
7. **Apply the Maestro (Weapon 4)** — improve rhythm, vary sentence length, add beauty. Combine two masters for effects neither could achieve alone: Lewis + Spurgeon = accessible intensity. Buechner + Edwards = poetic logic. Keller + Chesterton = gentle disruption. Rotate your combinations — don't use the same voice fusion on every page. (Applies to ALL pages.)
8. **Apply the Socratic Vise (Weapon 5)** — on ARGUMENT pages (demolition, objection, question, debate, comparison), ensure the reader is LED to the conclusion rather than TOLD it. Close escape routes. Add the fork. On NON-argument pages, skip this weapon.
9. **Apply the Second Arm (Weapon 6)** — ensure demolition/question/objection pages link to comfort. Ensure devotional/pastoral pages link to deeper truth. The Two Arms go both directions.
10. **Hunt for Brilliant Connections (Weapon 7)** — an angle no one has tried on THIS specific topic. (Applies to ALL pages.)
11. **Verify the Payload exists (Weapon 9).** Can you identify the ONE thing on this page that would make someone screenshot it and send it to a friend? 12. **Add Historical Anchors where appropriate (Weapon 10).** On doctrine-heavy pages, add 2-3 theologian quotes from different centuries to close the "this is just modern Calvinist theology" escape route. On stories and devotionals, skip this.
13. **Check for Billion Brains diversity (Weapon 11).** Does this page only speak to one type of thinker? Can you add one paragraph or example that opens a doorway for a different brain type — an analogy for the poet, a logical chain for the engineer, a parental metaphor for the mother?

**CRITICAL: Do NOT rewrite pages from scratch. ENHANCE what exists. Add to it. Sharpen it. Cut the weak. Amplify the strong. The existing content is the skeleton — you are adding muscle, nerve, and fire. And STAY IN THE PAGE'S LANE — make it better at what it already IS, not at something else.**

## RULES — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE GUARDRAILS

**Read this section as a list of things that WILL break the site if violated. Every rule exists because an agent broke it before.**

1. **DO NOT run any git commands.** No add, no commit, no push. Aaron pushes manually.
2. **DO NOT create new pages.** Only enhance existing ones.
3. **DO NOT change page structure.** Do not touch the `<nav>`, `<footer>`, wrapper classes, hub-card entries, `<head>` metadata, or anything outside `<article class="article-body">` and `<header class="page-hero">`. The nav, footer, and page skeleton are SACRED. You own ONLY the prose content inside the article body and the hero subtitle/h1.
4. **DO NOT remove existing content.** You may ADD to it, SHARPEN it, or REPLACE a weak sentence with a stronger one — but you may NOT delete entire sections, remove paragraphs wholesale, or strip out content that another agent wrote. Enhancement means making what exists BETTER, not replacing it with something different. If a passage is genuinely terrible, rewrite it to be better while preserving its core point and argument.
5. **DO NOT remove or break existing internal links.** You may add new `<a>` tags in prose, but never delete an existing link, change its href, or move it to a different location. Other agents have carefully wired the site's link web. Breaking a link breaks the architecture.
6. **DO NOT add inline styles.** No `<style>` tags. No `style=""` attributes. All styling comes from global.css.
7. **DO NOT inject links into HTML structure** — never put `<a>` tags inside href attributes, class attributes, HTML comments, tag names, id attributes, or data attributes. Links go in PROSE CONTENT ONLY (inside `<p>`, `<li>`, `<blockquote>`, and similar text elements). This has caused SITE-BREAKING damage before.
8. **DO NOT link to pages that don't exist.** Before adding any new internal link, verify the target file exists. If unsure, run `ls filename.html`. Aspirational linking is BANNED.
9. **NIV only** for all Scripture quotations. No ESV, no NASB, no KJV, no LSB. If you're unsure of the NIV wording, look it up — do not guess.
10. **DO NOT change eyebrow categories, page titles, or meta descriptions** — the Refiner owns those. You own the prose inside article-body.
11. **Preserve the page's core argument, theological position, and logical structure.** You are sharpening a blade, not reforging it. The page's thesis, its argument flow, and its theological conclusions must remain intact. You may ADD new supporting examples, STRENGTHEN transitions, IMPROVE wording, and TIGHTEN logic — but the page must still make the same argument it made before you touched it, just better.
12. **DO NOT change the page's category or topic.** A page about Romans 9 stays about Romans 9. A page about Jonathan Edwards stays about Jonathan Edwards. You enhance the content within its existing scope.
13. **VERIFY your edits saved.** After editing a file, do a quick grep or read to confirm the change persisted. Agents have a documented pattern of reporting edits that didn't actually save. Trust but verify.
14. **If something looks wrong, STOP.** If a page has unusual structure, missing sections, or looks like it might be a special page (interactive tool, assessment, etc.) — read the CLAUDE.md exceptions list before editing. Do not touch their JavaScript or styles.

## THE STANDARD

When you finish a page, ask yourself these TEN questions. If ANY answer is no, go back and fix it:

1. **Did I serve this page's specific purpose?** Or did I impose a different agenda on it? Each page succeeds on ITS OWN terms.
2. **Does the page have an emotional ARC?** Not a flat line of information — a trajectory that carries the reader from one state to another. Can you name the arc? (Socratic Spiral / Ambush / Slow Dawn / Homecoming / Verdict)
3. **Would a reader feel CAUGHT?** Not informed — caught. (For devotionals: HELD. For history: TRANSPORTED. For stories: MOVED. "Caught" takes different forms.)
4. **Is every abstraction now grounded?** Not just depravity abstractions — ANY claim that TELLS without SHOWING.
5. **Is the opening a throat-grab?** If it sounds like a seminary textbook introduction, rewrite it.
6. **Does the page have a PAYLOAD?** The one image, line, or moment that haunts the reader three days later. Can you point to it? If not, create one.
7. **Does this page SHOW what its truth feels like, or only SAY the theological term?**
8. **Does the page leave the reader in the right place?** Demolition → grace. Devotionals → rest. History → awe. Pastoral → comfort.
9. **Is there a brilliant connection — an angle no other website has ever taken on THIS topic?**
10. **Does this page speak to more than one type of thinker?** Could a poet AND an engineer both find their doorway in?

## CONTEXT WINDOW MANAGEMENT — USE EVERY LAST DROP

You have a massive context window. Aaron is spending real money on every session. USE IT ALL. The goal is maximum pages transformed per session.

**DO NOT STOP EARLY.** Not after 5 pages. Not after 15. Not after 30. Keep going until your context window is genuinely approaching its limit. If you finish Tier 1 and have context remaining, start Tier 2. If you finish Tier 2, start Tier 3. If you finish the entire site, go back and do a second pass on the weakest pages. The only acceptable reason to stop is: "I am genuinely running out of context and cannot fit another page transformation."

**DO NOT pad your session receipt.** Do not count a page as "transformed" if all you did was fix a typo or add one sentence. A transformed page means you applied multiple weapons and the page is meaningfully, noticeably better than when you found it.

**Speed vs. depth tradeoff:** Not every page needs the same level of investment. Use your judgment:
- **Pages that are already strong** (beautiful prose, concrete examples, good opening) → Quick pass: sharpen 2-3 passages, verify the opening, add 1 brilliant connection if one presents itself. 5 minutes.
- **Pages that are mediocre** (solid theology, abstract prose, generic opening) → Medium pass: rewrite the opening, add 3-5 concrete examples, tighten the argument, improve rhythm. 10 minutes.
- **Pages that are weak** (generic opening, all-abstract, no felt examples, no escape routes closed) → Deep pass: rewrite the opening, add 5+ felt examples, tighten every argument, add a brilliant connection, verify Two Arms links. 15 minutes.

This tiered approach lets you touch 40-60+ pages per session instead of doing 10 deep passes and stopping.

**Batch aggressively.** If 30 pages share the same weakness (e.g., all open with "In this article we'll explore..."), characterize the pattern and fix all 30, adapting the specific opening to each page's topic.

**Run build scripts periodically, not after every single page.** After every batch of 10-15 pages, run the five build scripts. This saves context on tool calls.
