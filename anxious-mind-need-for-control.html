THE ANXIOUS MIND

The Need for Control: Why Anxiety Is a Theology Problem

Your exhausting grip on life isn't just psychological—it's a claim to sovereignty. Here's why grace is the only release.

1,800 words | 7 min read
Home / Why We Resist / The Anxious Mind / This Article

1Inside the Grip

The alarm goes off but you've been awake for an hour. You've already run through the day's variables: the 9am meeting where you said something stupid last week, the email you need to craft perfectly, the backup plan in case the first plan fails, the backup plan for the backup plan.

You arrive early everywhere. You reread things before sending them. You have contingencies. Not because you're conscientious—though you've told yourself that—but because if you don't do it, it won't be done right. If you don't hold everything together, it falls apart. You have a death grip on control, and you know it's killing you, and you can't let go.

Other people say you're "responsible." What they don't see is the terror underneath. The panic that if you're not hypervigilant, if you're not watching, if you're not managing every contingency, then something catastrophic is waiting to happen. And it will be your fault.

You're exhausted. Not because you're busy. But because you're trying to be God.

2The Theology Underneath

Here's what most people—including most anxious people—get wrong about anxiety: they treat it as a psychological problem. And it has psychological symptoms, certainly. Hypervigilance. Racing thoughts. Obsessive planning. The body locked in a posture of threat.

But underneath all of that is a theological claim.

When you insist on controlling every variable, when you cannot delegate because no one else will do it right, when you check things repeatedly, when you lie awake running scenarios, what you're actually saying is this:

"I am the one keeping this together. If I stop holding on, everything collapses."

That is a statement about sovereignty. You are claiming responsibility for outcomes that don't belong to you. You are assuming a burden that was never meant to rest on human shoulders. You are, in effect, trying to be omniscient and omnipotent—to see every threat and control every outcome.

And anxiety is what happens when a finite creature tries to carry an infinite weight.

Anxiety is what happens when you try to be sovereign over things only God can be sovereign over.

This is why telling an anxious person "just relax" or "stop worrying" is useless. They're not worrying because they want to. They're worrying because, at the deepest level, they don't believe anyone else is in control—so they must be. Their anxiety is their theology made visible.

3What the Science Confirms

Research on anxiety disorders reveals something fascinating: people with generalized anxiety disorder don't fear specific threats more than others do. What distinguishes them is intolerance of uncertainty (Dugas, Buhr, & Ladouceur, 1998). Bad things might happen—and that possibility itself becomes unbearable.

Why? Because uncertainty means no amount of vigilance can guarantee safety. It means you've lost control. And for the anxious person, losing control is the actual threat—more terrifying than whatever bad thing might happen.

Your brain is trying to solve an impossible equation: If I can just stay alert enough, plan enough, prepare enough, then I can guarantee a safe outcome. But outcomes don't depend entirely on your planning. They depend on variables you can't see, forces you can't predict, and a future you can't control.

So your mind keeps spinning. Keep planning. Keep checking. Keep running scenarios. Maybe this time you'll find the combination that guarantees safety.

You never will. Because you're asking control to do what only sovereignty can do.

4What Jesus Actually Said About Anxiety

When Jesus addresses anxiety in Matthew 6:25-34, He doesn't say: "Anxiety is irrational—think positive thoughts." He doesn't offer breathing techniques or mindfulness hacks. Instead, He does something radical. He reframes the entire problem.

Therefore I tell you, do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or drink; or about your body, what you will wear. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothes? Look at the birds of the air: they do not sow or reap or store away in barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not much more valuable than they?

— Matthew 6:25-26 (ESV)

Notice what He's doing. He's not saying "don't worry because everything will be fine." He's saying "look at who's actually in charge." The birds don't control their food supply. They don't plan. They don't strategize. And they're fed. Not because they're anxious enough, but because Someone else is responsible for them.

Then He cuts to the heart of it:

And which of you by being anxious can add a single hour to his span of life?

— Matthew 6:27 (ESV)

This is the demolition punch. Your anxiety does not extend your life. Your hypervigilance does not make outcomes safer. Your white-knuckling does not give you control. In fact, it steals your peace while giving you nothing in return.

Then comes the pivot to sovereignty:

So do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own.

— Matthew 6:34 (ESV)

He's not saying tomorrow won't have trouble. He's saying: Let tomorrow be God's problem. It's already in His hands. Your job is to trust that.

5The Irony That Haunts Every Anxious Person

Here's the truth that most anxious people have never articulated: You are anxious because you reject sovereignty.

Think about it. If you truly believed that God is sovereign—that He ordains the number of your days (Psalm 139:16), that He works all things together for good (Romans 8:28), that not a single sparrow falls without His knowledge (Matthew 10:29)—then what would you be afraid of?

Not that bad things won't happen. Scripture never promises that. But that those bad things will be outside of God's control? That they'll catch Him by surprise? That He won't be there? That He won't use them for your good?

The doctrines of grace are the answer to anxiety—and they're the very thing the anxious person instinctively rejects.

Why? Because genuine trust in God's sovereignty requires surrendering the illusion of control. And the anxious person is clinging to that illusion with everything they have. It feels like if they let go, everything falls apart.

But the irony is exquisite: the very grip they can't release is what's destroying them. The control they're desperate to maintain is an illusion. And the only way out is to admit: I was never actually in control. I never could be. And trying to be is what's killing me.

6The Specific Comfort of Sovereignty for the Anxious

If God is sovereign, then:

You are not the last line of defense. You're not the only thing standing between your family and disaster. You don't have to carry the weight of preventing catastrophe. That weight was never yours to carry.

You cannot fail at something you were never responsible for. You cannot make a mistake large enough to derail God's purposes for your life. Not because you're a perfect decision-maker—you're not—but because God's purposes don't depend on your perfection. They depend on His sovereignty.

Uncertainty is not your enemy. Not because bad things won't happen—they will—but because uncertainty means God gets to be God. It means there's room for grace. It means you get to be surprised by goodness instead of haunted by what if.

The future is already held. This is perhaps the deepest comfort of all. You don't know what's coming. You can't plan for every contingency. And that would destroy you—except that the God who knows everything already holds everything. Your Tuesday is not a mystery to Him. Your health. Your finances. Your relationships. He knew about them before the foundation of the world and He's already arranged them according to His perfect purposes.

Your job is not to control. Your job is to trust.

7Practical Surrender—For When You're Spiraling

When you catch yourself catastrophizing, when the what-ifs are looping, when you're checking things again and again, try this:

Name the claim. "I'm assuming I'm responsible for something that belongs to God." Say it aloud if you can. Name what you're trying to control: the outcome of the conversation, the test results, the plane landing safely, your child's choice, tomorrow's weather. Get specific.

Speak it back to Him. "Lord, I am trying to be you again. I'm claiming sovereignty I don't have. I'm carrying a weight that isn't mine. I give this back to You." This isn't magic. It's confession. It's agreement with reality.

Breathe. Literally. Your nervous system is locked in a threat posture. Breathing—real, slow, intentional breathing—signals to your body that you're actually safe. Not because the threat disappears, but because you're surrendering your hypervigilance. You're choosing trust over control, even if it feels terrifying.

Rehearse what's true. The verse that haunted Aaron Forman into faith was Psalm 139:16—"All the days ordained for me were written in your book before one of them came to be." Speak that. Speak whatever promises grip you. David in the Psalms spoke his anxiety directly to God, and in the speaking, he found rest.

A Tender Word About Help

If anxiety has its hooks in you—if you're having panic attacks, intrusive thoughts, if you've tried everything and nothing helps—then hear this clearly: God's sovereignty does not mean you don't need help.

A therapist who understands cognitive behavioral therapy can help retrain your brain's threat detection. A psychiatrist might recommend medication that helps your nervous system find its baseline again. A spiritual director can help you process the theological implications of trust. These are not failures of faith. They are gifts of grace. The God who is sovereign over your anxiety is also sovereign over the neurotransmitters in your brain. He is sovereign over the counselor you call and the medication that helps you sleep.

Receiving help is not weakness. It's wisdom. It's agreement with the truth that your life belongs to God—all of it, including your healing.

8The Release That Comes

When you finally—really finally—believe that God is in control and you are not, something shifts.

Not immediately. The anxious patterns of thought will still fire. Your heart will still race sometimes. But somewhere underneath it, a knot begins to loosen.

You realize: I don't have to have all the answers. What a relief. What a profound, devastating relief.

You realize: I can do my part and trust God with the outcome. You can prepare for the presentation and trust Him with how people respond. You can take care of your health and trust Him with the diagnosis. You can work hard and trust Him with provision. This isn't passivity. It's the marriage of effort and faith.

You realize: My value doesn't depend on my control. You are chosen. Before the foundation of the world, before you ever did anything right or wrong, before you ever proved your worth, you were chosen. That's not what sovereignty offers—that's what sovereignty gives.

The anxiety won't disappear overnight. The thought patterns won't vanish. But the absolute, white-knuckled, death-grip desperation will ease. Because you finally stop trying to hold together a kingdom that was never yours to hold.

And in that release, you find something you didn't expect: peace. Not the absence of threat, but the presence of Someone more capable than you.

That presence is enough.
