Theologians

J. Gresham Machen

The warrior scholar who refused compromise — and proved at the hour of death that the truths he defended were the ground beneath his feet.

In Brief

When liberal theology captured Princeton Seminary, J. Gresham Machen (1881-1937) refused to bend. He argued that liberalism was not a variant of Christianity but a different religion entirely — works-righteousness in academic dress. He lost his career, his denomination, and his health. He founded Westminster Seminary and the Orthodox Presbyterian Church. His dying words — "I'm so thankful for the active obedience of Christ. No hope without it" — remain the finest one-sentence summary of the gospel any theologian ever uttered.

The Telegram

January 1937. A man lay dying in a North Dakota hospital, thousands of miles from the life he had built. His heart was failing. He had fought on every front — in Princeton classrooms, in church assemblies, in newspapers and books — and institutional Christianity had turned against him. In his final hour, barely able to write, J. Gresham Machen sent one last telegram to his friend John Murray:

"I'm so thankful for the active obedience of Christ. No hope without it."

At the moment of death, when all earthly achievement crumbles and a man stands utterly exposed, Machen did not cling to his accomplishments. He clung to a truth — that Christ's perfect righteousness is imputed to the believer. Not works. Not his own theological brilliance. Only Christ's obedience. That telegram is the measure of the man.

The Baltimore Aristocrat Who Saw the Rot

Machen was born in 1881 into old Baltimore money. He attended Johns Hopkins University and Princeton Seminary, where he showed the marks that would define him: meticulous scholarship, passionate love for truth, and a growing alarm at what he was witnessing. After seminary, he spent two years studying in Germany — at the very heart of liberal Protestant theology — and the experience was transformative, though not in the way liberalism intended. He saw where that path led. He returned to America convinced that liberal theology had abandoned the faith entirely.

In 1906, Machen joined the Princeton faculty. For twenty-three years his lectures became legendary — demanding, precise, and unrelenting in their defense of the apostolic faith. He was not merely teaching. He was training soldiers for a war most of the church refused to acknowledge was happening.

The Crisis at Princeton

Liberalism — the belief that the gospel could be stripped of the supernatural and still remain Christian — was sweeping through the seminaries of mainline Protestantism. The modern spirit demanded that faith be reasonable, palatable, shorn of miracle and mystery. The virgin birth became embarrassing. The bodily resurrection became optional. The depravity of man became a misunderstanding of human potential. God's sovereign election became an affront to human dignity.

Machen watched his own seminary fall. In 1929, Princeton was reorganized to bring liberalism into the inner councils of power. The institution he had served for twenty-three years was betraying the faith it was founded to defend.

He faced the choice many scholars face: compromise with the institution, or lose everything. He was comfortable, tenured, respected. History is full of scholars who kept their chairs by keeping their mouths shut. Machen could not even give them silence. He refused.

Christianity and Liberalism

In 1923, Machen published his masterwork. His argument was devastating in its simplicity: liberalism is not a variant of Christianity. It is a different religion entirely. Christianity teaches that God became incarnate, died to atone for sin, rose from the dead, and will judge the world. Liberalism teaches that Jesus was a moral teacher, that sin is ignorance, and that the gospel's power lies in its ethical teachings.

These are not two interpretations of the same faith. They are two fundamentally different worldviews. You cannot have the security of salvation without substitutionary atonement. You cannot have genuine faith without the supernatural working of the Holy Spirit. Remove the supernatural and you do not have a reformed Christianity — you have moralism dressed in Christian language. You have works-righteousness with a Christian veneer.

What made Machen's argument so powerful was that he refused to be unkind. He did not call liberals villains. He called them what they were: people who had exchanged one religion for another. And that exchange, however sincere, was a catastrophe for the church.

The Exodus

When Princeton fell, Machen did something radical: he founded a new seminary from scratch. Westminster Theological Seminary was designed to be everything Princeton had stopped being — a place where the doctrines of grace were not an embarrassment but a banner. Founding a seminary at age forty-eight with no guaranteed income was an act of extraordinary courage. Within a few years, Westminster became the finest theological school in America for the defense of the confessional faith.

He also founded the Orthodox Presbyterian Church — a denomination committed to the Westminster Confession. Machen was not fighting a rearguard action. He was building a future.

The Cost of Faithfulness

He was defrocked by the Presbyterian Church he loved. His former friends abandoned him. The institution he had served declared him a troublemaker. He lived in near poverty for his final years.

In early 1937, his health failing, Machen traveled to North Dakota to encourage believers being persecuted for their faithfulness to Reformed truth. Pneumonia seized him. His body gave out. He lay dying in Bismarck — far from Philadelphia, far from the seminary he had built with his own hands. From an earthly perspective, he had lost everything.

And in that moment, with nothing left but truth, he reached for his pen: "I'm so thankful for the active obedience of Christ. No hope without it."

At the threshold of eternity, theology became prayer.

Doctrine became devotion. The scholar became a saint resting in grace.

Why Machen Matters Now

The battle Machen fought is the same battle the church fights today. If Machen were alive today, would he recognize the difference between the liberalism he fought and the evangelicalism that claims to oppose it? Modern evangelicalism has, in many ways, become what liberal Protestantism was in Machen's day: a system that denies God's sovereignty in salvation, denies the total depravity of man, and makes the human choice — not God's grace — the decisive factor in eternal destiny.

The language is different. The denials are more subtle. But the disease is the same. You cannot have a gospel of grace if you believe man contributes the decisive act. You cannot stand before God at the hour of death resting on your own choice, your own faith, your own righteousness. You can only stand there resting on Christ's active obedience.

Machen reminds us that some truths are worth losing everything for.

Some hills are worth dying on. His final telegram remains — the finest summary of the gospel any theologian ever uttered, from a man who proved with his last breath that the truths he defended were not abstractions. They were the ground beneath his feet when everything else gave way.
