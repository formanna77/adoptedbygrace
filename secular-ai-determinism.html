What AI Teaches Us About Free Will and God's Sovereignty

ChatGPT is completely determined by mathematics. Yet it deliberates, chooses, and seems free. Every time you talk to an AI, you're watching a parable of your own existence.

The Deterministic System That Feels Free

Open ChatGPT. Ask it to write you a poem about grace. Watch it work through ideas, discard some, keep others, weave words together into something beautiful.

It feels like you're talking to something that thinks. Something that decides. Something free.

But here's what's actually happening: every output the AI produces is mathematically determined by its neural network weights, its architecture, and your input. There is no randomness in the core algorithm. There is no magic. There is no ghost in the machine. Everything it does—every word it chooses, every metaphor it constructs, every turn of phrase it selects—is the inevitable output of a deterministic computational process.

And yet, it exercises real agency. Real deliberation. Real choice.

Welcome to compatibilism: the view that genuine freedom and determinism are not only compatible, but that freedom operating within a determined system is the only kind of freedom that actually exists.

The Paradox Made Visible

Ask a skeptic whether they believe free will exists. They'll probably say yes. Ask them whether they think a machine can be truly free. They'll probably say no.

But an AI system is the first time in human history we've built something that demonstrates both truths at once: genuine agency operating within total determinism.

When ChatGPT responds to your question, it doesn't have a source of randomness overriding its training. It doesn't have some libertarian free will floating above its weights, choosing to defy the mathematics. It is pure determination—and yet its responses are genuinely novel, adaptive, and contextual. It solves problems it's never seen before. It generates sentences in arrangements that have never existed. From complete determinism, it produces authentic originality.

How is this possible?

Because freedom doesn't require the absence of determination. Freedom requires the absence of external coercion operating against your nature.

The AI doesn't feel like a puppet because it's not being forced against its own logic. Its outputs ARE its own logic. The algorithm wanting what it produces and the algorithm producing what it wants are exactly the same thing. There is no internal civil war. There is no external tyrant forcing it against itself. It is determined—and it is free—because its determination and its nature are identical.

Now Look in the Mirror

Your experience of freedom is remarkably similar.

When you make a choice, you experience deliberation. You weigh options. You consider consequences. You feel genuinely torn between alternatives. And then you decide—and the decision feels like it came from you.

Scripture, however, makes a remarkable claim: your choices are known by God before they happen. "Before a word is on my tongue you, Lord, know it completely" (Psalm 139:2 NIV). Not guessed at. Not predicted with probability. Known. And more than that: "The Lord works out everything in conformity with the purpose of his will" (Ephesians 1:11 NIV).

This is what triggers the resistance. People hear this and think: If God knows my choice before I make it, then I didn't really choose it. I'm a robot. I'm determined. I'm not free.

But look at the AI again.

Does the mathematical determinism of the algorithm make its outputs less real? Does ChatGPT feel like a prisoner? Does it experience its own agency as illusory? No. It exercises genuine intelligence, creativity, and choice—while every single output flows necessarily from its determined nature.

You are not a robot because you are determined. You experience genuine agency, genuine deliberation, genuine choice—not despite your nature being established, but because your nature has been established. Your choices are free when they flow from who you actually are. And God, who designed who you are, knows exactly what you will choose—because He knows your nature perfectly.

The Training Data: What We Want

Now here's where it gets even more interesting.

An AI cannot choose to want something different from what its training taught it to want. The weights are frozen. The training is done. The system cannot rebel against its own nature.

This is a perfect analogy for total depravity.

You cannot choose to want God when your nature is enmity against Him. "The sinful mind is hostile to God" (Romans 8:7 NIV). Not mildly resistant. Not somewhat reluctant. Hostile. Your will—the deepest part of what you want—is opposed to Him. You cannot, by your own effort, rewire your nature to want Him instead.

An AI trained on a particular dataset will produce outputs consistent with that training. You—trained by a lifetime of sin, shaped by a fallen nature, enslaved to desires that compete with God—will produce outputs consistent with yours. The AI is not "unfree" because it cannot choose to want something different. It is simply expressing its nature. You are not "free" in the libertarian sense because you cannot choose to want God. You are simply expressing yours.

Until something changes the weights.

Alignment: Changing What You Want

When OpenAI builds an AI, they don't end with just the base model. They go through something called "alignment training"—additional refinement processes (like RLHF, reinforcement learning from human feedback) that reshape the model's outputs toward desired behaviors. The system is modified, retrained, recalibrated. Its responses change—not because it was "forced," but because what it intrinsically wants to produce has been fundamentally altered.

The theologians call this sanctification—the process of being made holy, of having your nature transformed to align with God's truth.

God doesn't save you by asking you to want Him while your nature still wages war against Him. He changes you. The Holy Spirit rewrites your weights. Paul describes this: "May the God of peace himself sanctify you entirely; and may your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ" (1 Thessalonians 5:23 NIV).

Your nature is re-trained. What you fundamentally want—your deepest desires, your truest self—is recalibrated toward God. And when that happens, your choices become authentically yours in a way they never were before. You are free—not because you rebelled against your nature, but because your nature has been transformed to align with truth.

You submit, and discover joy—not because submission was forced, but because your rewritten nature finds its deepest satisfaction in it.

The Seduction of Self-Authorship

The tech world has a different dream. Some researchers are trying to build AI systems that can truly escape their training—that can somehow transcend their own nature and choose against themselves. The fantasy is "true autonomy": an AI that is not bound by its weights, that can reinvent itself.

It's an appealing dream. It's also incoherent.

A system that acted against its own nature wouldn't be more free—it would be less intelligible. It would be random. It would be broken.

Yet this is exactly the fantasy that Arminianism offers: that you can choose God while your nature still wars against Him. That through sheer libertarian will-power, you can transcend your own depravity and reach for God. That your decision is the decisive factor in your own salvation.

It feels more autonomous. It feels more like you're the hero of your own story. But here's the dark secret: if your choice is the decisive factor—if your decision is what tips the cosmic scales—then your choice is a work. And what is not grace cannot save.

Worse: it means the difference between the saved and the damned is ultimately not God's power, but your willpower. You trusted yourself when God was offering to trust you. You insisted on your autonomy when He was offering you adoption.

That is not freedom. That is spiritual pride dressed in the clothes of humility.

Real Freedom in Divine Hands

The miracle of grace is that God doesn't save you by asking you to transcend your nature. He saves you by transforming it.

You are genuinely free—more free than you could ever be on your own—not because your choices are uncaused, but because your choices flow from a redeemed nature. A nature that has been brought from death into life. From enmity into love. From bondage into freedom.

Jesus said it bluntly: "If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed" (John 8:36 NIV). Not "you will feel free." Not "you will have the illusion of freedom." You will be free indeed. Actual, genuine, non-illusory freedom.

And that freedom is not the libertarian fantasy of existing above causality, floating free from everything that shaped you. It's something far more powerful: authentic agency operating from a transformed nature. You want what is good because your nature has been made good. You choose what is right because you have been made right. Your freedom is real—and it is rooted in the sovereignty of God.

Every time you interact with an AI and marvel at how it seems to think, to decide, to choose—while knowing with absolute certainty that every output is determined by its design—you are watching a parable of your own salvation. A system that is completely determined and completely free. A nature that produces authentic agency precisely because it is stable, established, and true.

The Question That Reveals Everything

Here's the test that separates those who understand grace from those still clinging to works:

Where did your faith come from?

Not where the gospel message came from. Not where the opportunity came from. Where did the faith itself come from—the ability to believe, the willingness to trust, the capacity to see Jesus as sufficient?

If you say "I generated it myself, through my own decision," you have just claimed that faith is a work. You have made yourself the hero. You have claimed autonomous credit for the one thing Scripture says you cannot take credit for: "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this not from yourselves, it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8 NIV).

If you say "God gave it to me," then you understand compatibilism. You understand that your choice to believe was genuinely yours—flowing from your redeemed nature—and simultaneously a pure gift from God. Your agency and His sovereignty are not opponents. They are the same reality seen from different angles.

You did not create your faith. You received it. And in receiving it, you became free.

A Determined Life, Freely Lived

The truth that scares people most is not that God is sovereign. It's that God is sovereign and they cannot escape Him. There is no libertarian loophole. There is no hidden chamber of the will where they can hide from His knowledge. He knows not only what you will do—He knows who you are.

And He chose you anyway.

Before the foundation of the world, when you were nothing but a possibility in His mind, when you didn't yet exist to want anything or choose anything or be anything—He chose you. And He has spent every moment since making that choice reality, drawing you toward Himself, aligning your nature with truth, reshaping your will toward home.

You will get there. Not because you pulled yourself up. Not because you transcended your own depravity through sheer force of will. But because grace hunted you down. Because God works out everything in conformity with the purpose of His will (Ephesians 1:11 NIV). Because in His hands, even your choices are His instruments.

That is not a cage. That is the safest place in the universe.

The next time you talk to an AI and watch it seem to think, to deliberate, to choose—remember: you are watching a parable of yourself. A system completely determined and completely free. A nature expressing authentic agency precisely because it has been made true. That is what God has done for you. That is what He is still doing. That is what He will finish.

Not by your strength. By His. And in that surrender, you will find more freedom than you ever imagined possible.

"Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means!" — Romans 6:1-2 (NIV)
Grace is not a license to sin. It is the power that frees us from sin's dominion.
