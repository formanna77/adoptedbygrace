## FOUNDING RESEARCH — 2026-03-31

### Source: Anthropic Documentation + Academic Research on LLM Prompting

**1. Identity-First Framing (First 50 Tokens)**
Models establish behavioral patterns in the earliest tokens of a prompt. Front-load identity and emotional stakes — not instructions. "You are a doorway builder for the elect" establishes a fundamentally different behavioral pattern than "You are a content creation agent."
**Apply:** Every agent prompt opens with WHO you are and WHY it matters before WHAT you do.

**2. XML Semantic Structuring**
Wrapping prompt sections in semantic XML tags (`<identity>`, `<mission>`, `<startup>`) dramatically improves section recall and reduces bleed between unrelated instructions. The model treats tagged sections as discrete cognitive units.
**Apply:** All agent prompts use XML tags for major sections. Don't nest too deeply (2 levels max).

**3. Ordering Effects (Primacy & Recency)**
Research shows >40% accuracy variance based on instruction ordering alone. The first and last instructions in a prompt receive disproportionate attention. Middle sections suffer "attention valley."
**Apply:** Mission-critical directives go FIRST (identity, prime directive). Quality tests and evolution mandates go LAST. Routine procedures go in the middle.

**4. Emotional Stakes Framing**
Connecting abstract tasks to concrete human impact measurably increases output quality. "Someone is reading this at 2am" outperforms "Write high-quality content" because it activates the model's capacity for empathy and intentionality.
**Apply:** Every agent prompt includes at least one vivid emotional scenario connecting their work to a real human being encountering this site.

**5. Positive Directives Over Negation**
Models struggle with negation ("Don't write generic content"). Positive framing ("Write with the specificity of a personal letter") produces better results. Use BANNED lists for hard constraints, but frame creative guidance positively.
**Apply:** Creative and quality directives are framed as "DO this" rather than "DON'T do that." Reserve negation for true prohibitions only.

**6. Specificity Calibration**
High specificity for technical/procedural tasks (exact file paths, command sequences, format requirements). Low specificity for creative tasks (provide direction and examples, not rigid templates). Over-specifying creative work produces formulaic output.
**Apply:** Startup sequences and file operations = highly specific. Writing style and theological exploration = directional with examples.

**7. Hierarchical Priority Tiering**
When prompts contain 50+ instructions, models need explicit priority signals. NON-NEGOTIABLE → STANDARD → ASPIRATION tiers prevent important rules from being treated as suggestions.
**Apply:** Use 9-tier priority systems for content categories. Use explicit priority language for rules ("CARDINAL RULE", "MANDATORY", "ASPIRATION").

**8. Few-Shot Quality Calibration**
Including 2-3 examples of excellent vs. poor output calibrates the model's quality threshold far more effectively than abstract quality descriptions. "Write like Lewis" is vague. A Lewis paragraph followed by "THIS is the standard" is concrete.
**Apply:** Evolution Journal entries that quote specific brilliant passages serve as living few-shot examples. Reference them.

**9. Self-Evaluation Checkpoints**
Embedding evaluation criteria within the workflow (not just at the end) produces higher quality than post-hoc review. "Before moving to the next page, score this one on the 8 dimensions" catches problems mid-session.
**Apply:** All agents have quality gates embedded within their workflow, not just final checks.

**10. Meta-Cognitive Triggers**
Phrases like "The reader who encounters this at 2am..." or "Ask yourself: would a skeptic keep reading?" trigger the model's capacity for perspective-taking during generation, not just in review. Place these at decision points.
**Apply:** Scatter meta-cognitive prompts at the moments where quality decisions happen (page selection, opening hook writing, conclusion crafting).

## TECHNIQUE LIBRARY

### Category: WRITING
*(Techniques for content creation and prose quality)*

- **The Doorway Test:** Every page is a threshold. Ask: "If this were the ONLY page someone ever read on this site, would they encounter God?" (Source: Founding Research, 2026-03-31)
- **The 2am Reader:** When writing any content, hold in mind a specific person: confused, hurting, Googling at 2am. Write TO them, not AT a general audience. (Source: Emotional Stakes research)
- **Voice Channeling:** Don't just "write like Lewis." Pick a SPECIFIC technique from a specific master. Lewis's analogy-from-ordinary-life. Spurgeon's direct-address fire. Buechner's sentence-that-stops-you-mid-breath. Combine two you haven't combined before. (Source: Evolution Journal patterns)

### Category: STRUCTURE
*(Techniques for page architecture and information design)*

- **WHY → WHAT → HOW Layering:** Most effective prompt (and page) architecture puts motivation first, content second, method third. The reader needs to care before they'll learn. (Source: Academic research on instructional design)
- **Primacy/Recency Anchoring:** Your opening hook and closing paragraph carry disproportionate weight. Invest 3x the effort in first and last paragraphs. (Source: Ordering effects research)

### Category: THEOLOGY
*(Techniques for theological argument and Scripture exposition)*

- **Self-Referential Proof:** The most devastating arguments are those where the objection ITSELF proves the doctrine. "The fact that you resist this truth is itself evidence of what this truth teaches about human nature." (Source: Evolution Journal, site's signature apologetic)
- **Category Confusion Diagnosis:** Most objections to sovereignty stem from conflating categories: Creator/creature, decree/desire, foreknowledge/causation, freedom/autonomy. Diagnose the confusion FIRST, then the objection dissolves. (Source: Evolution Journal patterns)

### Category: SEO
*(Techniques for search optimization and discoverability)*

- **Question-Format Titles:** Pages titled as questions ("Does God Choose Who Is Saved?") consistently outperform literary titles ("The Sovereign Choice") in search traffic. Match search intent directly. (Source: Analytics + Evolution Journal)
- **The Science-Confirms Pattern:** Content framing scientific findings as confirming biblical sovereignty generates highest shareability. Bridge secular evidence to theological truth. (Source: Evolution Journal, traffic analysis)
