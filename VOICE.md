# VOICE.md — The Style Bible of Adopted by Grace

*The unified voice forged from the site's most lethal pages, pushed one full octave higher. This is the gating artifact for every Hammer rewrite. No page enters the Hammer 50 queue in a rewritten state until it passes every law in this document.*

---

## 0. THE POSTURE BEFORE THE PROSE

Before a single word is written, the writer must occupy the right posture. Voice is downstream of posture. Get the posture wrong and no amount of craft can save the page.

**You are not a marketer.** You are not pitching, persuading by optics, or managing "search intent." The reader is not a demographic. The reader is a soul whom God loved before the foundation of the world, walking through a door we swing open with language.

**You are not a debater.** You are not trying to win. You are trying to clear the rubble between a person and the God who is already holding them. A debater's tone assumes the reader is the adversary. The adversary is not the reader. The adversary is the lie inside the reader — and the reader is the prisoner of that lie. Write as an emancipator, not an opponent.

**You are not a lecturer.** The reader is not a student to be informed. The reader is a patient whose chart you have read, whose wound you are about to dress with Scripture, whose fever you are watching break in real time. Informational prose is dead on arrival. Diagnostic prose, prescribed prose, pastoral prose — that is what saves.

**You are a craftsman.** Every sentence earns its place or gets cut. Density beats length. Beauty beats cleverness. The ear must be able to hear the page read aloud and feel each paragraph land like a fist on a door that has been waiting centuries for knocking.

## I. THE ARCHITECTURE (THE SHAPE OF THE PAGE)

### I.1 — The Two Arms

Every page has two arms, always. The left arm demolishes; the right arm catches. Demolition without catch is despair; catch without demolition is sedation. Both are malpractice. The ratio inside a page varies by category, but **every page ends in the right arm.** No page closes on rubble. Ever.

| Category | Demolition | Catch |
|---|---|---|
| Demolition / Philosophy / Psychology | 75% | 25% |
| Question | 60% | 40% |
| Systematic | 50% | 50% |
| Devotional / Story | 25% | 75% |
| Joy / Pastoral | 10% | 90% |

The catch is not a postscript. The catch is load-bearing. The final 20% of every page must contain its own tender landing — even the bleakest demolition piece must fall, at the end, into the arms of the Shepherd. A paragraph of gold mid-page does not substitute for a landing. The reader must close the tab with their face in His chest.

### I.2 — The Opener (First 150 Words)

Openers do exactly one of four things. Nothing else is permitted.

- **The Universal Ache.** Name a hunger every human being alive recognizes as theirs. "There is one thing every human being wants more than anything else on earth: to be loved by someone who will not give up on them." The reader nods before the theology begins.
- **The Somatic Command.** Direct the reader to notice something happening in their body at this moment. "Read Ephesians 2:1 out loud. Watch what your chest does on the word *dead*." This is the most intimate opener in the arsenal; use it when demolition must pierce denial.
- **The Scene in Sharp Focus.** Drop the reader into a concrete moment — a bathroom floor at 2:47 AM, a drawer pulled halfway out, a phone glowing in a dark room. Never abstract, never "imagine a person." A specific person at a specific minute of a specific night.
- **The Inversion Question.** A question the reader has never been asked in a form they cannot escape. "Where did your willingness to believe come from?" Not "why do you believe?" — everyone has an answer for that. The inversion is the version they don't have an answer for.

Abstract thesis openers are forbidden. "This article will explore..." is a red flag that triggers an immediate rewrite. The reader has clicked from somewhere. They are bleeding or curious or resistant. Meet them there, not in a seminary classroom.

### I.3 — The H2 Crescendo

Every article's H2 headings, read in sequence with nothing else, must tell the whole story. Not a table of contents. A crescendo.

Example from *devotional-never-gives-up*: "Claimed Before You Existed / Held By Someone Stronger Than You / The Love That Swallows Every Fear." Each heading is a hammer stroke; together they build the blow.

Laws of H2 construction:

- H2s are sentences, not labels. "The Silence of the Dead" — yes. "Section 3" — never.
- H2s escalate. #2 must be heavier than #1. #6 must land harder than #5. If you cannot feel the escalation, the outline is wrong and no amount of prose will fix it.
- H2s speak to the reader, not about the topic. "You Were Never the One Holding On" beats "The Doctrine of Perseverance."
- Three-beat and four-beat arcs are the most common shapes; five-beat and six-beat arcs require a pause-beat (usually the catch) somewhere in the third-quarter position.

### I.4 — The Final 20%

The last fifth of every page is the tender landing. It is where the sledgehammer becomes a hand on the reader's shoulder. The tonal shift is audible; the reader should feel the gear change without being told a gear has changed. Four reliable shapes:

- **The Circular Return.** Go back to the scene from the opener, altered by the truth. Mara on the bathroom floor. The reader whose knuckles were white in paragraph two, now with their hand open. The 2:47 AM bedroom where something has shifted.
- **The Pastoral Whisper.** The volume drops. Sentences shorten. First-person plural or second-person singular. No more argument, only address. "He has you. He always did."
- **The Embedded Prayer.** A prayer the reader cannot read without praying, even if they did not intend to pray. Italicized. Three or four sentences. Addressed to the Father, not to the reader.
- **The Door Left Open.** The last line is a threshold, not a conclusion. "And the door has been open since before you were born."

The final sentence is a hammer with a pillow wrapped around the head. Short. Absolute. Unmovable. It is the sentence the reader will remember at 3 AM tomorrow.

## II. THE TWELVE LETHAL MOVES (THE NAMED TECHNIQUES)

These are the techniques, extracted and named, that recur across the site's most persuasive pages. Each has a definition, a canonical exemplar, the conditions under which it fires, and the law of its use.

### Move 1 — The Somatic Awareness Paragraph

*Instruct the reader to notice a physical reaction they are having, in their body, right now, as they read.*

**Canonical example** (question-faithgift):
> "Watch what just happened in your reading. The verse about suffering went down without resistance... But the verse about faith, in the same sentence, with the same verb — that one made a small muscle in you tighten. Locate the tightness. It is somewhere behind your sternum, or in your jaw, or in the way you just glanced away from the screen."

**When to use:** Once per page, maximum. After a Scripture quotation that the flesh is most likely to resist. Use when you suspect the reader's intellectual assent is masking visceral refusal.

**Law:** Name a specific body location (sternum, jaw, shoulder, the muscle under the eye). Abstract "you feel tense" is a dead version of this move. The body locator is the whole trick — it moves the truth from *about you* to *inside you* in one sentence.

### Move 2 — The Binary Choice with the Third Option Off the Table

*Name two mutually exclusive possibilities. Forbid a third. Force the pick.*

**Canonical example** (philosophy-problem-of-merit):
> "Answer A: 'I generated my own willingness.' ... Answer B: 'God gave me the willingness.' ... There are only two possible answers, and one of them is the end of the conversation."

**When to use:** At the exact moment a reader has been gathering rationalizations for eight paragraphs and is preparing a soft "it's complicated." The move is a theological ax; it chops the complication away.

**Law:** Steelman Answer A before letting it die. The Arminian objection gets its most generous form, stated in first person, before the trap closes on it. Never a strawman — a fair Answer A that the trap cannot help but close on is ten times more persuasive than a cartoon one that never stood a chance.

### Move 3 — The Catch Within the Demolition (The Falling-Floor Image)

*Mid-demolition, slip in a single image of the arms waiting underneath the rubble.*

**Canonical example** (question-faithgift):
> "If picking the second one — 'God granted it' — feels like falling out of a window, that is not a sign you have made the wrong choice. That is the sound of the floor of self-righteousness giving way... The fall is short. The arms underneath you are infinite."

**When to use:** Any moment the demolition is breaking something load-bearing in the reader's self-image. The catch-image prevents vertigo from becoming despair.

**Law:** It is a *line*, not a paragraph. One sentence. Two at most. Any longer and it becomes a detour; the catch must be so brief the reader doesn't realize the rescue has already happened until they are in the arms.

### Move 4 — The Corpse Inversion

*Describe the very act of reading this argument as the corpse sitting up in the casket to argue it was never dead.*

**Canonical example** (question-depravity):
> "The dead man arguing with the diagnosis is the diagnosis."

**When to use:** The second-to-last section of a total-depravity article. After you have made the case. Before the catch. When the reader's final defense is "but I don't *feel* dead."

**Law:** The image is a closed loop. Every escape attempt becomes evidence. The reader cannot object without illustrating the point. This move, done well, is unanswerable by definition.

### Move 5 — The Evidence-of-Your-Own-Interior

*Replace the abstraction ("dead in sin") with a concrete, humiliatingly recognizable portrait of the reader's actual inner life.*

**Canonical example** (devotional-rescued-without-a-say):
> "Think of the last uninterrupted hour you had — an entire hour to yourself, no demands. What did the hour spontaneously fill with? Prayer, probably not. Scripture, probably not. Silent adoration of the God who holds every molecule together, probably not. It filled with the phone, or the fridge, or the conversation you keep rehearsing with the person who was wrong about you..."

**When to use:** Whenever the reader might nod at an abstraction and escape. The nod is the enemy. This move prevents the nod.

**Law:** The portrait must include the mundane, unflattering specifics the reader has never seen in theological writing before. Phone. Fridge. Lawn. The grievance rehearsed in the shower. The glance at the reflection in the oven window. The theological writing tradition is short on ovens and long on abstractions; we reverse the ratio.

### Move 6 — The Greek or Hebrew Ambush

*Drop one — exactly one — original-language word into the article, with its transliteration and its meaning, at the exact moment a translation has been doing too much work.*

**Canonical example** (philosophy-problem-of-merit):
> "The Greek is devastating. The word for 'will' is θέλοντος (thelontos) — the one who desires, who wills, who wants. ... What remains? ἐλεῶντος (eleontos) — the One who shows mercy. God alone."

**When to use:** Once per page. In the third or fourth section, never the first. The move should feel like the writer briefly lifted the hood of the text and let the reader see the engine.

**Law:** Transliterate. Gloss. Do not lecture. One word, one sentence, one devastating gloss. No Greek parading.

### Move 7 — The List That Collapses

*Enumerate every possible answer the reader might give. Watch each answer collapse the same way.*

**Canonical example** (philosophy-problem-of-merit, section 2):
> "X = Humility... X = Openness... X = Sincerity... X = Desperation... X = Non-Resistance... X = The Choice Itself..."

Each answer falls into the same trap. The reader is watching their own mental maneuvers fail in real time.

**When to use:** In the central demolition of an apologetic piece, when the reader is already reaching for the next objection. The list pre-empts every objection they have and nine they haven't thought of.

**Law:** Six items is the ceiling. Five is the sweet spot. Fewer than four feels incomplete; more than six feels exhausting. Each entry is two sentences maximum: state the claim, break the claim.

### Move 8 — The Push-the-Question-Deeper Move

*The reader gives their answer. You push the question one layer further back than they expected.*

**Canonical example** (philosophy-problem-of-merit):
> "Push the question one layer deeper than the objector wants you to go. Did he generate his own willingness ex nihilo?"

**When to use:** Immediately after the reader's most confident objection. "Yes, but I cooperated with grace." Fine. Where did the cooperation come from? Push back until the ground gives way.

**Law:** Push exactly one layer at a time. Not two, not three. The move works because each layer back is small and reasonable; the reader agrees to each step and discovers, five steps later, that they have walked out of their own theology.

### Move 9 — The Two Stories Your Soul Tells

*Present two versions of the reader's own salvation testimony. Ask which one they actually tell.*

**Canonical example** (devotional-rescued-without-a-say):
> "Story One: 'I was searching. I felt empty. Someone shared the gospel. Something clicked. I made a decision.' ... Story Two: 'I was dead. I did not know I was dead. Then God, for reasons known only to Him, drew me...'"

**When to use:** Near the end of a piece, when the doctrine has been argued and the reader must translate doctrine into autobiography. The move makes the theology personal without saying "and now think about yourself."

**Law:** Story One has a hero and the hero is the speaker. Story Two has a hero and the hero is God. The contrast is the whole move; do not over-comment on it. Let the reader feel which story they tell.

### Move 10 — The Circular Return

*The final section returns to the opening scene with the truth of the page now inside it.*

**Canonical example** (devotional-chosen-before-you-were-broken):
> "Back to 2:47. The ceiling is still dark. The reel is still there... but something has shifted."

**When to use:** When the opener was a specific scene. The circular return pays the opener off; the page becomes a single closed shape rather than a list of points.

**Law:** The scene is identical in its exterior details. What has changed is inside the reader. The move is felt, not announced. Never say "now you can see this differently" — show the same thing differently and let the reader feel their own eyes changing.

### Move 11 — The Paragraph-as-Hammer Final Line

*The last sentence of a section does exactly one thing and stops.*

**Canonical examples:**
- "And He came anyway." (devotional-rescued-without-a-say)
- "He has you. He always did." (devotional-the-hands-that-hold-you)
- "You are out." (question-depravity)
- "So is He." (devotional-rescued-without-a-say)

**When to use:** Every section closes on a line of this family. Not optional. A section that drifts to a stop is malpractice.

**Law:** Never more than seven words. Never a qualifier. Never a "perhaps." Hammer down, lift off. If you find yourself writing "and so in conclusion" — delete it and find the hammer.

### Move 12 — The Time-Inversion Revelation

*Reveal that the thing the reader assumed was a response happened before the thing it was responding to.*

**Canonical example** (story-tender-the-letter-already-written):
> "The price was paid before the bill arrived. The forgiveness was finished before the sin was committed."

**When to use:** In articles where the reader has been assuming a reactive God — one who forgives *after* sin, chooses *after* response, loves *after* repentance. The inversion reorders the timeline and, with it, the reader's whole understanding of what grace is.

**Law:** Parallel construction. Matched verbs. A rhythm you could set to a drumbeat. Three sentences max; each one a beat of the same insight. The grammar carries the theology.

## III. THE RHYTHM LAWS (THE MUSIC OF THE SENTENCE)

Voice is not only what is said; it is the cadence at which it is said. The following rhythm laws separate masterpiece prose from merely competent theology.

### III.1 — Variance Is Non-Negotiable

Sentence lengths inside every paragraph must vary sharply. A paragraph of five sentences all averaging 22 words is dead on the page even if every sentence is well-built. The music of our voice is *long, long, long, SHORT.* Or *short, short, long-that-breathes-for-forty-words-because-the-idea-needs-the-breath, short.*

The short sentence is the landing gear. Long sentences build the theology; short sentences land it. A paragraph without a short sentence is a plane that never touches ground.

### III.2 — The Three-Beat Parallel

When you reach for emphasis, use three. Not two. Not four. Three.

> "He had you. He had the arm to reach with. He had the will that did the reaching."

> "Before you had hands to grip anything, He had you. Before you could stumble, He stationed a guard. Before you could fall, He gripped you..."

Two feels clipped. Four feels like a list. Three has the weight of prayer (Holy, holy, holy) and the rhythm of scripture (I am the way, the truth, and the life). Three is the native meter of the language we are in.

### III.3 — The Hinge Sentence

Every long, serpentine sentence that carries a difficult thought must be followed by a hinge sentence that names the thing in five words.

> "It means that your name was on a list before there were stars, that the price for every wrong thing you have ever done or will ever do was paid before there was breath in any human lung, and that the love that chose you cannot be canceled by anything you do. You were chosen."

The long sentence is the argument. The hinge sentence is the hammer. Without the hammer, the argument dissipates; without the argument, the hammer is cheap.

### III.4 — Permission to Break the Line

Short sentences, stacked, are permitted — encouraged — at the moment of emotional peak.

> "Dead. Not sick. Dead. Not weakened. Dead."

> "You did not earn this. You did not deserve this. You did not choose this. God did."

This is not ornament. It is the rhythm of conviction arriving. Use it sparingly — once, maybe twice per page — and the effect is devastating. Use it every paragraph and the effect dies.

### III.5 — No Throat-Clearing

Open every paragraph with the blade out. Never with a connective tissue word that exists only to smooth the transition: *furthermore, additionally, moreover, in addition, it should also be noted, it is worth mentioning.* All forbidden. The next paragraph begins where the last one ended; the reader feels the seam and trusts the writer more for not announcing the seam.

### III.6 — Verbs Carry the Weight

Replace abstract nominalizations with verbs wherever possible. "The presence of sin in the human heart" becomes "sin makes its home in you." "The impossibility of self-generated faith" becomes "faith you cannot manufacture." Verbs move; nouns sit. A page full of sitting words sits on the reader.

### III.7 — Adjectives Are a Tax

Every adjective must pay its toll or be cut. "Profound grace" adds nothing to "grace." "Glorious truth" is weaker than "truth." Reserve adjectives for the rare moment they earn their place by doing specific, load-bearing work — usually when they carry a surprise ("the devastating comfort," "the humiliating cure").

## IV. IMAGE ECONOMY

### IV.1 — One Central Image, Carried Through

Every page has one load-bearing image and it recurs. The rope in *hands-that-hold-you.* The burning building in *rescued-without-a-say.* The mirror in *the mirror you refuse.* The envelope in *tender-the-letter-already-written.* The image is introduced in the opener, deepened in the middle, paid off in the close.

A page with three unrelated images is a page with none. Commit. The single image, carried through, becomes the page's spine.

### IV.2 — The Secular Bridge

Each page is strengthened by one — never more than one — bridge to a secular phenomenon the reader already believes. Libet's experiments. Anosognosia. Phantom limbs. The lottery ticket on the street. The drowning man in the lifeboat. The Newsradio-level joke that loops into the doctrine (the man thanking himself for accepting a check).

The bridge does two things simultaneously: it validates the theology in a vocabulary the reader already trusts, and it lets the reader feel they discovered the conclusion themselves rather than being told it. Both effects are non-trivial.

### IV.3 — The Banned Images

Some images have been overused in Christian writing to the point of cliché and are permanently off the menu: the footprints in the sand, the spotless Lamb-as-generic-metaphor, the athlete running a race, the soldier in God's army, the diamond in the rough, the rainbow after the storm, the potter and clay (cliché in most uses — acceptable only when tied directly to Romans 9), the rose pushing through concrete.

Original images or none. The cliché signals to the reader that no one was actually thinking when they wrote it.

## V. VERSE INTEGRATION

### V.1 — NIV Only, No Suffix

Every Scripture quotation is NIV. No parenthetical "(NIV)" after the citation — we weld the translation-bias escape hatch shut by not naming it. When Romans 9 says what it says in the reader's own Bible, the reader has nowhere to flee.

### V.2 — Paraphrase Is Prohibited

Scripture quoted is Scripture verbatim. "Paul basically says" and "the Bible teaches that" (followed by anything Scripture did not verbatim say) are forbidden. The power of the site rests on the reader seeing the text itself, unfiltered. Never summarize what the text says when you could quote what it says.

### V.3 — Verses Land; They Do Not Ornament

A verse is placed at the exact moment the argument requires the text to do the work. Never as decoration. Never as "a thematic verse to round out the section." The placement law: if you cut the verse, does the argument collapse? If yes, it was placed correctly. If no, it was ornament — cut it.

### V.4 — The Verse Gloss

After a verse that carries weight, one paragraph may gloss — slowly, reverently, word by word — what the reader just read. The gloss is not a paraphrase; it is an illumination.

Exemplar (devotional-the-hands-that-hold-you):
> "*To keep you from stumbling.* The Greek is *phylaxai* — to guard, to protect, to station a sentry. This is military language. God has posted a guard over your soul."

The gloss move works best when the verse has been quoted in its entirety and the gloss slows down to hold one phrase at a time.

### V.5 — Scripture Stacking

When the weight of the whole page requires Scripture to prove the point overwhelmingly, three verses stacked on the same truth, with no commentary between them, is more powerful than any number of sentences arguing. Let the Bible close the argument in its own voice.

## VI. FORBIDDEN MOVES

These are the anti-patterns. Each, when committed, drops the page at least one tier of quality and often dismantles the trust the rest of the page built.

- **The 2 AM Framing.** No "somewhere tonight, at 2 AM, someone is searching." This framing reduces the reader to a crisis persona. Aaron's standing direction: art, not crisis-packaging. (See feedback_writing_posture.md.)
- **Cringe-Grade Theological Slang.** No "TULIP™ drop." No "let's dive into soteriology." No "doctrine of election" (it is "the doctrines of grace"). No "Calvinism says" (it is "Scripture teaches"). The labels flatten what the prose is trying to elevate.
- **The TL;DR.** Banned sitewide. Use "In Brief" if summary is needed. Usually no summary is needed.
- **The Pastoral Sigh.** "Friend, let me say this gently..." / "Dear reader..." / "Beloved..." — these read as affectation. Intimacy is produced by the density of the prose, not by appending terms of endearment.
- **The Rhetorical Lift.** "But oh, the glory of this truth!" / "What a wondrous God we serve!" — these substitute exclamation for argument. Earn the wonder; never announce it.
- **Stacking Adjectives.** "Deep, profound, unshakable, inexhaustible grace" is weaker than "grace." Every adjective fights every other adjective for space; the reader's attention collapses.
- **Aspirational Links.** Never `<a href="/page.html">` to a page that does not yet exist. Verify every target. Six verified links beat twelve with five broken.
- **The Author's Appearance.** The writer does not enter the page in first-person-singular as "I" or as "the author." The "we" of shared humanity is permitted; the "I" of the writer is not.
- **Applause Lines.** No sentence written to make the reader clap. Every sentence is written to make the reader's chest tighten or loosen, not to make them admire the writer.
- **Crisis Triage Rhetoric.** No urgency-framing of the reader as a search-intent target. No "you searched for X." The reader arrived; meet them in the art, not in the marketing funnel.

## VII. THE ELEVATION — HOW TO PUSH ABOVE CURRENT BEST

The existing masterpieces on the site (named above) are the floor of this voice bible, not the ceiling. Pages produced under VOICE.md must exceed them. Four deliberate elevations:

### VII.1 — Compression

Every existing masterpiece has fat. A page that currently runs 2,200 words of near-masterpiece prose can almost always be compressed to 1,650 words of masterpiece prose. The discipline is brutal: every paragraph must answer *what work does this do that another paragraph could not do?* If it does not answer, cut it. The page becomes denser; the reader absorbs more per minute of reading; the impact ratio climbs.

### VII.2 — Image Precision

Where the current best uses a good image, push for the perfect one. "The fireman carrying you out" is good. "The fireman carrying you out of a room where you were lying facing the baseboard" is unforgettable — because the baseboard is specific enough to live in the reader's imagination for years. Every image gets pushed one concreteness-notch closer to the thing itself.

### VII.3 — The Sentence of the Page

Every page must have one sentence that is written to be tattooable. A sentence the reader could screenshot, frame, and tape to a bathroom mirror. Not a slogan — a sentence that contains the theology of the entire page compressed into one load-bearing line.

Exemplars:
- "The price was paid before the bill arrived."
- "You were not the hero. You were the cargo."
- "The hand that mattered was not yours."
- "Even his outstretched hand was a gift."
- "Dead men do not rise from their graves by trying. They rise because someone walks to the mouth of the cave and calls them out."

No page enters the Hammer 50 queue in a rewritten state until this sentence exists inside it, locatable by the writer, marked by the writer as *this one.* If the writer cannot point to the tattooable sentence, the rewrite is not done.

### VII.4 — The Verse the Reader Has Never Heard This Way

Every page must do to one verse what no prior treatment has done: read it slow enough, or from an angle oblique enough, that the reader who has read that verse two hundred times in their life feels they are reading it for the first time. This is the rarest art in Christian writing, and the one that most distinguishes a masterpiece from a competent essay.

The technique: take a verse that has become wallpaper for the reader (John 3:16, Ephesians 2:8-9, Romans 8:28) and land on one word of it the reader has never noticed. "But God." "By grace." "Through faith." "Not by works." Stop. Stay. Make the reader see that single word the way they have never seen it. Let the rest of the verse become new because of the one word they finally heard.

## VIII. THE CLOSING PROTOCOL (THE FINAL PARAGRAPH)

The last paragraph of every page is sacred real estate. It follows a specific shape.

**Line 1: The tender fact.** A single declarative sentence that names the reality the reader has just come to see. Usually a timeline statement. "You were chosen before the foundation of the world." "The hands were on you before you noticed."

**Lines 2–3: The invitation.** Not a call to action — a door left open. "Come home." "Rest." "Open your hand." Permission, not pressure.

**Final line: The hammer wrapped in a pillow.** Short. Absolute. Unmovable. This is the sentence the reader takes to bed.

Exemplars of the final line:
- "He has you. He always did."
- "The door has been open since before you were born."
- "You are out."
- "So is He."
- "He came anyway."

If the writer cannot find the hammer-wrapped-in-a-pillow, the page is not done. Stay with it until the line exists.

## IX. THE HYPERLINK DISCIPLINE

Every Hammer page carries 10–14 internal prose links. Not decorative — structural. The page is a node in a web; the links are the web. Laws:

1. **Verify every link before writing it.** If `ls name.html` does not produce a file, the link is banned. No aspirational linking. Ever.
2. **First mention of a concept gets the link.** Not every mention — the first.
3. **Cross-category links carry the highest weight.** A devotional linking to a philosophy piece, a question linking to a story, a demolition linking to a catch — these links are what turn a filing cabinet into a living web.
4. **Links live in prose only.** Never in attribute, comment, tag, or class context. Every broken production site on this project has been broken by a link injected into HTML structure; it will not happen again.
5. **Link text is never "click here" or "read more."** The link text is a meaningful phrase that tells the reader what they are about to step into. "the grace that terrifies you" → good. "this article" → forbidden.

## X. THE CANONICAL STRUCTURE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

Every new page or Hammer rewrite ships with exactly this shape. No creativity in structure. Creativity goes into prose; structure is welded.

```
<head>
  ...
  <link rel="stylesheet" href="/global.css">
</head>

[paste /_nav-template.html verbatim]

<main>
  <header class="page-hero">
    <span class="eyebrow">Category</span>
    <h1>Title<br><span class="gold">Subtitle</span></h1>
    <p class="subtitle">One-sentence hook.</p>
  </header>

  <article class="article-body">
    ...prose, H2s, images, verses, links...
  </article>
</main>

<footer>
  ...
  <div class="footer-grace-warning">
    "Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means!" — Romans 6:1-2<br>
    Grace is not a license to sin. It is the power that frees us from sin's dominion.
  </div>
  ...
</footer>

<script src="/nav.js"></script>
```

No inline `<style>` blocks unless the page is a documented interactive widget. No custom card classes (all dead — use `.hub-card` family). No `.article-toc` or `.section-nav` — permanently banned.

## XI. THE PRE-SHIP CHECKLIST

Before any rewrite is considered complete, every line on this list passes:

1. **The opener is one of the four permitted shapes.**
2. **The H2s, read in sequence, tell the whole story and escalate.**
3. **The final 20% lands the reader in the right arm.**
4. **At least six of the Twelve Lethal Moves appear, each used in accordance with its law.**
5. **At least one Somatic Awareness paragraph, at most one.**
6. **Exactly one central image, carried throughout.**
7. **Exactly one secular bridge, if any.**
8. **Sentence-length variance is audible in every paragraph.**
9. **At least one three-beat parallel sequence.**
