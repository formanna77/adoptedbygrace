History

The African Fathers: Augustine Wasn't Alone

Before Geneva. Before Wittenberg. Before Rome knew what to do with him. The doctrine of grace has African roots — and the father of Reformed theology was only the last in a long line of North Africans who had been preaching sovereignty for two hundred years before he was born.

There is a map of church history taught in most American seminaries, and on that map the doctrine of grace is a European phenomenon. It starts with a Swiss lawyer in a cold city on a lake. It runs backward through a German monk in a tower in Wittenberg and forward through Dutch theologians in a canal town called Dordrecht. The map is coherent. It is tidy. It is also wrong — or at least, it cuts off the most important part of the story. Because the doctrine of grace did not begin in Europe. It began in Africa. And the man the Europeans call "the father of Reformed theology" would have been insulted by the title. He would have said he was standing on the shoulders of men who had been saying these things in Carthage and Hippo and Alexandria when Geneva was still a Roman outpost and most of Europe was forest and smoke.

If you have ever heard a well-meaning critic say that Calvinism is a "dead white European theology," you are listening to a man who has not read his own history. Reformed theology is not white. It is not European. Its earliest and fiercest defenders were speaking Punic and Latin under the North African sun, pastoring congregations of Berbers and Romans and freed slaves, and writing some of the most radical grace theology the church would ever produce — in sentences that would not have been out of place on the lips of Spurgeon.

Tertullian: The African Lawyer Who Invented Theological Vocabulary

His full name was Quintus Septimius Florens Tertullianus, and he was born around 155 AD in Carthage, the great port city on the coast of what is now Tunisia. His father, by most accounts, was a Roman centurion stationed in the province. Tertullian trained as a lawyer, became one of the sharpest Latin rhetoricians of his generation, and then — sometime before 197 AD — he was converted and turned his lawyer's brain on the heretics threatening the early church. He did not write like a philosopher. He wrote like a prosecutor. Every sentence is a closing argument.

Tertullian is the man who gave us the Latin word Trinitas — Trinity. He coined the theological formula "one substance, three persons" that still anchors the orthodox doctrine of the Godhead. He gave us words like sacramentum, persona, and substantia. He essentially invented Christian Latin as a theological language. But what most Reformed readers never hear is that Tertullian, a century before the Pelagian controversy, was already writing about the radical inability of the human will apart from grace.

"The soul, corrupted at its roots by the first fall, has no power of itself to return to what it was. Only the divine hand that made it can remake it."

PARAPHRASED FROM TERTULLIAN, ON THE SOUL

Read Tertullian's On the Soul and his Against Marcion, and you will find a man who believed that sin has wrecked human nature so thoroughly that the sinner cannot repair himself. You will find the seeds of what would later be called total depravity — not as a systematic doctrine, but as an obvious fact assumed throughout the prose. Tertullian was not a Calvinist. No one in the second century was. But the building blocks were already being laid in Carthage, and the man laying them had dark skin and a Punic accent.

Cyprian: The Bishop Who Preached Grace Under Persecution

Fifty years after Tertullian, the chair of the bishop of Carthage was filled by another African: Thascius Caecilius Cyprianus — Cyprian. He was born around 200 AD, probably in Carthage itself, to a wealthy pagan family. He was a celebrated rhetorician, a lawyer, a man of position and comfort. Then, sometime around 245, he met an old priest named Caecilius who led him to Christ. Cyprian gave away his wealth, took the orders of priesthood, and by 249 was consecrated bishop of Carthage — one of the three or four most influential sees in the Western church.

Cyprian's African church was not a safe church. Within a year of his consecration, the emperor Decius launched the first empire-wide persecution, and Christians across North Africa faced a choice: burn incense to Caesar or die. Many died. Many more — the lapsi, "the lapsed" — denied Christ to save their lives. When the persecution ended, the lapsed wanted back into the church. Cyprian had to decide what to do with them, and the decisions he made shaped Christian pastoral theology for the next thousand years.

What interests us here, though, is not Cyprian's theory of readmission. It is the fact that under pressure, when his sheep were being dragged before magistrates and thrown to lions, the doctrine he preached was not self-help or moral exhortation. It was sovereign grace. He wrote: "We should rather glory and rejoice, that whatever happens contrary to the usual course of nature, is directed by God's will." He understood that a persecuted church only survives on a theology of sovereignty — because when the sword is at your throat, you need a God who has planned every hair of your head and every second of your agony, and who has promised that not one of His sheep will be lost. A god whose plans can be thwarted by an emperor is no comfort to a martyr. Only an absolutely sovereign God will do.

"For what have you that you did not receive? And if you did receive it, why do you boast as though you did not?"

1 CORINTHIANS 4:7 — PAUL'S QUESTION, CYPRIAN'S FAVORITE

Augustine would later quote 1 Corinthians 4:7 as the verse that clinched his case against Pelagius. Cyprian had been quoting it in the same spirit one hundred and fifty years earlier, in a language (Punic-inflected Latin) and a context (African Christianity under Roman persecution) that had nothing to do with medieval Europe. The verse was already doing in 250 AD what it would do in 418 AD and in 1519 AD and in your living room tonight: stripping the sinner of every ground for boasting and leaving him with nothing but the gift.

The North African Theological Climate

When Augustine of Hippo was born in 354 AD in the town of Thagaste (in modern Algeria), the African church into which he was born had already been reading Tertullian for a hundred and fifty years and Cyprian for a hundred. That church had a distinctive theological DNA. It was rigorist, it was Scripture-soaked, it was persecution-hardened, and it was suspicious of the kinds of optimistic humanism that kept drifting in from the Greek East. The African church had no illusions about human nature. It had seen its own bishops crack under torture. It had watched wealthy converts apostatize at the first whiff of trouble. It knew, by experience, that the human will is not a reliable engine of fidelity — and that if anyone perseveres under pressure, it is because God is holding them up, not because they have the strength to hold themselves.

This is the theological soil into which Augustine was planted. His famous doctrine of grace did not drop out of the sky in 396 when he wrote To Simplician, the little book that crystallized his mature position. It was the harvest of two centuries of African reflection on what happens to a church when persecution strips away every illusion about human capacity. Augustine was not an innovator. He was a curator. He gathered the threads that Tertullian and Cyprian and the anonymous African bishops of the Donatist crisis had been weaving, and he braided them into a rope strong enough to hold the Western church for a thousand years.

When Pelagius arrived in Rome from Britain around 380 and began teaching that human beings could, by natural effort, fulfill the commands of God, Augustine heard him the way a Tunisian bishop would naturally hear such a claim — as obviously, catastrophically, heretically wrong. The African church had learned, at the cost of its own blood, that the sinner could do no such thing. It took an African to recognize the Pelagian lie for what it was, because it took an African church to have already proven, in real time and under real torture, that sinners cannot hold themselves together apart from the grace that is doing all the holding.

Why the Africans Saw What the Europeans Missed

Here is a claim worth sitting with: the doctrine of sovereign grace was most clearly articulated by Christians who were on the underside of history. Not by comfortable academics. Not by state-church theologians in warm studies. But by Africans under Decius, by Frenchmen fleeing the Marian burnings, by Scottish Covenanters hunted across the moors, by Korean Presbyterians under Japanese occupation, by Chinese house-church pastors in labor camps. Whenever the church is comfortable, it drifts toward a theology that flatters the comfortable. Whenever the church bleeds, it rediscovers that the God who is holding it together is doing all the holding.

Tertullian and Cyprian were not theorizing about grace. They were living off it. They were watching their own congregations get shredded and still preaching, because the only thing that keeps a church preaching under that kind of pressure is a theology in which God's purposes cannot fail and God's elect cannot be lost. Everything else collapses. Free-will theology is a luxury item. It is what you preach when the worst problem you have is whether your coffee bar has enough pastries for Sunday morning. It is not what you preach when you are counting corpses in the basilica after the soldiers have left.

This is why the African fathers saw the grace of God so clearly. Not because they were smarter than the Greeks. Not because their Latin was better than everyone else's Latin (though it often was). But because their pews were full of people who had been broken and put back together by nothing but grace, and they knew it, and they were not about to let any visiting philosopher talk them into a theology that handed out credit to the broken people for their own rescue.

Why This Matters Now

Two things. First: if you have ever felt a quiet nervousness that Reformed theology might be a Western, culturally conditioned, maybe even colonial imposition on an otherwise free gospel — you can put that nervousness down. The deepest roots of the doctrines of grace are not in Europe. They are in Africa. They are in the prayers of Cyprian in a prison cell in 258, waiting to be beheaded. They are in the legal briefs of Tertullian, who gave the church the word Trinity in the same prose rhythm with which he insisted the will is bound by sin. They are in the pastoral exhaustion of Augustine, watching the empire collapse around him while the Vandals surrounded Hippo, and yet preaching that God's city cannot be conquered because God's election is secure. These are not the views of colonial theologians. These are the views of the colonized — the North African church writing under Roman occupation, insisting that the Emperor does not get the final word because God does.

Second: if the theology of sovereign grace was already present, already fierce, already African in 250 AD, then the modern evangelical habit of treating it as a narrow sectarian position belongs to someone else's playbook. You are not resisting a niche seventeenth-century Dutch invention when you bristle at election. You are resisting the theology that was keeping the North African church alive when the lions were loose. You are resisting the theology that the church already settled, over and over, in every generation that actually had to rely on it for its survival. You are, to put it plainly, resisting Cyprian.

The question is why. And the answer, if you will let it land, is this: you are resisting because the flesh cannot hear this doctrine without feeling its own death. And you resist because in a comfortable American church, you have never had to rely on it the way Cyprian did. You have not been dragged before a magistrate. You have not watched your congregation die for the name. You have never had to stake your survival on the promise that God is the one holding the rope. So you do what comfortable Christians have always done: you hold on to a sliver of credit for yourself — the sliver that says "I chose." And as long as that sliver stays in your hand, the African fathers are making you nervous.

The Question No One Asks

If the African church — persecuted, brilliant, fiercely Scriptural, a full century earlier than Augustine — was already preaching sovereign grace, then who exactly is the latecomer in this story? Not the Reformed. The latecomer is the free-will theology that slipped in through the comfort of the post-Constantinian empire and has been posing as "the original gospel" ever since. You were told that sovereign grace was the innovation and that libertarian free will was the default. The historical record says the opposite. The African church had been saying, in Cyprian's words, "What do you have that you did not receive?" for a hundred years before anyone bothered to invent the Pelagian alternative. The lie is the latecomer. The grace is the original.

The Bridge

There is a strange comfort in realizing that your deepest theological convictions have older and darker-skinned ancestors than you knew. It means the grace you have found was not invented for you yesterday. It was being preached over the graves of martyrs seventeen hundred years ago, in a Tunisian church, in a language you will never speak, by a bishop you will never meet. The God who held Cyprian's neck steady at the sword is the same God who is holding yours steady right now, whatever the sword in your life looks like. He chose Cyprian before the foundation of the world, and He chose you before the foundation of the world, and the same sovereign purpose that carried a North African bishop through a Roman persecution is going to carry you through whatever is trying to undo you this morning.

The African fathers did not write their theology for scholars. They wrote it for sheep who needed to know, at 3 AM in a prison cell, whose hands were holding the rope. The answer has not changed. The rope has not frayed. The Shepherd is still the same yesterday and today and forever. And the chain from Cyprian to Augustine to Luther to Edwards to Spurgeon to the person reading this sentence is not a chain of academic agreement. It is a chain of rescued people holding a rope they did not tie, pulled to a shore they did not swim to, by a God who would not lose a single one.

That is the golden thread. It runs through Africa. It always did. And if that thread is wrapped around your wrist this morning, it was tied there before the stars were hung, by a Father whose sons and daughters come from every tribe and tongue and nation — beginning, very possibly, in a hot bright city on the coast of Tunisia, almost two thousand years ago.
