Start Here

But What About Free Will and Fairness?

If you've made it this far, you've earned the right to ask the hard questions—and you should. Every sincere soul who glimpses God's sovereignty feels the weight of these objections. That weight is where truth is forged. Let's face them not as obstacles to bypass, but as battlegrounds where Scripture proves its unshakeable power.

We take objections seriously. If Scripture teaches something we cannot defend under fire, we should abandon it. But here's what centuries of the church's deepest thinkers discovered: the objections don't weaken the case for sovereign grace—they prove it. Follow them all the way down, and every objection collapses against the bedrock of Scripture. (We promise. Scripture's answer is more devastating than the question.)

1
Objection
"What about free will? Doesn't God give us a choice?"
+

The Short Answer

You absolutely have a will. You make real choices every moment. The question is not whether you have one, but whether it's free from sin's slavery. Scripture teaches: it is not—not until you are reborn.

The Biblical Answer

Scripture is blunt: we are "slaves to sin" (John 8:34), "dead in trespasses" (Ephesians 2:1), blind to spiritual truth (1 Corinthians 2:14), and enemies of God (Romans 8:7). Each phrase carries its own weight. A slave is not free. A corpse does not choose. An enemy does not cooperate. These are not metaphors about weakness—they are the spiritual condition of every human being apart from grace.

John 8:34, 36
"Jesus answered them, 'Truly, truly, I say to you, everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin. … So if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed.'"

Notice the order: He sets you free first. Then you become truly free. Before the Spirit's work in you, your will is not a prisoner to someone else's force—it's a prisoner to your own nature. Your heart doesn't choose God because your heart does not want to. The enslaved will always chooses its chains.

Your will is free in one sense—you act according to your nature without coercion. But it is not free in the way that matters: without regeneration, you cannot want God, cannot seek Him, cannot come to Him. That ability? That desire? It is grace alone.

2
Objection
"Is God unfair if He chooses some and not others?"
+

The Short Answer

This is the objection Paul saw coming in Romans 9—and his answer doesn't explain the truth away. It strikes at the root of why the question itself is wrong.

The Biblical Answer

The objection rests on a false premise: that fairness demands God save everyone. But fairness would send everyone to judgment. We are not innocent parties waiting at heaven's gates. We are rebels in the dock. The question is not "Why doesn't God save everyone?" The only sensible question is "Why does God save anyone?" And the answer is scandalous: grace.

Romans 9:19–21
"You will say to me then, 'Why does he still find fault? For who can resist his will?' But who are you, O man, to answer back to God? Will what is molded say to its molder, 'Why have you made me like this?' Has the potter no right over the clay, to make out of the same lump one vessel for honorable use and another for dishonorable use?"

Paul doesn't soften the blow. He sharpens it. God is the potter; we are clay. The potter shapes the clay according to His wisdom and will. Is the clay going to argue with the potter? The staggering thing is not that God hardens whom He pleases—it is that He shows mercy at all.

Grace, by definition, is undeserved. The moment you argue that God must save everyone to be just, you've unmade grace entirely. You've turned a free gift into a legal obligation, a thunderclap into a hollow echo. Grace that is owed is grace that ceases to be.

3
Objection
"But John 3:16 says 'whosoever believes' — doesn't that mean anyone can?"
+

The Short Answer

Yes—absolutely. Whoever believes will be saved. Scripture is clear: faith in Christ brings salvation. The real question Scripture asks is deeper: but who will believe?

The Biblical Answer

"Whosoever believes shall be saved"—this is true. It describes the result of faith, not its origin. It tells you what happens when someone trusts Christ, but it does not explain why some do and others do not. The answer to that question lies elsewhere in Scripture:

John 6:65
"This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father."

And look at the context of John 3 itself. Just verses before the famous declaration, Jesus tells Nicodemus: "You must be born again" (John 3:7). The new birth is what makes belief possible. The Spirit—not your will, not your reasoning, not your strength—accomplishes this. Jesus says it this way: "The wind blows where it wishes" (John 3:8). You cannot make the wind blow. You can only receive it.

John 3:16 is glorious—a telescope into God's heart. He loved the world so deeply He gave His only Son. But it does not teach that we have the native ability to believe apart from His Spirit's work. It teaches that when God's Spirit makes us alive, and we believe, we will be saved. The promise is sure. The power is His.

4
Objection
"Doesn't God want all people to be saved? (1 Timothy 2:4)"
+

The Short Answer

God desires all kinds of people—the powerful and powerless, the near and far—to be saved. He is not a tribal God with a narrow love. But God also has a sovereign, eternal purpose that never fails. Both are true.

The Biblical Answer

Read 1 Timothy 2:1–4 for yourself. Paul tells Timothy to pray "for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions." Then: God "desires all people to be saved." Look at the movement: Paul is talking about different categories of people, not claiming God wishes to override His own will regarding every individual on Earth.

But here's the logic that matters most: if God truly desires every single human to be saved, and if God has the omnipotence to accomplish it, then why is anyone lost? The answer is inescapable—either God cannot do what He wishes (which denies His power) or His wishes are more mysterious than a single verse can contain (which Scripture affirms).

Isaiah 46:10
"My counsel shall stand, and I will accomplish all my purpose."

God always accomplishes His counsel. His will never fails. If His purpose were to save every individual without exception, then every soul would be safe in His hand. But they are not. What does that tell us? That His decreed will reaches deeper than we have measured, that His sovereignty is vaster than our objections, and that we are invited to trust Him even in the mysteries.

5
Objection
"Doesn't election make evangelism pointless?"
+

The Short Answer

Precisely the opposite. Election is the very fuel that fires the furnace of evangelism.

The Biblical Answer

Think it through: if salvation were left to the human will—to hearts corrupted by sin and hostile to God—then evangelism would be an exercise in futility. You'd be pleading with the spiritually dead to resurrect themselves. You'd have zero leverage, zero hope. But because God has chosen a people, you know that when the gospel is proclaimed, it will strike living ears. It will awaken the dead.

Acts 18:9–10
"And the Lord said to Paul one night in a vision, 'Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you, and no one will attack you to harm you, for I have many in this city who are my people.'"

Notice what moved Paul to action: God had already chosen people in that city. Election was his reason to preach, not his excuse to be silent. Paul preached because he knew the Word would not fail—not because he was eloquent, but because God had ordained both the message and the hearts it would reach.

Look at the evangelists God raised up in history's greatest seasons of harvest. Whitefield. Spurgeon. Edwards. Carey. Judson. Every one was a Calvinist, unashamed. They didn't hold back because they believed God was sovereign. They surged forward because of it. They preached with urgency and confidence, knowing that God's sheep would hear His voice and follow.

A final word: If you still have objections, that is not weakness—it is wisdom. The greatest saints in history were not born believing these truths; they were broken by them. Augustine wrestled. Luther wrestled for years. Spurgeon trembled. They did not suppress their doubts; they excavated them. They followed the objections deep into Scripture and found that God's word held, that His goodness was deeper than their questions, that His sovereignty was gentler than they feared. Your wrestling is holy. Follow your objections all the way down. Let Scripture answer. And in that answering, let yourself be transformed.

Romans 9:20
"But who are you, O man, to answer back to God?"

That verse is not meant to slam a door on your questions—it is meant to reorient them. It tells us that when Scripture speaks with clarity, our posture shifts from debate to listening, from negotiating to receiving. And in that receptive posture—where we stop arguing and start believing—we discover a peace so deep it defies the logic that once troubled us.

Reflection Questions

  1. Which objection landed deepest in your soul? Has Scripture's answer shifted your perspective, or are you still wrestling?
  2. Where did you learn what "fairness" means to you? Is that definition from Scripture, or from the culture around you? What would it mean to rebuild your understanding of fairness from the text?
  3. If God's sovereignty in salvation is true, does it draw you closer to worship—or push you away? What does your answer tell you about what you actually believe?
  4. Notice how Paul responds to objections: not with softening, but with the deepest truths (Potter, clay, Romans 9). What would change if you approached your doubts the same way—not looking for compromises, but for deeper Scripture?
