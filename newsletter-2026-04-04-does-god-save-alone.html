DAILY NEWSLETTER — THE DEBATE

Does God Save Alone — or Do You Help?

April 4, 2026

This is the oldest argument in Christianity. It has split churches, ended friendships, and determined how billions of people understand their own salvation. The question is deceptively simple: when God saves someone, does He do it alone — or does the person help?

Both sides believe they are honoring God. Both sides believe they are reading Scripture faithfully. Here they are, as honestly as possible.

The Case for Cooperation

God initiates. No one disputes that. He sends the gospel, provides the Spirit, and opens the door. But He does not drag anyone through it. He invites. "Whoever believes" — John 3:16 — implies a real choice. "Behold, I stand at the door and knock" — Revelation 3:20 — implies that someone must open it. Love, by definition, cannot be forced. A coerced "I love you" isn't love. So God, in His wisdom and His respect for the image He placed in us, extends grace and waits for the human heart to respond. Faith is a human act empowered by divine grace — a cooperation, not a coercion. God does ninety-nine percent. The person does one percent. But that one percent — the "yes" — is real, meaningful, and necessary.

The Case for God Alone

If the person does one percent and God does ninety-nine, then who is the decisive factor? The person. Because that one percent is the difference between heaven and hell. Nine hundred ninety-nine people could receive the same ninety-nine percent of grace — and the one who said "yes" is saved while the others are not. That makes the human decision, not God's grace, the determining factor. And a determining human decision is a work, no matter how small. Scripture says we are "dead in transgressions" — Ephesians 2:1 — not sick, not weakened, but dead. Corpses don't cooperate. They don't open doors. They don't say "yes." They lie in the grave until someone raises them. Faith itself is a gift — Ephesians 2:8-9 — not a contribution. God doesn't offer salvation and wait. He accomplishes it entirely, from first to last, so that no one may boast.

"So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."

ROMANS 9:16
