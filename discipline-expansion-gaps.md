## §I — Executive Summary

The site demonstrates strong foundational coverage of Reformed soteriology but has significant gaps in four of eight expanded registers. **Analytical theology** (1/5), **comparative theology** (2/5), **theological ethics** (1/5), and the **philosophy of theology** (2/5) are severely under-represented. **Reformed apologetics** (3/5) has ad-hoc presence in scattered articles but lacks systematic focus. **Reformed dogmatics** (4/5), **polemic apologetics** (2/5), and **presuppositional apologetics** (3/5) show moderate scaffolding. **The headline gap:** the site needs 40-50 new articles across all eight registers, with highest priority on comparative theology (10-12 new pieces naming non-Christian traditions), theological ethics (12-15 applied ethics pieces), and analytical theology (8-10 technical philosophical articles). These three registers alone account for approximately 35 of the recommended 48 new builds.

## §V — Cross-Cutting Observations

### A. Presuppositional Reasoning Already Pervasive (Consolidation Opportunity)

The site already demonstrates presuppositional thinking throughout demolition pieces, analogies, and philosophy articles. Many readers encounter the *method* without learning its name or history. **Recommendation:** Create a presuppositional-hub.html and a small cluster of meta-pieces (Van Til, Bahnsen, Frame) that make implicit presuppositional reasoning explicit. This will accelerate learning and deepen understanding of pieces already on the site.

### B. Comparative Theology Creates an Unforeseen Strength for Interfaith Outreach

The site currently positions itself narrowly as "for the elect," with an inward-facing soteriology. Comparative theology pieces will expand the site's reach to interfaith dialogue readers, young Christians in pluralistic settings, and apologetic students. **Recommendation:** Treat comparative theology as a *reaching arm* of the existing cathedral — these pieces deepen the site's mission (reaching the chosen) by removing interfaith barriers for seekers who are questioning Islam, Judaism, etc.

### C. Theological Ethics Bridges Theory and Life

The site's existing strength is theological *argument*. Theological ethics moves from "what does Scripture teach?" to "how do I live?" **Recommendation:** Ensure every ethics piece opens with a concrete case (a real abortion decision, a real marriage crisis, a real tech dilemma), then grounds the answer in doctrine and Scripture. These pieces will resonate with pastors and ordinary Christians more immediately than analytical theology or philosophy of theology.

### D. Analytical Theology Elevates Intellectual Rigor

Analytical theology will appeal to philosophy students and intellectually curious Christians uncomfortable with "faith" as an alternative to reason. **Recommendation:** Do not water down modal logic or metaphysical precision; the site's contribution is *clarity through rigor*, not *simplicity through reduction*. These pieces should be denser and shorter than standard site articles (2000-2400 words is appropriate for analytical theology; 3000+ is appropriate for systematic theology).

Phase A's consecration audit showed that most articles lack doxological closure and Christological apex. The new discipline-expansion pieces should be born *fully consecrated* — written from inception with all twelve marks in mind. **Recommendation:** All 56 new pages should carry both `<!-- HAMMER-LOCKED -->` and `<!-- CONSECRATED -->` markers, and should be written under AUDIT-2.md §II's twelve-mark test.
