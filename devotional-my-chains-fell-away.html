My Chains Fell Away

On grace that does not ask permission — and the freedom that follows

The Prisoner and the Open Door

Picture a prisoner who has spent decades in darkness. The chains have worn grooves into his wrists — grooves so familiar he no longer feels them. He has stopped trying to escape. Worse: he has grown to love the chains. They are his identity. He would not know himself without them.

One day, the door opens. Not from inside. From outside. A hand reaches in — scarred, pierced, a hand that has worn chains of its own. The prisoner flinches. Freedom terrifies him more than captivity ever did.

But the hand does not ask permission.

The chains shatter. The prisoner stumbles into light so brilliant it blinds him. He is free. Not because he chose it. Not because he earned it. But because someone who loved him more than he loved his bondage reached in and broke it.

Charles Wesley understood. "My chains fell off, my heart was free." Not "my chains slowly loosened." Not "I gradually escaped." Fell off. In a moment. In an act not his own. And then the cry that only the truly liberated can make: "I rose, went forth, and followed Thee."

What Is True Freedom?

Our culture worships autonomy — the power to choose, to define yourself, to answer to no one. We call this freedom. Scripture calls it something else entirely.

True freedom is not the power to choose God. True freedom is being freed from the bondage that makes you unable to choose Him.

A man enslaved to alcohol does not need more options at the bar. He needs to be liberated from the desire that is killing him. A heart in love with sin does not need a wider menu. It needs a transplant. A soul bound in spiritual darkness does not need the ability to find God on his own terms. He needs a hand to reach in and drag him into light.

This is what makes grace dangerous. Grace does not respect your autonomy. Grace does not ask permission. Grace reaches into the prison and breaks chains the prisoner loves — then rewires the prisoner's heart so that he loves the light more than he ever loved the dark.

For freedom Christ has set us free; stand firm therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery.

Galatians 5:1 (ESV)

Notice Paul's stunning redundancy. For freedom Christ has set us free. You are not freed for probation. Not freed for a trial period. You are freed for freedom itself. And then the command: stand firm. Do not pick up new chains. Do not crawl back into the cell. You have been liberated — now live like it.

The Slavery We Do Not Recognize

Jesus answered them, "Truly, truly, I say to you, everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin. The slave does not remain in the house forever; the son remains forever. So if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed."

John 8:34-36 (ESV)

Sin is not a collection of bad decisions. Sin is slavery. And the most terrifying thing about slavery is this: the enslaved think they are free.

The Pharisees were certain they were free. Children of Abraham. Keepers of the law. Masters of their own spiritual destiny. Jesus looked at them and saw men in chains so old they had mistaken the weight for the weight of their own bones. They loved their bondage — not because it was good, but because it was theirs. The thought of bowing before a Messiah they could not control terrified them more than the slavery they could not see.

This is the deepest tragedy. Not that the chains are on us. That we do not want them removed. We practice sin not because we are forced to, but because — in our fallenness — we have come to love it more than holiness. We love the comfort of known darkness more than the terror of unfamiliar light.

Reflect: What chains do you love? Not the sins you know are wrong. The ones you have rationalized into virtues. The ones that feel like freedom — and are anything but.

The Miracle of Changed Desires

Here is where grace becomes something other religions cannot even imagine. God does not simply break your chains and shove you into light you hate. That would be torture, not salvation. Instead, the Spirit does something infinitely more radical: He makes the prisoner want to be free.

He changes what you love. He rewires the deepest machinery of your heart so that holiness — which once looked like death — suddenly looks like the only life worth living. After the chains fell off Wesley's wrists, he did not run back to his old life. He rose, went forth, and followed Christ. Why? Because the same grace that broke his chains had broken his love of sin. He could no more return to bondage than a man raised from the dead could prefer the coffin.

This is what irresistible grace actually means: not force that overrides your will, but transformation so deep it captures your will. A person with a new heart is not coerced into holiness. She is liberated into it. Freedom is not choosing between options. Freedom is desiring the good — and choosing it because you finally, truly, want to.

Augustine knew. After his conversion he wrote: "O Lord, you had broken my chains. I will offer to you the sacrifice of praise." Not "I will offer if I feel like it." The chains were broken, the heart was new, and worship was the only response that made any sense at all.

The Hard Truth About Freedom

Real freedom is not the freedom to do whatever you want. Real freedom is the freedom from wanting what destroys you. This is scandalous to modern ears — but it is the deepest truth of the gospel.

You are free. Truly, eternally free. But you are free the way a child is free in the arms of a father — not independent from Him, but safe in Him. Your liberty is not autonomy. Your liberty is belonging to someone whose love will never let you go and whose wisdom will never lead you wrong.

So Jesus said to the Jews who had believed him, "If you abide in my word, you are truly my disciples, and you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."

John 8:31-32 (ESV)

The chains have fallen. The door is open. The light is brilliant and terrifying and real. The question is not whether you can be free — in Christ, the chains are already broken. The question is whether you will stand in that freedom, or crawl back into a cell that was never home.

And this is what makes the gospel news so unimaginably good: not that you have the freedom to choose God, but that God has the power to choose you, free you, and transform you so completely that worshiping Him becomes not obligation — but the deepest joy you have ever known.

A Closing Prayer

God, You saw me in my chains — loving them, defending them, building my identity on the very things that were killing me. And You reached in anyway. Your scarred hand broke what my willing hands could never release.

Now keep me free. Make me love the light more than I ever loved the dark. And when I am tempted to crawl back into that cell, remind me: the door was opened from the outside, by hands that will never, ever let me go. Amen.
