The Parable

The Seed That Spoke

April 4, 2026

Once, in a field that had forgotten its own name, a seed lay buried.

It had been there longer than it could remember. The darkness above it was so total that the seed had come to believe darkness was the only thing that existed. It did not know there was a sun. It did not know there was rain. It did not know there were other seeds, or fields, or a Gardener who had placed it exactly where it lay.

The seed had opinions about itself. It believed it was self-sufficient. It believed that if anything ever changed, it would be because the seed had decided to change. It had a philosophy: "I am the author of my own becoming."

One morning — though the seed didn't know it was morning, because it didn't know about mornings — water arrived. The seed hadn't asked for it. Hadn't summoned it. Hadn't even known it was thirsty. But the water came anyway, seeping through the black soil, finding the seed's shell, softening what had been hard for years.

Then warmth. Not from the seed — the seed had no warmth of its own. From above. From a source the seed still couldn't see and didn't believe in. The warmth penetrated the soil, reached the shell, and did something the seed could not have done to itself in a thousand years of trying: it woke the life inside.

The seed cracked. Not violently — tenderly, the way dawn cracks the horizon. And from inside the shell, a green thread emerged. Fragile. Reaching upward through dirt it didn't choose, toward light it couldn't see, propelled by a life it didn't generate.

When the seedling finally broke through the surface and felt the sun on its face for the first time, it said — because in this story, seeds can speak — "I did it. I chose to grow."

The Gardener, who had selected the seed from His storehouse, planted it in that exact spot, sent the rain at that exact hour, and aimed the sun at that exact angle, smiled.

He did not correct the seedling. Not yet. He knew that in time, as it grew taller and its roots went deeper and it began to understand how roots work — that they receive, that they do not manufacture — the seedling would look down at the soil and the water and the light and realize: none of this was mine. All of it was given.

And on that day, the seedling would not feel robbed of its story. It would feel, for the first time, the full weight of what it means to be planted.

"I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth. So neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but only God who gives the growth."
— 1 Corinthians 3:6-7
