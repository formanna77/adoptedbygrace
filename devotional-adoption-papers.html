Adoption Papers

A meditation on being chosen and adopted as a child of God

Before You Existed, You Were Chosen

A couple in a coffee shop, a country away from a child they've never met, signs papers that will reshape everything. The child—you—might not even exist yet. You've done nothing. Asked for nothing. Earned nothing. And yet somewhere, a pen moves across a page. Your name appears. Your future solidifies. Not by your choice. Not by your effort. By someone else's.

This is adoption. This is what God did before the world began.

Paul's opening to Ephesians should stop your breath:

He chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and blameless before him. In love he predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ, according to the purpose of his will.

Ephesians 1:4-5 (ESV)

Before stars burned. Before earth existed. Before your first breath. God marked the path. Not as a maybe. Not as a possibility. He predestined it—proorizo in the Greek, which means He carved it certain. He didn't wait to see if you'd qualify. He settled it before time started.

What Roman Adoption Actually Was

When Rome adopted a child, they didn't casually welcome a guest. They made them irrevocably, permanently, completely family.

You inherited everything: the wealth, the name, the honor. Your old family's debts? No longer yours. Their shame? Erased. You were born new. The child you had been was legally, spiritually dead. A different person stood in your place.

This is Paul's image. God hasn't provisionally adopted you, hasn't contingently welcomed you, hasn't invited you to try being family. He's made you His child. Permanently. The blood of Christ made it legitimate. The decree of the Father made it official.

You are not striving for a seat at the table. You are already sitting there. You are not a guest. You are home.

The Cry That Proves It

Paul writes to the Romans about how you know adoption is real:

For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, "Abba! Father!" The Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God, and if children, then heirs—heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ, provided we suffer with him in order that we may also be glorified with him.

Romans 8:15-17 (ESV)

The evidence: the cry "Abba! Father!" You don't teach an orphan to call someone Father. You don't train it. The cry comes from something deeper—something that knows, in the marrow, that it belongs.

That cry in your heart isn't your achievement. It's the Spirit's witness. He vouches for you. He testifies with your spirit that you are a child of God. Not earned. Not performed. Testified to. And from that testimony flows everything: you are an heir, not of God's leftover benevolence, but of God Himself. You inherit His glory. You will sit where Christ sits.

Reflect: When you pray, does "Father" come easily, or does it feel distant? The proof of adoption is the ability to cry "Abba"—with tenderness, not terror. If that cry doesn't come naturally, ask the Spirit to teach you to say your Father's name.

You Did Not Choose—You Were Chosen

This doctrine strikes like a sword through human pride. You didn't apply. Didn't interview. Didn't argue your case. You were chosen. Before you breathed. Without permission. Without even knowing.

Sin had claims on you. The law had claims on you. You were an orphan in the deepest sense—abandoned, undone, with no power to rescue yourself.

And Jesus came. The eternal Son reached into the wreckage of your life and said, "That one. Mine." The Father agreed. The papers were signed in blood.

You cannot boast about it. You cannot claim it as wages for goodness. You were chosen. The only honest response is gratitude. The only rational response is wonder. The only logical life is the life of someone who holds an incomparable gift—one you could never have earned, one that can never be revoked because it was never earned in the first place.

J.I. Packer called adoption "the highest privilege of the gospel." Not redemption alone. Not justification alone. But adoption—the reality that you are God's child, that you bear His name, that you inherit His promises. Why? Because redemption exists so that you can be adopted. Justification exists so that you can be adopted. Every doctrine of grace points toward this: you are brought home, brought into family, brought to belong.

The Inheritance That Is Yours Now

What did the Roman child inherit? Everything the father possessed.

You inherit eternal life—not as a possibility if you perform well, but as the certain gift of a child brought home forever.

You inherit the Father's love. Not conditional. Not mercurial. But the love that looks at your worst self and says, "You are still mine. You are still loved."

You inherit glory. Paul says you are "fellow heirs with Christ." His resurrection becomes yours. His seat at the Father's right hand becomes your destination. His splendor becomes yours—not in becoming God, but in knowing forever that you are loved and treasured by Him.

You inherit the name itself. In ancient adoption, a new name marked the birth into a new family. God gives you a new identity. Not orphan. Not condemned. Not separated. Not lost. You are a child of God.

The Identity That Changes Everything

When you feel worthless, the truth is: you have been adopted. Your name is on papers signed before time began.

When you feel abandoned, the truth is: your Father will not leave you. This is not sentiment. This is the irrevocable pledge of God.

When you wonder if you truly belong, the truth is: your adoption is as real as Christ's blood. Your home is as certain as the Father's heart.

You are no longer an orphan. You are family. You are home. Live like it.

A Closing Prayer

O God, my Father—even to say it fills me with wonder. I didn't choose you. I wasn't worthy. I earned nothing. Yet before time existed, you looked ahead and chose me. You wrote my name on papers of adoption.

That you, infinite and eternal, would claim me as family? That you would send your Son to bleed for my adoption? That you would seal it with your Spirit, making me cry "Father" with a tenderness I could never have found alone?

Help me live as your adopted child. Help me stop earning what's already been given. Help me accept what is mine. Help me call you Father—not from fear, but from the deep, bone-deep knowledge that I am loved, that I am home, that I belong to you forever.

Nothing can take this away. For the praise of your great name. Amen.
