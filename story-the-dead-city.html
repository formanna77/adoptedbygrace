The Dead City

An allegory on regeneration and election — where the awakening comes not by choice, but by grace.

The city had always been dead. No one knew this because no one had ever been alive.

It was a city of habit. Morning came, and the people rose from their beds in the gray light that felt like sunlight. They moved through routines that felt like choice: a bitter liquid they called coffee, work they called purpose, conversations they called connection. They felt the dust on their tongues and believed it was the taste of living. They moved their lips and thought they heard each other's words. They walked the streets and thought they were going somewhere.

But they were not alive. They were corpses walking, and the city was a grave.

The people of the city had names and faces, but no one could see color. The sky was grey, the buildings were grey, the faces were grey. They passed grey structures they called markets and grey spaces they called homes. A woman stood before a grey mirror and saw grey, and believed this was seeing. A man held something grey and smooth and felt it and believed he was touching. No one spoke of color because no one had ever known color existed. The very concept was impossible to them—a dead city cannot dream of light.

Sound, too, was absent, though no one noticed. The people produced noise—the scrape of chairs, the murmur of voices—but there was no music, no resonance, no harmony. Just the mechanical grinding of routine. A child once asked what singing was. The elders had no answer. It was not a thing that was, so it could not be taught. The question faded like all questions fade in a place where wonder is impossible.

They could eat, but they tasted nothing. Food passed through them like ash. They brought it to their mouths because the dead are taught to feed themselves, and so they did, swallowing dust and calling it nourishment. A baker once wondered what her bread might taste like to someone alive. She never finished the thought. To wonder such a thing would be to admit that something was missing. And in a dead city, nothing is admitted to be missing.

This was the arrangement of their world: a city of the dead, pretending to be alive. And the dead are excellent pretenders. They practice the motions until the motions feel like existence.

✦

Then, one day, a Voice spoke from beyond the city walls.

It came from outside, from a place the dead did not know existed. The Voice was not like the noise the dead made to each other. It was alive. It was warm. It carried in it something the dead had no word for—something that sounded like music, though the city had no music. It sounded like color, though the city had no color. It sounded like taste, like sweetness, like belonging, like home.

Most of the dead did not hear the Voice. It passed over them like wind passes through a graveyard—present, but unnoticed. They continued their routines, their grey habits, their ash-eating and their pretending. They had not been called, and so they heard nothing. And because they heard nothing, they remained as they were.

But some heard it.

Not because they were better listeners. Not because they reached out to listen. Not because they chose to hear. The Voice simply spoke to them, and in the speaking, made them able to hear. That was the nature of the Voice—it did not offer a choice to those long-dead. It simply spoke, and the dead were made alive.

A man named Marcus heard the Voice while he stood in the grey marketplace, buying grey grain from a grey merchant. The Voice came to him like a command and a gift at once: "Live."

And Marcus gasped.

His lungs filled with air that was not grey but gold—light, burning, impossible. His eyes that had never seen opened and saw color. Red rushed into them. Gold. Blue. Violet. Green. The sky was not grey—it was endlessly, impossibly blue. The buildings around him blazed with warmth. The face of the grey merchant became a man with skin like honey and eyes like chestnuts. A woman beside him had hair like autumn fire.

Marcus wept. He fell to his knees and wept because he saw what he had been—dead, truly dead, moving through a grey existence without knowing it—and what he had become. Living. Alive. Awake.

Around him, the other dead did not understand. "Why do you weep?" they asked in their flat voices. "Are you broken?"

"I am alive," Marcus said, and his voice was different now, too—it had music in it, like wind across water.

They looked at him as if he had spoken nonsense.

A girl named Sophia heard the Voice while she worked at the grey loom, making grey cloth for grey garments. The Voice came to her like an awakening: "Come alive."

And Sophia gasped.

She heard, for the first time in her life, music. It came from everywhere at once—from the loom, from the earth, from the air itself. It was like every note she had never known existed, singing at once. Her hands moved across the loom and felt something impossible: texture, warmth, the grain of wood, the softness of thread. The cloth she was making was not grey—it was white as cloud, and beneath the white was dyed with purple like the night sky.

She brought the cloth to her face and it was soft, softer than anything she had ever known. She wept like Marcus had wept. She had been dead and did not know it. Now she was alive and could not deny it.

Around her, the dead said nothing. They had no framework for her weeping. They returned to their looms and made grey cloth. Sophia could not join them, even if she tried. The Voice had chosen her. The Voice had called her. The call was effectual—it did not merely invite her to life. It made her alive. And the alive cannot return to being dead.

✦

More heard the Voice. A mother in a grey home. A father at a grey desk. Children in grey schools. The Voice came to each of them, and to each one it said the same thing: "Live." "Awake." "See." "Hear." "Taste." "Come alive."

And every time the Voice spoke, the dead were made alive. The call never failed. When the Voice spoke to someone, that person became alive. There were no exceptions. The Voice spoke, and life followed, as surely as the sun follows the morning.

These awakened ones moved through the grey city, seeing it aflame with color, hearing it alive with music, tasting its sweetness. And the dead looked upon them and were troubled. Some thought them mad. Some thought them defective. Some thought them privileged, and demanded to know why they had been chosen for such madness while others remained sane, remained grey, remained dead.

Marcus and Sophia found each other in the city. When their eyes met, they both wept again, because they could see each other truly—not as grey forms moving through grey space, but as they actually were, alive, awake, burning with the reality of existing.

"When did this happen to you?" Sophia asked.

"At the marketplace," Marcus said. "The Voice spoke, and I had no choice. The choice was taken from me—but not to my loss. When the Voice spoke, I was chosen. And once chosen, I cannot return to being dead, no matter how hard I try. And I will not try, because I have tasted sweetness, and the sweetness is real."

They walked together through the city that was still grey to the grey, but alive with color to those with eyes to see. And as they walked, they encountered the others—all the ones the Voice had called. Some were angry at the dead, at their refusal to see, at their insistence that the dead city was the only reality. Others were grieved, knowing that loved ones remained sleeping, walking corpses who would never hear the Voice.

But Marcus and Sophia knew the truth that the awakened ones all came to understand: the dead could not choose to hear the Voice. The Voice did not work by invitation. It worked by resurrection. You could not wake yourself up. You could not see color if you had been born blind. You could not hear music if you had never been given ears to hear.

The Voice had to speak. The Voice had to choose. The Voice had to call. And when it did, the called could not refuse. Nor would they want to.

One evening, as Marcus stood at the city's edge, looking out toward the place from which the Voice came, a dead man approached him. The man was grey and hollow, like all the dead, but there was something in his eyes—a question, unformed and perhaps unform-able.

"Are you truly alive?" the dead man asked.

"Yes," Marcus said.

"Can I become alive?"

Marcus wanted to say yes. He wanted to reach out and pull the man into life, to wake him as he had been woken. But he could not. Marcus had not given himself his awakening. The Voice had. And Marcus could not speak with the Voice's power.

"You cannot wake yourself," Marcus said gently. "But the Voice can wake you. The Voice is speaking still. Listen."

The dead man listened, but he heard nothing. Around him, the grey city continued its grey routines. He returned to his grey home, to his grey bed. And Marcus stood at the edge of the city, his heart aching with the knowing that some would remain dead, not because they had to, but because the Voice had not chosen them—and without that choice, without that call, without that resurrection, they could not and would not believe that there was anything to wake up to.

But the Voice was still speaking. And would speak until its purpose was fulfilled, until every last one it had chosen was awakened, alive, and singing color into existence in a city that had been dead for so long it forgot how to dream of light.

And those who had heard the Voice, who could not refuse it, who had been chosen before time was time, who could never return to death—they would wait. They would grieve for the sleeping. But they would not doubt. They knew what they had been. They knew what they had become. And they knew that the Voice, having spoken them alive, would never let them slip back into the grave.

The Allegory Unveiled

Scripture teaches us through this story the reality of our condition, God's sovereignty in salvation, and the certainty of grace. Below, the symbols of the allegory point us to the biblical truths:

The Dead City

→ Spiritual Death

The city represents humanity in its natural state: dead in sin, unable to perceive spiritual reality, enslaved to routine and habit without knowing it. The dead do not know they are dead.

Ephesians 2:1–5

Grey & Tasteless

→ Total Depravity

The absence of color, music, taste, and beauty represents humanity's total inability to perceive or desire spiritual truth. Dead hearts cannot hunger for what they do not know exists.

Romans 3:10–11

The Voice from Outside

→ God's Grace

The Voice comes from beyond the city walls—outside and other than the dead. It is not generated from within the city; it is pure gift, pure calling, pure election from beyond.

Can a dead person choose to live? Our analogy page explores this profound question through the lens of a physician and an unwilling patient.

"The Voice comes from beyond the city walls—outside and other than the dead. It is pure gift, pure calling, pure election from beyond."

Ephesians 2:4–5

Not Everyone Hears

→ Unconditional Election

The Voice speaks to some, not others—not because some are better listeners, but because the Voice chooses whom to awaken. The call is unconditional, unearned, undeserved.

Romans 9:11–16

The Call to Live

→ Effectual Calling

When the Voice speaks, the dead are made alive. The call is not an offer that can be rejected. It is a command that resurrects. The effectiveness lies in the Voice, not the hearer.

"When the Voice speaks, the dead are made alive. The call is not an offer that can be rejected. It is a command that resurrects."

What does total depravity actually mean? Dive deeper into how dead hearts cannot generate saving faith on their own.

John 5:25, John 11:43–44

Awakening to Color & Music

→ Regeneration

The moment of awakening—seeing color, hearing music, tasting sweetness—represents being born again. Suddenly, spiritual reality becomes visible, audible, tangible. A new nature is given.

John 3:3–7, 2 Corinthians 5:17

Cannot Return to Death

→ Perseverance

Once awakened, the alive cannot choose to return to being dead. The Voice has secured them. They may struggle, but they cannot renounce what they have become.

Romans 8:28–30, John 10:27–29

Weeping at What They Were

→ Repentance & Wonder

The tears of the awakened flow from seeing the truth of their former condition and the grace of their present state. Repentance is not effort; it is clarity—seeing clearly what was and what now is.

Romans 5:8, 2 Corinthians 7:10

A Pastoral Word

→ If This Story Is About You

If you read this and wondered whether you're among the awakened or still sleeping—if you felt unsettled or defensive—know this: the fact that you're here, seeking, wrestling with these questions, is itself evidence that something has been stirring in you. The Spirit does not leave His own in darkness. If you sense you're being called, that sense is the call. Listen to it. Rest in the promise that the Voice that awakens will never let you go.

This is not bondage—it is the most complete freedom: to be alive.
