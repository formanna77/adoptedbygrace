Romans 9 — The Inescapable Text

The passage, in NIV, without footnotes. Followed by six observations no honest reader can dodge.

Designed to print on one US Letter or A4 page. Use your browser's Print function (Ctrl/Cmd + P). Take this to your small group.

Print This Page →

The Passage — Romans 9:10-23

10Not only that, but Rebekah's children were conceived at the same time by our father Isaac. 11Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad — in order that God's purpose in election might stand: 12not by works but by him who calls — she was told, "The older will serve the younger." 13Just as it is written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated."

14What then shall we say? Is God unjust? Not at all! 15For he says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." 16It does not, therefore, depend on human desire or effort, but on God's mercy.

17For Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." 18Therefore God has mercy on whom he wants to have mercy, and he hardens whom he wants to harden.

19One of you will say to me: "Then why does God still blame us? For who is able to resist his will?" 20But who are you, a human being, to talk back to God? "Shall what is formed say to the one who formed it, 'Why did you make me like this?'" 21Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for special purposes and some for common use?

22What if God, although choosing to show his wrath and make his power known, bore with great patience the objects of his wrath — prepared for destruction? 23What if he did this to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory…

Six Observations

1. Verses 11-13 make election pre-moral. The twins had done nothing — good or bad. God's choice fell before anything they would do. If the objection "but God saw who would choose Him" were right, Paul would not have written this verse this way. Salvation cannot be a response to foreseen faith here.

2. Verse 16 settles the source. "It does not depend on human desire or effort, but on God's mercy." Paul does not say mainly on mercy. He says not on human effort. The axis is God. Read rescued without a say for the catch.

3. Verse 18 mentions both sides — mercy and hardening. This is the verse the objectors dread. But Paul does not flinch; he doubles down in verse 19 by letting the objection run out loud, then — verse 20 — does not retract a syllable.

4. Verse 19 is the exact objection Arminianism raises: "who can resist his will?" If Paul were an Arminian, this is where he would correct the misunderstanding. He does not. He rebukes the question as impertinence (v. 20). That is the tell. See philosophy: resistance is proof.

5. Verses 20-21 describe the potter making some vessels "for common use" — the Greek literally says "for dishonor." If the wills of the vessels determined their use, the potter analogy collapses. Paul's picture only works if God shapes ends. Walk the Greek at word study: all/pas and word study: world/kosmos.

6. Verse 23 names the purpose: "to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory." You were prepared. Not predicted. Not foreseen. Prepared. You are a vessel made for mercy. That is the doctrine the crown jewel rests on. Begin with am I chosen?.

The Catch

If every word of this chapter is true, you do not carry your salvation. It carries you. "Jacob I loved" was spoken before Jacob existed. So was your name. You were chosen before you were broken. The God who decided before time to show His mercy in you — that God never gives up.
