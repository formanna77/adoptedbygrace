Theologians

Jonathan Edwards

The mind that set a nation on fire — and never stopped burning.

By Aaron Forman · January 1, 2026

In Brief

Jonathan Edwards (1703–1758) was America's greatest theologian and the intellectual architect of the Great Awakening. His Freedom of the Will demolished the Arminian concept of libertarian free will. His Religious Affections proved that genuine faith transforms the heart's desires, not merely the mind's opinions. His vision of God's sovereignty was so total, so beautiful, and so terrifying that it ignited a revival that reshaped a continent — and still burns wherever someone encounters the God he described.

The Horse in the Woods

Forget the angry-God sermon for a minute. Everybody knows that one. It has been chewed over for three centuries by people who mostly did not read it. Start somewhere else.

Start with the horse.

It is sometime in the 1720s. A young pastor — not yet famous, not yet infamous — rides his horse into the New England woods to be alone with God. He is slight, pale, bookish. He is also, by any reasonable measure, one of the sharpest minds alive in the Americas. The Puritans around him are not known for theatrical piety; they walk with a kind of reserved, buttoned-up holiness, their handkerchiefs folded, their emotions tucked under starched collars. Jonathan Edwards rides past the last farmhouse, ties his horse, walks into a clearing. And then — the way his own journal would later describe it — he is overtaken.

He does not plan it. He does not schedule it. The beauty of the triune God simply comes down on him in the clearing, in the ordinary light of an ordinary afternoon, and the most precise theologian in colonial America falls on his face in the grass and weeps with joy. For hours. At the glory.

Hold that picture, because it is the one you are not told about at the conference. The man who described hell with the calm of a surgeon cutting a tumor out of the world was the same man who had to be peeled off the forest floor by the dusk because he had lost track of time in the weight of God's loveliness. Everything else Edwards ever wrote — the Freedom of the Will, the Religious Affections, Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God — is downstream of that clearing. Without the horse, the sermons are just careful prose. With the horse, they are the carefully measured overflow of a man who had seen something.

The Quiet Fire

On July 8, 1741, a slight, soft-spoken pastor stood in the pulpit at Enfield, Connecticut, and read from a manuscript in a monotone voice. He did not shout. He did not pace. He held his notes close to his face because his eyesight was poor. By the time he finished, grown men were gripping the pews to keep from sliding to the floor, crying out to God for mercy. Some fainted. Others wept so loudly he could not continue.

The sermon was Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God. It remains the most famous sermon in American history. But here is what most people miss about it: the power was not in the fear. The power was in the precision. Edwards did not scream about hellfire. He described it — with the clinical exactness of a physicist explaining gravity. And the congregation did not weep because they were manipulated. They wept because they recognized it was true.

That combination — devastating precision married to spiritual fire — defined everything Edwards ever did. He was the rarest of creatures: a mind that could dismantle any philosophical argument in the Western tradition, housed in a heart so consumed by the beauty of God that he once rode his horse into the woods just to weep with joy at the sight of divine glory in the trees.

The Life

Born in 1703 in East Windsor, Connecticut, Edwards entered Yale at twelve and was reading Locke's Essay Concerning Human Understanding with more pleasure, he said, "than the most greedy miser finds when gathering up handfuls of silver and gold." By twenty-three, he succeeded his grandfather Solomon Stoddard as pastor of the Northampton, Massachusetts church — one of the largest congregations in New England.

What followed was extraordinary. Under Edwards' preaching, the Great Awakening erupted — a revival that swept the American colonies and transformed the religious landscape of a nation. Edwards did not manufacture it. He studied it, tested it against Scripture, and then documented it with a philosopher's rigor in works like A Faithful Narrative of the Surprising Work of God and The Distinguishing Marks of a Work of the Spirit of God. He was simultaneously the revival's greatest defender and its most careful critic — insisting that true spiritual awakening produces lasting fruit of the Spirit, not merely emotional excitement.

Then his own congregation fired him. In 1750, after a dispute over communion requirements — Edwards insisted that only those who showed credible evidence of conversion should take the Lord's Supper — the Northampton church voted him out. The greatest mind in American Christianity was dismissed by the people he had shepherded for twenty-three years. Edwards preached his farewell sermon with breathtaking grace, warning his congregation not to nurse resentment, and moved his family to the frontier mission at Stockbridge, where he ministered to the Mohican and Mohawk peoples.

It was there, in that intellectual exile, that he produced his greatest works.

The Intellectual Demolition

Edwards' Freedom of the Will (1754) is the most devastating philosophical work ever written against libertarian free will. The argument is deceptively simple: the will always chooses according to its strongest inclination. Always. You do what you most want to do in any given moment — and what you most want is determined by your nature, which you did not choose.

This is not coercion. It is something far more unsettling. You are free — horrifyingly free — to do exactly what your nature desires. And your nature, dead in sin, desires everything except the holiness of God. The sinner who rejects Christ is not being forced to reject Him. They are doing precisely what they want to do. That is the problem. That is the depravity. Not that the will is chained from the outside, but that it is rotten from the inside.

Edwards demolished every philosophical escape route. If the will determines itself, what determined it to determine itself? An infinite regress that collapses into absurdity. If the will is uncaused, then your choices are random — not free, but arbitrary. The only coherent account of human choice is that the will acts according to the nature of the agent. And the agent's nature is either renewed by grace or dead in sin. There is no neutral ground. There never was.

In Original Sin, he proved that human corruption is not an occasional failure but a constitutional reality — the heart's default orientation away from God, present from birth. In Religious Affections, he drew the most careful distinction in church history between genuine spiritual experience and its counterfeits, arguing that true grace transforms the "sense of the heart" — not merely the intellect's assent but the whole person's deepest desires. And in The End for Which God Created the World, he argued that God's chief purpose in creation is the communication of His own glory — that everything exists to display, and enable creatures to delight in, the infinite beauty of God.

The Beauty Underneath

But Edwards was not merely a demolition expert. Beneath every argument was a vision — and the vision was breathtaking. Edwards saw the universe as a ceaseless communication of divine beauty. The sun was God's glory made visible. Human love was a reflection of the Trinity's inner life. Music, mathematics, nature, reason — all were beams radiating from one infinite source. To know God was not to master propositions about Him but to be ravished by a beauty so overwhelming that every other pleasure became a shadow.

"The soul of a true Christian appeared like such a little white flower as we see in the spring of the year; low and humble on the ground, opening its bosom to receive the pleasant beams of the sun's glory; rejoicing, as it were, in a calm rapture; diffusing around a sweet fragrancy; standing peacefully and lovingly in the midst of other flowers."

JONATHAN EDWARDS, Personal Narrative

This is what the Arminian reading of Edwards always misses. They remember the spider dangling over the pit. They forget the white flower opening to the sun. That ratio says more about the reader than the preacher. Edwards' God was not merely sovereign — He was irresistibly beautiful. And that beauty was not incidental to the theology. It was the engine of it. Revival happened not when people were scared into faith but when the Holy Spirit opened their eyes to a glory so overwhelming that their old desires simply couldn't compete. The will was not overpowered.

It was outshone.

The Legacy That Would Not Die

In early 1758, Edwards was inaugurated as president of the College of New Jersey (now Princeton). Weeks later, he received a smallpox inoculation — a procedure he supported as an act of public health. The inoculation went wrong. On March 22, 1758, at fifty-four years old, Jonathan Edwards died. His last recorded words:

"Trust in God, and you need not fear."

He left behind a theological legacy so vast that scholars are still cataloguing it. His collected works fill twenty-six volumes. His students and intellectual descendants — Samuel Hopkins, Joseph Bellamy, the "New Divinity" movement — carried his vision forward into American evangelicalism. And his core conviction — that God's sovereignty is not a problem to be solved but a glory to be adored — remains the dividing line between theology that transforms and theology that merely informs.

Edwards matters today because he proved what most people would rather not face: that the question of free will is not abstract philosophy. It is the question that determines whether your salvation rests on the unshakable purpose of God or on the shifting sands of your own decision. He showed, with a rigor no one has surpassed in three centuries, that the human will always acts according to its nature. If your will always follows your nature — and you did not choose your nature — what exactly did you choose? Only sovereign grace can change a nature bent irreversibly toward self. And he showed it not to win an argument but because he had seen something so beautiful on the other side of that truth that he could not stop talking about it for the rest of his life.

Trust in God, and you need not fear.

Back to the Clearing

Return, one last time, to the horse and the clearing and the pale young man lying in the grass.

Notice what is missing from the picture. No pulpit. No crowd. No revival to point to. No students. No reputation. No sermon to preach later. Just a man and a God and a patch of sunlight, and an overwhelming, wordless, disarming recognition that the God he had been writing about in his notebooks was also the God who had been there the whole time, behind every leaf, inside every birdsong, underneath every line of every page of Scripture he had ever read. And the weight of that recognition was more than his body could quite carry, so his body did the only honest thing left to do. It let him weep.

That is the thing Edwards spent the rest of his life trying to get other people to see. Not an argument. Not a doctrine. Not a list of five points to be memorized and deployed. A person. A specific God, of specific beauty, whose glory is so saturating and so excessive and so real that once you have caught even a glimpse of Him you cannot go back to the shadows you used to live for. This is why his theology of the will is not a philosophy-class exercise. Your will follows your nature. Your nature follows what it finds beautiful. And until the Spirit of God opens your eyes to the specific beauty Edwards saw in that clearing, every other beauty will win. Every single time.

Which is why the last sentence he spoke matters more than the most famous one he ever wrote. Trust in God, and you need not fear. Not: decide harder for God. Not: work up the feelings. Not: compete with your doubts. Trust. And the trust itself — the ability to lean on Him at all — is something He gives you, the same way He gave a young pastor in a New England clearing the eyes to see what had been there all along.

If your life has felt, for a long time, like a careful argument for a God you cannot quite feel — if the prose on the page has always been more real to you than the Person behind it — this is the quiet invitation underneath the whole arc of Edwards' life. You cannot manufacture the clearing. You cannot schedule the moment in the woods. But the God who found a young Puritan in the grass is the same God who is already finding you, page by page, in the small unguarded moments between the arguments, and He is more patient than your inability to see Him. Keep reading. Keep praying. Keep getting on the horse.

He has been waiting in the clearing the whole time.
