Systematic Theology

The Glory of Christ

He is not a good teacher who offers advice. He is God in flesh who accomplishes salvation — and the difference changes everything.

The Truth: If Christ is not fully God, He cannot save. If He is not fully man, He cannot represent us. If His death is not a definite, purposeful atonement for specific people, then the cross accomplished nothing — it merely created an opportunity that might fail. But Scripture says Christ laid down His life for the sheep, purchased His bride with His blood, and secured eternal redemption. His atonement does not make salvation possible. It makes salvation certain — for everyone the Father gave Him.

If Jesus is merely a good teacher, you can take His advice or leave it. If He is merely a moral example, you can admire Him from a safe distance. If He is merely a prophet, you can file Him alongside the others and move on with your life. But if He is who Colossians says He is — the One in whom all things hold together, the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation — then He is not a figure in your story. You are a figure in His. And everything you think you know about salvation, about choice, about where your faith came from, must be re-examined in light of who is actually sitting on the throne of the universe.

"The Son is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation. For in him all things were created: things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or powers or rulers or authorities; all things have been created through him and for him. He is before all things, and in him all things hold together."

COLOSSIANS 1:15–17

Who He Is

Christ is not a created being promoted to divine rank. He is God the Son — eternally God, one in being with the Father, without beginning or end. "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God" (John 1:1). He did not come into being. He was always there. Everything that exists was made through Him and for Him, and without Him nothing was made that has been made.

And yet — this is the staggering part — the Word became flesh. The infinite became finite. The Creator entered His own creation as a baby who needed milk and a mother's arms. He hungered (Matthew 4:2), wept (John 11:35), bled, and died. Not in appearance. In genuine, bone-deep, nerve-ending human reality.

Why does this matter? Because salvation depends on it.

Only God has the power to bear the infinite weight of sin against an infinite God. A creature — no matter how exalted — cannot pay a debt that large. If Christ is not God, the cross is a tragedy, not a triumph. But only a human can represent humans. Our kinsman-redeemer had to be one of us — flesh of our flesh, bone of our bone — to stand in our place and die our death. If Christ is not man, His suffering is irrelevant to ours.

He must be one of us to represent us and one with God to save us. The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) said it best: two natures, one person — without confusion, without separation. The natures are not blended into some divine-human hybrid. They are united in a single person who is fully God and fully man, forever. This is the mystery at the center of the universe, and every thread of redemption depends on it.

What He Did

Christ did not come to make you a better person. He came to take your place on a cross you deserved and to bear a wrath you could never survive.

"Surely he took up our pain and bore our suffering, yet we considered him punished by God, stricken by him, and afflicted. But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was on him, and by his wounds we are healed."

ISAIAH 53:4–5

This is penal substitutionary atonement — the truth that Christ died in the place of sinners, bearing the penalty their sins deserved, satisfying the justice of God, and securing their pardon. God is not a grandfather who winks at rebellion. He is perfectly just, and justice demands payment. Christ paid it. His blood satisfies God's wrath — propitiation — and turns condemnation into favor for everyone in Christ.

But His work is not only passive — suffering what we deserved. It is also active — living the perfect obedience we could never achieve. Christ kept every command, fulfilled every requirement of the law, and His spotless righteousness is credited to our account. We stand before God not merely pardoned but righteous. Not merely forgiven but clothed in the perfection of Christ Himself. The great exchange: our sin for His righteousness (2 Corinthians 5:21).

For Whom He Did It

Here is where Christology collides with the truth most Christians have never been forced to face. Christ did not die to create a vague, general possibility of salvation that depends on human decision for its success. He died to accomplish the salvation of specific people — those the Father gave Him before the foundation of the world.

"I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" (John 10:11). Not for humanity in general. For the sheep. And who are the sheep? The ones the Father gave Him. The ones who hear His voice. The ones who follow — not because they decided to, but because they were drawn.

John Owen posed the question that settles this forever. Christ died either for all the sins of all people, or for all the sins of some people, or for some sins of all people. If He died for all sins of all people, everyone is saved — but Scripture says they are not. If He died for some sins of all people, no one is fully pardoned. The only option left: Christ died for all the sins of some people — those whom the Father chose — and every one of them will certainly be saved.

This is what Scripture calls definite atonement. "Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her" (Ephesians 5:25). Not for a hypothetical crowd that might or might not respond. For His bride. For His church. For the people whose names were written in the Lamb's book of life before a single star burned.

The Arminian alternative — that Christ died equally for all, hoping some would accept — means the atonement fails for every person who rejects it. It means Christ's blood was shed in vain for millions. It means the cross was not a victory but a gamble. And it means the decisive factor in your salvation is not the Son's sacrifice but your decision. Which is, at its root, a work.

Why This Changes Everything

If Christ's atonement is definite — if He died specifically for you, personally, by name, before you existed — then your salvation was never a coin flip. It was never hanging in the balance, waiting for you to tip the scales with your choice. The scales were settled at Calvary. The blood was sufficient. The verdict was pronounced. And every link in the chain that followed — your calling, your regeneration, the faith you thought was yours, your justification, your ultimate glorification — flows from that one definite, purposeful, accomplished act on the cross.

He is not a teacher offering suggestions. He is God in flesh, accomplishing what no human being could accomplish, for people who could never have accomplished it themselves.

"He entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, thus obtaining eternal redemption."

HEBREWS 9:12

Obtaining. Not offering. Not making available. Obtaining. Eternal redemption — secured, purchased, finished. For you. Before you drew breath. By a Savior who is not in the habit of letting go.
