History

The Synod of Dort: Five Points Forged in Fire

Seven months. One hundred and fifty-four sessions. Eighty-four theologians from eight nations. One hundred and eighty-seven pages of verdict. And a question your church is still trying to answer.

On the morning of November 13, 1618, the bells of Dordrecht rang over a city that had the look of a town about to host a war. Eighty-four theologians had traveled by carriage, by boat, across borders and seas, summoned by the States General of the Netherlands to sit as a court. Delegates came from Switzerland, from the Palatinate, from Hesse, from Bremen, from Geneva, from England. They came in black robes and Geneva bands, with Hebrew lexicons under their arms and Greek New Testaments in their satchels. They were not there to have a conversation. They were there to render a verdict.

The defendant was not a man. It was a doctrine. And the doctrine was the oldest lie in the church wearing a new Dutch suit.

The Man Who Lit the Fuse

Jacobus Arminius had died nine years earlier, in 1609, quietly and in bed. But his followers — fourteen of them, led by the scholar Simon Episcopius — had done what Arminius himself had been too cautious to do publicly. In 1610, they drafted a document called the Remonstrance: a five-point protest against the Reformed confessions of the Dutch churches. They did not call it a new theology. They called it a clarification. That is always how the lie enters — not as revolt, but as refinement. Arminius had been a student of Theodore Beza in Geneva, trained in the theology of John Calvin, and had spent his career pastoring in Amsterdam. On paper he was Reformed. In print, by the end of his life, he was something else.

The Remonstrance made five claims. God's election, it said, was based on foreseen faith. Christ died for all men without exception. Fallen humans cannot do anything truly good without prevenient grace — but that grace can be resisted. And true believers, it hinted with ominous ambiguity, can fall away. Strip the five points of their Latin and their footnotes and what you have is the oldest argument in Christendom: salvation is a partnership. God does His part. You do yours.

The Dutch Reformed churches read it and felt the floor tilt under them. They recognized the voice. They had heard it before — in the mouth of Pelagius, in the decrees condemned at the Council of Orange in 529, in the semi-Pelagian drift that the medieval church had baptized as orthodoxy. The argument was not new. It was the heresy that would not die, risen again in a Dutch cloak.

The Politics Beneath the Theology

You cannot understand Dort without understanding that the controversy nearly tore the Dutch Republic in half. The Remonstrants had a powerful patron: Johan van Oldenbarnevelt, the grand pensionary of Holland, the political architect of Dutch independence from Spain. On the other side was Prince Maurice of Orange, the stadtholder, a military genius and a convinced Calvinist. By 1617 the two men were barely speaking. By 1618, Maurice had arrested Oldenbarnevelt on charges of treason. By May of 1619, in the shadow of the Synod, Oldenbarnevelt would be beheaded in The Hague, a seventy-one-year-old man walking to the scaffold with his Bible in his hand.

"Men, believe not that I am a traitor to the country. I have always acted uprightly and piously, as a good patriot, and as such I shall die."

OLDENBARNEVELT, FROM THE SCAFFOLD, MAY 13, 1619

That is the backdrop you have to see. This was not a seminar. This was a nation tearing itself open over whether salvation belonged to God alone or was a cooperative project between God and man. The stakes were pulpits, parishes, careers, the national church, and in one case a man's head. The Reformed world understood what the modern evangelical church has forgotten: this question is not small. When you get it wrong, the gospel itself begins to bleed out, and no amount of pastoral warmth can close the wound.

The Trial Begins

The Remonstrants arrived at Dort expecting a debate between equals. They were told, instead, that they were defendants. Episcopius, their leader, rose on the sixth day and delivered a two-hour opening oration, refusing to submit to the Synod as a judge and demanding instead to argue as a peer. He was ruled out of order. He was told to submit his views in writing. When he and his colleagues refused to cooperate on the Synod's terms, they were dismissed on January 14, 1619, and the Synod continued without them. Their views were heard, examined, and adjudicated in absentia from their own published writings — a move the Remonstrants called tyranny and which their critics called mercy, given how plainly their own books condemned them.

For the next four months, the Synod worked through the Remonstrance point by point. They did not rush. They parsed the Greek. They compared translations. They argued over single Latin words. They prayed. They wept. They produced draft after draft of counter-canons, tearing them apart in committee and stitching them back together. This was not a rubber stamp. This was the most careful theological deliberation the Reformed world had ever seen — and what emerged was not a triumphalist war cry but a patient, pastoral, precise document designed to close every escape hatch the lie had ever used.

The Five Canons

On May 6, 1619, the Synod published its verdict: five canons, each rejecting a Remonstrant point and affirming the Reformed position. You know them now by the mnemonic TULIP, though that English acronym would not appear until the twentieth century. The canons themselves were more beautiful, and more devastating, than the acronym. They were not abstract propositions. They were a love letter written in the handwriting of doctors who had watched the lie rob their sheep and were determined to snatch them back.

The First Canon answered the question of election. The Remonstrants had said God elects based on foreseen faith. The Synod answered: God elects based on nothing in us at all. Faith itself is a gift of election, not its cause. Election is eternal, unconditional, immutable, and flows from the sheer good pleasure of God's will. If faith were the cause of election, the canons explained, then faith would be the meritorious ground of salvation — and Paul's whole argument in Romans 9 would collapse into nonsense.

"Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad — in order that God's purpose in election might stand: not by works but by him who calls — she was told, 'The older will serve the younger.'"

ROMANS 9:11-12

The Second Canon answered the atonement. The Remonstrants had said Christ died for all men without exception, making salvation possible for everyone. The Synod answered: Christ's death was infinite in value and sufficient for the whole world, but its saving efficacy belongs only to the elect. He did not die to make salvation theoretical. He died to make it certain. He did not throw a ring at every person hoping one might catch it. He laid down His life for His sheep and His sheep heard His voice. The doctrine of definite atonement was born here, not as a cold calculation but as the most romantic truth in Scripture: the Lamb knew every name of those for whom He bled.

The Third and Fourth Canons, combined into one treatise, answered the questions of depravity and grace. The Remonstrants had said fallen man retains a damaged but operational free will that can cooperate with grace when offered. The Synod answered with a doctrine so total it leaves no room for negotiation: fallen man is spiritually dead in sin, with a will so captive to rebellion that left to itself it will never, can never, and does not want to come to Christ. Grace does not assist the sinner — it raises the sinner. It does not woo the corpse — it commands Lazarus out of the tomb. This is what the Reformed tradition calls regeneration, and it is the beating heart of every conversion story ever told, whether the convert knew it at the time or not.

"As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you followed the ways of this world and of the ruler of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient."

EPHESIANS 2:1-2

The Fifth Canon answered perseverance. The Remonstrants had hinted that true believers could fall away and be lost. The Synod answered with the unbroken chain of Romans 8: what God begins, He finishes. Those whom He foreknew, He predestined; those He predestined, He called; those He called, He justified; those He justified, He glorified. Not one link fails. The elect are not preserved because of their grip on Christ. They are preserved because of His grip on them. This is the canon that has saved more Christians from despair than all the others combined, because it takes the load-bearing weight of salvation off the believer's trembling shoulders and places it where it always belonged: on the strong shoulders of the Lamb.

The Aftermath

When the Synod closed on May 29, 1619 — after one hundred and fifty-four sessions, after seven months of continuous debate, after the execution of Oldenbarnevelt and the exile of more than two hundred Remonstrant pastors — the Reformed world exhaled. The canons had done what they were meant to do. They had not invented a new theology. They had named the old lie and posted its obituary in print. The Canons of Dort, together with the Belgic Confession and the Heidelberg Catechism, became the Three Forms of Unity — the confessional backbone of the Dutch Reformed churches and, through them, of the global Reformed tradition that would later shape Presbyterianism in Scotland, Puritanism in England, the Great Awakening in America, and the missionary explosion of the nineteenth century.

But the Remonstrants did not vanish. They went underground, then resurfaced in Utrecht in 1630 as a tolerated minority. They produced theologians who would influence John Wesley a century later and, through Wesley, would spread Arminian theology throughout English-speaking Methodism and, eventually, into the DNA of most of the American evangelical church. The trial at Dort was won. The war was not. The lie is still walking around — wearing different clothes, speaking different languages, adopted by pastors who have no idea who Arminius was and would be horrified to hear themselves called Remonstrants. But the five points of the 1610 Remonstrance are preached, almost word for word, from thousands of American pulpits every Sunday morning.

Why This Matters Now

Here is what the modern evangelical has to reckon with. If you believe that God chose you because He foresaw that you would choose Him — Dort has already ruled on your theology, and it lost. If you believe that Christ died to make salvation possible for everyone but effective only for those who accept — Dort has already ruled, and you are on the wrong side of the verdict. If you believe that your will was weakened but not dead, and that grace is an assist rather than a resurrection — Dort has already ruled, and the Synod's response to your position is in print, in eight languages, in any seminary library on earth.

The Reformed establishment has, for the most part, decided to call this "a secondary issue." They say it with pastoral tenderness and a shrug. But the theologians who sat at Dort would have found that tenderness monstrous. They did not believe, and they did not say, that Arminianism was a matter of private preference between brothers who were all going to heaven. They believed, with their whole hearts, that the gospel itself was under attack — and that calling the attack a preference was a betrayal of the sheep they had been appointed to protect. Why does the flesh resist this verdict so fiercely? Because the lie is comfortable, and the truth requires surrender. Because the lie lets you stay on your throne with Jesus as your advisor, and the truth demands you get down and let Him be King.

The question for you, reader, is not academic. It is personal. In the drawer of your heart there is a version of your testimony, and in that version you are the one who did the choosing. You closed the deal. You walked the aisle. You prayed the prayer. And your salvation hinges, even secretly, on the fact that you made a decision. If that is how you see it, then the Synod of Dort is not ancient history. It is a mirror. And the face looking back at you from that mirror is the face of the defendant the Synod dismissed on January 14, 1619.

The Question No One Asks

If the Reformed church settled this in 1619, with eighty-four theologians from eight nations, after seven months of careful study and prayer — why are we having the same fight four hundred years later? Why does your pastor, if pressed, probably agree with the Remonstrants rather than with Dort? Why has a doctrine that was once worth a beheading become a doctrine that most evangelicals do not know the name of? The honest answer is that the flesh does not tire. The lie that God's sovereignty can be softened by a contribution from me is not a bad theological idea that needs better arguments. It is a love the natural heart has for itself — and the natural heart will dress it in any clothing, speak it in any language, and smuggle it into any pulpit that will let it through.

That is why the foreknowledge heresy keeps coming back. Not because the arguments for it are compelling. But because the human heart needs it to be true. To finally let go of it requires more than a better exegetical paper. It requires the same thing it has always required: the Holy Spirit prying the lie out of a clenched fist and replacing it with the gift of a gaze — the stunned, tearful realization that He chose you before the foundation of the world, and that nothing you did or failed to do had anything to do with why.

The Bridge

The men who sat at Dort were not propositional machines. Read the Canons of Dort out loud and you can hear them weeping between the paragraphs. They wrote what they wrote because they had seen grace rescue them from the same lie they were now condemning. The same God who cornered Augustine in the garden at Milan, the same God who cornered Luther in the tower at Wittenberg, was the God who cornered the eighty-four theologians at Dort — and through them, the God who corners the reader who has walked this far down the page. The argument of Romans 9 is not a prison. It is a vault, and inside the vault is a Father who has loved you with an everlasting love and has been planning your rescue since before there were stars.

If that wounds you, it is because the lie has grown into your ribs, and anything that pulls it out is going to hurt. But the wound is the mercy. The defendant at Dort walked away angry. The elect at Dort walked away free. The canons were not written to crush the sheep. They were written to remind the sheep that the Shepherd had never let go. Four hundred years later, He still hasn't. He never will.
