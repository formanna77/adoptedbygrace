What If Suffering Is a Gift?

April 4, 2026

There is a verse tucked inside Paul's letter to the Philippians that most people read too quickly. It contains a single Greek word that, once you see it, rearranges everything you thought you knew about your hardest season.

Here it is:

"For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him."
— Philippians 1:29

The word is echaristhē (ἐχαρίσθη). It comes from the root charis — grace. Paul doesn't say suffering was allowed. He doesn't say it was permitted. He says it was graced to you. Gifted. Bestowed as a favor.

But here is the detail that stops every argument cold: Paul uses the same verb, in the same grammatical construction, for both things in the sentence. Believing and suffering. The faith you have? Granted. The suffering you endure? Granted — by the same hand, through the same grace, with the same verb.

This is not a throwaway parallelism. Paul is making an argument: if you accept that your faith is a gift from God — and every Christian must, because Ephesians 2:8-9 leaves no room to claim otherwise — then you must also accept that your suffering arrived through the same sovereign kindness. Same verb. Same Giver. Same grace.

Which means the season that felt like abandonment was actually appointment. The pain you thought proved God had forgotten you was the very thing He chose to give you — echaristhē — the way a king bestows an honor on a trusted servant.

This does not make the pain painless. Paul was writing from prison when he penned this. He knew exactly what suffering cost. But he also knew something the sufferer desperately needs to hear: the God who gave you the faith to believe is the same God who gave you the trial that is deepening it.

Your faith did not come from you. Your suffering did not come from nowhere. Both arrived from the same open hand. And if grace delivered the first, then grace — not cruelty, not chaos, not cosmic indifference — delivered the second.

One verb. Two gifts. The same grace.
