All Things Made New

01 · The Text

The Scripture of Glory

The doctrine of eschatology is rooted in the Word of God. Here we begin with the apocalyptic promise of the new creation and the apostolic hope that sustains the Church.

Revelation 21:1-5 ESV
"Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and the sea was no more. And I saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, 'Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God. He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away.' And he who was seated on the throne said, 'Behold, I am making all things new.'"
Romans 8:18-25 ESV
"For I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us. For the creation waits with eager longing for the revealing of the sons of God... We know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth until now. And not only the creation, but we ourselves, who have the firstfruits of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for adoption as sons, the redemption of our bodies."
02 · Definition

The Study of Last Things

Eschatology means the study of the eschatos — the last things. But for the Reformed Christian, this is not speculation or date-setting.

Eschatology: A Reformed Definition

Eschatology is the doctrine of God's glorious consummation of His eternal decree. Everything that began in divine election reaches its culmination in glorification. It is not frantic speculation about end-times timelines, but confident hope in the certain triumph of Christ's kingdom. The golden chain of Romans 8:29-30 finds its completion here: "those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified."

The terminology reminds us that eschatology concerns the last things — death, the intermediate state, the return of Christ, the resurrection of the body, the final judgment, and the new creation. But more profoundly, it is the unveiling of the ultimate purpose of God. Redemption is not an afterthought or a divine plan B. It is the centerpiece of cosmic history.

For the believer, eschatology is the blessed hope. It is the anchor of the soul in a world of chaos, suffering, and apparent meaninglessness. It reminds us that history is not spinning in circles — it is moving toward a glorious consummation where righteousness dwells, where God is glorified, and where His people dwell in His presence forever.

The eschaton is not mythology. It is the historical fulfillment of the covenant promises. When Jesus said, "It is finished," He was speaking both of the completed work of redemption and of the certain future glory that awaits His people. The resurrection of Christ from the dead is the historical guarantee that all His promises will be fulfilled.

03 · Death

The Last Enemy

Death is the final consequence of sin and the gateway through which all humanity must pass. Yet for the Christian, death has been conquered.

Death as the Consequence of Sin: Romans 6:23 declares, "The wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord." Death entered the world through Adam's transgression (Romans 5:12) and now reigns over all humanity (Romans 5:14). God's law was clear: "in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die" (Genesis 2:17).
Physical Death: Physical death is the separation of the soul from the body. The body returns to dust (Genesis 3:19), but the spirit is eternal. For the believer, death is not annihilation but transition — a doorway from earthly pilgrimage to heavenly glory.
Spiritual Death: More terrible than physical death is spiritual death — separation from God. This is the second death (Revelation 20:14), the lake of fire reserved for those who refuse the gospel. It is eternal alienation from the source of all life, beauty, goodness, and joy.
Death Conquered for the Believer: But for the Christian, Christ has conquered death. Paul cries out in triumph: "Death is swallowed up in victory. O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?" (1 Corinthians 15:54-55). The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law, but thanks be to God who gives us the victory through Jesus Christ!
To Die Is Gain: For Paul, death held no terror. "For me to live is Christ, and to die is gain" (Philippians 1:21). Death is the Christian's release from the body of sin and the portal to the beatific vision — to see Christ face to face and dwell in His presence forever.
Death as Gateway to Glory: The Christian does not fear death because death is not the end — it is a transition. It is the appointed time when the soul is absent from the body but present with the Lord (2 Corinthians 5:8). For the elect, death is the gateway through which we pass to enter into the inheritance prepared for us.
04 · The Intermediate State

Between Death and Resurrection

What happens to the Christian between physical death and the resurrection of the body? Scripture speaks clearly of consciousness and blessing for the believer.

Presence with the Lord

Paul writes: "We are of good courage, and we would rather be away from the body and at home with the Lord" (2 Corinthians 5:8). The intermediate state is not soul sleep — the dead in Christ are awake, conscious, and in the presence of their Savior.

Against Soul Sleep: Some traditions have taught that the soul sleeps from death until the resurrection. But Scripture contradicts this. The believers in the intermediate state are described as conscious and blessed, not unconscious. Jesus told the thief on the cross, "Today you will be with me in paradise" (Luke 23:43) — a promise of immediate conscious presence with Christ, not slumber.
The Rich Man and Lazarus: In Luke 16:19-31, Jesus describes a parable in which the rich man and Lazarus, both dead, are conscious and aware. Lazarus is comforted in Abraham's bosom; the rich man is in torment. They remember their earthly lives; they speak and perceive. This is not sleep.
Paradise for the Believer: For the Christian who departs this life, the intermediate state is paradise — presence with Christ. This is a state of joy and rest, though not the final consummation. We await the resurrection of the body and the final state in the new creation.
Conscious Torment for the Unbeliever: For those who reject Christ, the intermediate state is described as conscious torment and darkness. The parable of the rich man and Lazarus reveals a place of anguish and awareness. This intermediate torment foreshadows the final judgment.
Not the Final State: The intermediate state is crucial to understand: it is not the final state. Bodily resurrection is still to come. The eternal state of the redeemed is not disembodied immortality but embodied glory — glorified, physical resurrection like Christ's own (1 Corinthians 15:49).
05 · The Return of Christ

The Blessed Hope

The personal, visible, bodily return of Christ is the anchor of Christian hope and the promise that sustains believers through suffering.

The Second Advent

When Christ ascended into heaven, the apostles were given a promise: "Men of Galilee, why do you stand looking into heaven? This Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will come in the same way as you saw him go into heaven" (Acts 1:11).

Personal and Visible: Christ will return personally — not through His Spirit only, but in bodily form. "Every eye will see him, even those who pierced him" (Revelation 1:7). This is not a private revelation or a spiritual experience only — it is a cosmic, visible, historical event.
Bodily Return: Christ's return will be physical. He promises His disciples: "I will come again and will take you to myself, that where I am you may be also" (John 14:3). The same Jesus who rose from the dead, who ate fish to prove He was not a ghost, who could be touched — this Jesus returns.
The Certainty: Christ's return is not hypothetical or conditional. He said it as firmly as any promise could be made: "I will come again." The resurrection of Christ validates every word He spoke. His resurrection is the historical proof that He is Lord and that His word is trustworthy.
The Timing: Jesus Himself declared: "Concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor the Son, but the Father only" (Matthew 24:36). Date-setting is forbidden. Scoffers who say, "Where is the promise of his coming?" fail to understand that "with the Lord one day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years like one day" (2 Peter 3:8).
The Manner: Christ's return will be sudden and unexpected. "As a thief in the night" (1 Thessalonians 5:2-3). Those who are not watching and ready will be caught off guard. The world will be going about its business — eating, drinking, marrying — when suddenly the heavens will open and the King returns in glory.
Christ as King and Judge: When Christ returns, He comes not as a suffering servant but as a glorious King. He comes as the Judge of all the earth. "The Father judges no one, but has given all judgment to the Son" (John 5:22). Every knee will bow; every tongue will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord (Philippians 2:10-11).
06 · The Resurrection of the Body

Imperishable, Glorious, and Spiritual

1 Corinthians 15 is the great chapter of resurrection. Here Paul unfolds the mystery and the glory of the resurrection body.

The Resurrection Body: Paul describes the resurrection body with four qualities: it is imperishable, glorious, powerful, and spiritual (1 Corinthians 15:42-44). It is raised in dishonor but clothed with glory. It is sown in weakness but raised in power. It is a natural body sown, but a spiritual body raised.
Christ as Firstfruits: "Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep" (1 Corinthians 15:20). The resurrection of Jesus is not isolated — it is the pattern and guarantee of our resurrection. As Christ rose, so shall we. His resurrection is the prototype; ours is the fulfillment of the same power.
The Order of Resurrection: "Christ the firstfruits, then at his coming those who belong to Christ" (1 Corinthians 15:23). There is an order to the resurrection. Christ has already risen. At His return, His people will be raised. And then comes the end — the fulfillment of all things.
The Resurrection of All: Both the just and the unjust will be raised. "Do not marvel at this, for an hour is coming when all who are in the tombs will hear his voice and come out, those who have done good to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil to the resurrection of judgment" (John 5:28-29). The resurrection is universal, but its outcome is bifurcated — life for the righteous, judgment for the wicked.
Against Gnosticism: The Christian faith affirms the goodness of the body. Against gnosticism, which despises matter and flesh, Christianity proclaims that the body matters to God. He created it, redeemed it in Christ, and will resurrect it in glory. Disembodied immortality is not the Christian hope — embodied resurrection is.
Continuity and Transformation: The resurrection body is both continuous with and transformed from our earthly body. It is we who are raised — the same person, the same identity. Yet it is glorified, perfected, freed from the corruption of sin. We will be recognizable (the disciples recognized the risen Jesus), yet transformed. We shall be like Christ — for we shall see Him as He is (1 John 3:2).
07 · The Final Judgment

Before the Throne of Glory

All will stand before the judgment seat of Christ. This is both the most fearful and the most joyful doctrine in Scripture.

The Judgment of All

"We must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ, so that each one may receive what is due for what he has done in the body, whether good or evil" (2 Corinthians 5:10). And John sees the throne: "I saw the dead, great and small, standing before the throne, and books were opened" (Revelation 20:12).

Christ the Judge: "The Father judges no one, but has given all judgment to the Son" (John 5:22). Christ is the Judge. This is not a contradiction of His love — it is the very expression of it. The Judge is He who died for the world. The Judge is He who offers mercy to all who believe. The Judge is righteous.
The Standard: "God's law is perfect" and becomes the measure. The standard is not relative morality or cultural opinion — it is God's revealed will. The gospel is also a standard; Christ Himself is the measure. "Whoever believes in the Son has eternal life; whoever does not obey the Son shall not see life" (John 3:36).
Vindication for Believers: For the Christian, the final judgment is not condemnation — it is vindication. "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1). The redeemed will stand before the Judge knowing that their debt has been paid, their sins forgiven, their righteousness is Christ's. The judgment reveals the reality already secured by faith.
Just Condemnation for Unbelievers: For those who have rejected Christ, the judgment is condemnation according to their works. This is not arbitrary — it is just. Those who refuse the gospel have condemned themselves. "This is the judgment: the light has come into the world, and people loved the darkness rather than the light" (John 3:19).
The Justice of Eternal Punishment: Many object that eternal punishment seems disproportionate. But the punishment is not disproportionate if the crime is understood correctly. Sin against an infinite God deserves infinite consequence. God's holiness is infinite, His law is perfect, His justice is exact. To rebel against an infinitely holy God warrants an infinite response.
Against Universalism: Some teach that all will eventually be saved — that God's love is too great to permit eternal separation. But Jesus taught clearly about a real and eternal hell. "These will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life" (Matthew 25:46). Universalism contradicts Christ's teaching and undermines the seriousness of the gospel. If all are saved, why did Christ die?
08 · The New Creation

Eternity in All Its Glory

This is the consummation toward which all of redemptive history moves. This is the goal of the ordo salutis — glorification realized in the new heavens and new earth.

Revelation 21:1-5 ESV
"Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and the sea was no more. And I saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, 'Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God. He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away.' And he who was seated on the throne said, 'Behold, I am making all things new.'"
The New Heavens and New Earth: "Behold, I am making all things new." This is not the annihilation of creation but its renewal and perfection. The present heavens and earth will pass away, but God will create a new heavens and new earth — a re-creation, purified and perfected by fire, freed from the curse of sin.
The Descent of the Holy City: The new Jerusalem comes down from heaven — not up into heaven, but down to dwell with humanity on the renewed earth. This is profoundly material, physical, real. God does not rescue us from creation; He restores creation. We do not ascend to an immaterial heaven; the kingdom comes to earth.
God Dwelling with His People: "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man." This is the ultimate purpose of redemption. From Genesis to Revelation, God's goal is fellowship with His people. In the new creation, there is no barrier, no veil, no distance. "They will see his face" (Revelation 22:4). Face-to-face communion with God — this is the beatific vision.
The End of Suffering: "He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away." All the sufferings of this present age — disease, grief, loss, pain — all abolished. The curse is lifted.
The Curse Removed: "No longer will there be anything accursed" (Revelation 22:3). The curse that fell on creation in Genesis 3 is finally reversed. The thorns and thistles, the pain of childbirth, the sweat of the brow, death itself — all removed. Creation is restored to its intended glory.
The Glorification of the Saints: This is the end of the ordo salutis — the golden chain of Romans 8:29-30 reaches its completion: "those whom he justified he also glorified." The redeemed are made perfect, incapable of sin, fully transformed into the image of Christ, dwelling in the immediate presence of God forever.
The Certainty of Hope: Note the tense in Romans 8:30: glorified. It is written in the past tense, though it is future to us. From God's perspective, the eternal state is so certain that He speaks of it as already accomplished. Our hope is not speculative — it is grounded in the character of God and the promise of Christ.
09 · Biblical Foundation

The Testimony of Scripture

Eschatology is not a novelty of Christian theology but the constant theme of Scripture from beginning to end.

Old Testament Promises

The Covenant Promise: God's covenant with Abraham was not merely temporal — it promised an eternal seed and an eternal inheritance. "Look toward heaven and number the stars, if you are able to number them... so shall your offspring be" (Genesis 15:5). This points to the multiplication of God's people in the new creation.
Isaiah's Vision: The prophet Isaiah beheld the throne of God and heard the seraphim singing, "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory" (Isaiah 6:3). This eschatological vision points to the universal recognition of God's glory in the final age.
The Latter Days: Throughout the prophets, references to "the latter days," "the day of the Lord," and "the age to come" point to God's future kingdom. "The mountain of the house of the Lord shall be established as the highest of the mountains... and all the nations shall flow to it" (Isaiah 2:2).

The Gospels

The Kingdom Already and Not Yet: Jesus announced that the kingdom of God had come (Matthew 3:2), yet He also taught His disciples to pray, "Your kingdom come" (Matthew 6:10). The kingdom is present in Christ's reign but awaits its consummation. This "already-not yet" tension runs through all eschatology.
The Resurrection of Jesus: Christ's resurrection is the central fact of eschatology. "I am the living one. I died, and behold I am alive forevermore" (Revelation 1:18). The resurrection validates the entire eschatological hope. It is the historical proof of the power of God and the guarantee of our future resurrection.

The Epistles

Romans 8:28-30: The golden chain of salvation culminates in glorification. "We know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose... And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified." The final link is not hypothetical — it is certain.
1 Corinthians 15: The entire chapter is devoted to resurrection. Paul's argument is that Christ has risen, and therefore we shall rise. The resurrection is not a marginal doctrine but central to the gospel. "If Christ has not been raised, our faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17).
1 Thessalonians 4:13-18: Paul comforts the Thessalonians about those who have died in Christ: "For the Lord himself will descend from heaven with a cry of command, with the voice of an archangel, and with the sound of the trumpet of God. And the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive, who are left, will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air; and so we will always be with the Lord." This is the blessed hope that sustains the faithful.

Revelation

The Apocalypse Unfolds: Revelation is the most explicitly eschatological book of Scripture. It reveals the future — not with photographic precision in every detail, but with unmistakable clarity about the victory of Christ, the triumph of God's kingdom, and the judgment of the wicked. It is a book meant to comfort the persecuted and assure them that Christ will return and judge the nations.
10 · Historical Development

The Church's Hope Through the Ages

The doctrine of eschatology has been affirmed and refined throughout church history, always grounded in Scripture and the person of Christ.

The Early Church: The first Christians lived with immanent expectation of the Lord's return. "The Lord is near" was more than a creed — it was the pulse of early Christian worship. The Didache prays, "Maranatha — Come, Lord!" This urgent eschatological hope motivated mission, holiness, and persecution-endurance.
The Ecumenical Creeds: The Apostles' Creed, the Nicene Creed, and the Chalcedonian Formula all affirm eschatological articles: "He will come again to judge the living and the dead. His kingdom will have no end." Eschatology is not a novelty but part of the core confession of the Church universal.
Augustine and the City of God: When Rome fell to the Visigoths in 410 AD, Augustine wrote the City of God to defend Christian faith. He argued that history is the story of two cities — the City of God and the City of Man — and that ultimately God's city will prevail in the eschaton. This framework shaped medieval and Reformation theology.
The Reformation: The Reformation did not create new eschatologies but grounded eschatological hope more firmly in justification by faith. Luther and Calvin saw the resurrection and final judgment as the vindication of the sola fide principle. The believer stands before God not by works but by the imputed righteousness of Christ.
The Westminster Standards: The Westminster Confession of Faith and the Shorter and Larger Catechisms provide systematic eschatology. Chapters 32 and 33 of the Confession address death, the intermediate state, and the final judgment. They affirm the resurrection of the body, the personal return of Christ, and the eternal differentiation of the blessed and the damned.
Reformed Amillennialism: While some Protestants have adopted premillennialism (belief in a literal thousand-year reign of Christ on earth before the final judgment) or postmillennialism, the Reformed tradition has predominantly embraced amillennialism. This view holds that the kingdom of God is present in the Church and awaits its consummation without a literal millennium. The "thousand years" in Revelation 20 is interpreted symbolically as the present age of the Church.
11 · Objections

Answering the Honest Questions

The doctrine of eschatology, particularly the reality of eternal judgment, provokes the deepest questions. Here we address them with Scripture and reason.

How can a loving God send people to hell?

This objection assumes that love cannot coexist with justice. But God is not only love — God is just. A judge who punishes no crime is not good; he is a tyrant. God's holiness demands the punishment of rebellion. Hell is not the invention of a capricious deity — it is the necessary consequence of cosmic rebellion against an infinitely holy God. Moreover, Christ Himself taught more about hell than anyone in Scripture. Is He not loving?

Doesn't eternal punishment seem disproportionate?

The severity of punishment corresponds to the severity of the offense and the status of the one offended. To mock a neighbor is a small thing; to mock the King is high treason. To sin against a finite being deserves finite consequence; to sin against an infinite God deserves infinite consequence. Moreover, we tend to underestimate the seriousness of sin. Scripture calls sin "enmity against God" and "cosmic rebellion." Such treason deserves such punishment.

What about those who never heard the gospel?

Romans 1-2 addresses this directly. "What can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them" (Romans 1:19). All people have access to God's general revelation in creation and conscience. "When Gentiles, who do not have the law, do by nature what the law requires, they show that the law is written on their hearts" (Romans 2:14-15). God judges by the light given. His justice is beyond reproach.

Isn't eschatology just wishful thinking?

The Christian hope rests not on desire but on historical fact: the resurrection of Jesus Christ. If Christ did not rise, then Christianity is a delusion. But if He rose — and the historical evidence is overwhelming — then His promise to return carries absolute certainty. The resurrection of Christ is not a myth but a claimed historical event, testified by hundreds, confirmed by the transformation of the disciples from cowering fugitives to bold proclaimers willing to die.

Why hasn't Christ returned yet? Isn't it taking too long?

"The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance" (2 Peter 3:9). God's timeline is not our timeline. He may be using this time to gather His elect, to complete the gospel proclamation to the nations, to perfect the saints. Moreover, "with the Lord one day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years like one day" (2 Peter 3:8). Eternal perspective transforms the question.

What about universalism — won't God save everyone eventually?

While God's mercy is vast and His love is boundless, Scripture clearly teaches that rejection of Christ has eternal consequences. "These will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life" (Matthew 25:46). Jesus taught the reality of hell as clearly as He taught the reality of heaven. To deny eternal separation contradicts His teaching and undermines the urgency of the gospel. If all are saved, why evangelize?

12 · Witnesses

The Cloud of Eschatological Hope

Throughout the history of the Church, faithful theologians and preachers have proclaimed the blessed hope of the eschaton.

Augustine of Hippo (354-430)
"The City of God, in its pilgrimage through time, is sustained not by the power of this world but by faith in the world to come. All earthly kingdoms are but shadows and preparations for the eternal kingdom of Christ."
City of God, Book XIV
John Calvin (1509-1564)
"The hope of the resurrection is not a speculation of the philosophers but a promise of the Son of God. Just as Christ rose in a glorified body, we shall be raised to glory. This is not the escape from the body but the perfection of it."
Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.25
Jonathan Edwards (1703-1758)
"Sinners in the hands of an angry God rest on nothing but the mere pleasure of God... But to the redeemed, the future is not terror but exceeding joy. Heaven is a world of love, where all the inhabitants are united in one common interest."
Personal Narrative; Heaven: A World of Love
Charles Spurgeon (1834-1892)
"When Jesus comes again, every wrong will be righted, every tear will be wiped away, and the glory of God will fill the earth as the waters cover the sea. This is not escapism but the only rational hope for a fallen world."
Sermon on Revelation 1:7
Westminster Confession of Faith (1647)
"The bodies of the unjust shall, by the power of Christ, be raised to dishonor; the bodies of the just, by His Spirit, unto honor, and made conformable to His own glorious body. The bodies of the just shall be glorified with Christ; the souls of the wicked shall be cast into hell."
Chapter 32-33
B.B. Warfield (1851-1921)
"The resurrection of Christ guarantees the resurrection of all believers. The same power that raised Jesus from the dead will raise us. His resurrection is not an isolated miracle but the first fruit of God's new creation."
The Plan of Salvation
13 · Connections

The Doctrine's Broader Landscape

Eschatology does not stand alone in systematic theology. It connects to and completes several other major doctrines.

Ordo Salutis

Glorification is the final link in the golden chain of salvation. Understanding the order of salvation reaches its culmination in the eschaton — where the redeemed are perfected and made like Christ.

Divine Decrees

Eschatology is the fulfillment of God's eternal decrees. From before the foundation of the world, God has ordained the consummation of all things in Christ. The eschaton is not an afterthought but the predetermined end.

Christology

Christ is the returning King and final Judge. The eschaton centers on the person and work of Christ — His resurrection is the guarantee and pattern of ours; His judgment is the standard of justice; His glory fills the new creation.

Soteriology

The work of salvation is completed in the eschaton. Redemption is not merely justification and sanctification in this life but glorification in the age to come. The gospel promise extends to the resurrection and eternal life.

The Kingdom of God

Christ's kingdom is both present and future. Eschatology reveals the consummation of the kingdom — when Christ returns as King and God's rule is vindicated over all creation.

Anthropology

What we believe about human destiny shapes anthropology. The resurrection of the body affirms the goodness of human embodiment and the permanence of personal identity in the age to come.
