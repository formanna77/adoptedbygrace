## Script: "Dead People Can't Choose" — YouTube Short-Form (90 sec)
**Date drafted:** 2026-03-30
**Source page:** /story-the-dead-city.html
**Format:** Short-form video (TikTok / YouTube Shorts / Reels)
**Status:** READY FOR REVIEW

---

### SCRIPT

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:05]**
[CLOSE-UP on speaker, intense but warm]

"What if I told you that you didn't choose God?"

**[SETUP — 0:05–0:25]**
[Speaker walks slowly, talking to camera]

"Imagine a city where everyone is dead — but nobody knows it. They eat, but taste nothing. They see, but everything is grey. They talk, but there's no music in their voices. They've been dead so long, they think this is what living feels like."

[SHOW TEXT ON SCREEN: Ephesians 2:1 — "You were dead in your trespasses and sins"]

**[CORE ARGUMENT — 0:25–1:10]**
[Speaker stops, looks directly into camera]

"Now here's the question that changes everything. Can a dead person choose to come alive? Can a corpse in a graveyard decide to get up and walk?"

[Beat. Let it land.]

"Scripture doesn't say we were sick and needed medicine. It says we were *dead* and needed resurrection. Ephesians 2:4 — 'But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ.'"

[SHOW TEXT ON SCREEN: "But God" — Ephesians 2:4]

"'But God.' Two of the most explosive words in the Bible. You were dead — *but God* made you alive. Not you. Not your decision. Not your prayer. God."

**[CLOSE — 1:10–1:30]**
[Speaker, softer now, almost reverent]

"In the story, a Voice speaks from outside the dead city. It doesn't send an invitation. It doesn't wait for a response. It says one word: 'Live.' And the dead wake up. Not because they chose to hear — but because the Voice made them able to hear."

[Beat]

"That's the gospel. You didn't find God. God found you. And that should make you weep with gratitude."

[SHOW TEXT ON SCREEN: adoptedbygrace.net/story-the-dead-city]

## Script: "The Rescue Movie" — Podcast/Audio (12 min)
**Date drafted:** 2026-03-30
**Source page:** /devotional-rescued-without-a-say.html
**Format:** Conversational audio script
**Status:** READY FOR REVIEW

---

### SCRIPT

**[INTRO — 0:00–1:00]**

Hey. Thanks for listening. I want to talk to you today about a scene that shows up in almost every action movie ever made. You know the one. The building is on fire. Smoke everywhere. And the hero kicks down a door, finds someone unconscious on the floor — lungs full of smoke, seconds from death — picks them up, and carries them out.

The rescued person didn't ask for it. They didn't fill out a form. They didn't make a decision. They were dying. And someone came for them.

I want to suggest to you that this is the most accurate picture of the gospel that Hollywood has ever accidentally produced.

**[SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM — 1:00–3:30]**

We don't like to think of ourselves as the unconscious person. We prefer the language of choice. We're the architects of our own destiny. The captains of our own souls. The whole modern world runs on the assumption that you are the hero of your own story.

But when the Bible describes our spiritual condition apart from God, it uses a word that should stop us cold.

Dead.

Not struggling. Not confused. Not "spiritually seeking." Dead.

Ephesians 2:1 — "And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked."

[Pause]

Think about what that means. A dead person doesn't choose to be resurrected. A dead person doesn't file an application for life. A dead person lies still. And the gospel — the actual gospel, the one in Scripture — does not ask the dead what they think about being brought back to life.

**[SECTION 2: THE THREE NEGATIONS — 3:30–7:00]**

John chapter 1, verse 13, is one of the most carefully constructed verses in the New Testament. John is building a wall — brick by brick — eliminating every possible source of salvation except one. He says believers were born "not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God."

Three negations. Let's take them one at a time.

"Not of blood." Your family tree doesn't save you. Being born to Christian parents, growing up in church, having a pastor for a grandfather — none of this produces spiritual life. Heredity is no substitute for the new birth.

[Pause]

"Not of the will of the flesh." This is where it gets uncomfortable for a lot of people. Your desires can't save you. The modern church has built an entire system around the idea that you must first *want* to be saved — that you must feel a stirring, make a decision, choose Jesus. And then God cooperates with your will. But Paul says the natural person doesn't accept the things of the Spirit of God. They're foolishness to him. He *cannot* understand them. The flesh doesn't hunger for God until the Spirit makes it hunger.

[Pause]

"Not of the will of man." This is the one our age hates most. Your decision doesn't save you. Your commitment doesn't save you. Your sinner's prayer — sincere as it may be — doesn't save you. There's something in us that wants salvation to be a transaction where we have negotiating power. We want to sign the contract. We want our name on the dotted line.

But the gospel says: You were saying no. Violently. Continuously. In every fiber of your being. And while you were still hostile, still dead, still running in the opposite direction — God said yes. Not because you agreed. Despite your disagreement. Not because you chose Him, but because He chose you.

**[SECTION 3: THE STORIES — 7:00–10:00]**

Think about Jonah. God tells him to go to Nineveh. Jonah does the exact opposite. He buys a ticket on a ship going the other direction. He goes *down* into the hold. When the storm comes, he asks the sailors to throw him into the sea.

This is not a man who wanted to be rescued.

But God sent a fish. And for three days, in the belly of that creature at the bottom of the ocean, Jonah was held. Not gently invited to reconsider. Held. Rescued against his will. And when he finally understood what had happened, he spoke the words that every rescued soul eventually speaks:

"Salvation belongs to the Lord."

[Pause]

Or think about Saul of Tarsus. He wasn't seeking Jesus. He was hunting Christians. Breathing threats and murder, Acts 9 says. He was on his way to Damascus to arrest believers. And Jesus knocked him off his horse, blinded him, and said, "I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting."

Saul didn't choose that encounter. He didn't ask for it. He was the opposite of a seeker. He was a persecutor. And God chose him anyway.

**[SECTION 4: THE LANDING — 10:00–11:30]**

Here's why this matters to you, right now, today.

If your salvation depends on you — on the strength of your decision, the quality of your faith, the consistency of your commitment — then you're in trouble. Because you know how weak your decisions are. You know how fickle your heart is. You know that the same person who said "I'll follow you anywhere" is the same person who can't keep a New Year's resolution past February.

But if salvation is God's doing — if He's the one who kicked down the door, carried you out of the burning building, breathed life into your dead lungs — then your salvation is as secure as He is faithful. And He is very, very faithful.

Philippians 1:6 — "He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ."

He began it. He'll finish it. You are His workmanship. Not your own.

**[OUTRO — 11:30–12:00]**

If any of this stirred something in you — if you felt a flicker, a pull, a restlessness — I'd encourage you to sit with it. Read the full devotional this was based on. It's at adoptedbygrace.net/devotional-rescued-without-a-say.

And if what you're feeling right now is gratitude — if you're starting to see that your faith wasn't your achievement but God's gift — then let that gratitude become worship. Because the glory belongs entirely to Him.

Thanks for listening.
