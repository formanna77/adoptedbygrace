Theologians

Charles Haddon Spurgeon

The Prince of Preachers who proved that sovereign grace is not the enemy of evangelism — it is its fuel. (1834–1892)

In Brief: The greatest preacher in English history. Converted at fifteen. Pastored the largest church in the world by twenty-two. Preached to ten million people in his lifetime. Fought liberalism to the death. Battled depression throughout. And never once apologized for the doctrines of grace — because he believed, with every fiber of his being, that "Calvinism is the gospel, and nothing else."

January 1850. A fifteen-year-old boy is trudging through snow in Colchester, coat pulled tight, the wind cutting through the wool. He cannot reach his usual church — the drifts are too deep. He ducks into a tiny Primitive Methodist chapel, maybe a dozen people inside, the air thick with damp wool and cold breath. A man stands up to preach — a man so simple, Spurgeon later said, he could barely read — and opens to Isaiah 45:22: "Look unto me and be ye saved, all the ends of the earth." The man looks directly at the boy in the back pew and says: "Young man, you look very miserable. Look to Jesus Christ. Look! Look! Look!"

The boy looked. And the God who had chosen him before the snow, before Colchester, before England, before the first star was flung into the dark — that God opened his eyes.

"I have my own private opinion that there is no such thing as preaching Christ and Him crucified, unless we preach what is nowadays called Calvinism. It is a nickname to call it Calvinism; Calvinism is the gospel, and nothing else."

That is the man who walked out of that chapel. Charles Haddon Spurgeon — who spoke to ten million people, who preached to six thousand every Sunday for thirty years — telling you that sovereign grace is not one interpretation of the gospel. It IS the gospel. And if something in you just bristled at that — if you admire his fire but wish he'd been less dogmatic about the theology — notice: you are doing the exact thing his entire ministry was designed to prevent. You are separating the preacher from the truth that made him.

The Conversion

Born in 1834 in Kelvedon, Essex, Spurgeon was raised in a pious Congregationalist home but lacked genuine conversion until January 1850. On a cold evening, unable to reach his usual church through the snow, he ducked into a small Primitive Methodist chapel. A simple man — so simple Spurgeon later said he could barely read — stood and preached from Isaiah 45:22: "Look unto me and be ye saved, all the ends of the earth."

That was all it took. God opened his eyes. At fifteen, Spurgeon was converted with revolutionary assurance — and he never looked back.

By nineteen, he was pastoring New Park Street Chapel in London — a congregation in decline, meeting in a neglected building. Within weeks, it overflowed. By 1856, the Metropolitan Tabernacle opened with 5,600 seats — the largest congregation in the world — and it filled to capacity Sunday after Sunday for three decades.

Calvinism on Fire

Spurgeon's theology was thoroughly Reformed and radically evangelistic. He demolished the false choice between sound doctrine and passionate outreach. His key insight was devastatingly simple: if God has chosen a people, then the gospel will not fail. This gives the preacher liberty to preach to all, knowing God's elect will hear and come.

"I do not come into this pulpit hoping that perhaps somebody will of his own free will return to Christ. My hope lies in another quarter. I hope that my Master will lay hold of some of them and say, 'You are mine, and you shall be mine. I claim you for myself.'"

SERMON: EFFECTUAL CALLING

He preached total depravity without flinching, unconditional election without apology, particular redemption without softening, irresistible grace with tears in his eyes, and the perseverance of the saints with the confidence of a man who knew his salvation rested on God's character, not his own. He proved that a pastor could be both a rigorous Calvinist and a fervent evangelist — and that the two are not in tension but are the same fire burning in two directions.

The Downgrade Controversy

In the 1880s, Spurgeon stood alone against creeping liberalism in the Baptist Union. Higher criticism was eroding confidence in Scripture. Pastors were abandoning cardinal truths. Spurgeon would not bend. He resigned his membership in 1887 rather than compromise, a decision that cost him friendships, standing, and health.

Spurgeon's diagnosis of the mainline Protestant decline was both devastating and darkly humorous:

"A time will come when instead of shepherds feeding the sheep, the church will have clowns entertaining the goats."

He said it over a century ago. He was right.

The Suffering

Spurgeon battled depression throughout his ministry — what he called "the minister's fainting fits." He also suffered gout, rheumatism, and Bright's disease. Yet he preached through it all. His suffering was not evidence that his theology was wrong. It was evidence that the God of the gospel sustains His servants through the valley, not around it.

He died at fifty-seven in Mentone, France, his confidence in Christ unshaken. His honesty about pain — honest in a way that most preachers of his era were not — gave permission for generations of Christians to suffer openly while still trusting God.

Words That Still Burn

"I believe that every particle of dust that dances in the sunbeam does not move an atom more or less than God wishes."

"If God would have painted a yellow stripe on the backs of the elect, I'd go around lifting up shirt tails. But God didn't do that. He told me to preach the Gospel to every creature."

"If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to Hell over our dead bodies. And if they perish, let them perish with our arms wrapped about their knees, imploring them to stay."

"The truth of grace is simply this — that God is the Alpha and the Omega in the salvation of men. He begins the work, He carries it on, and He will complete it."

Why He Matters Now

Spurgeon modeled what the modern church desperately needs: sound truth married to passionate evangelism, scholarly rigor wed to popular accessibility, Reformed conviction burning with evangelical fire. Martyn Lloyd-Jones studied his sermons obsessively. John MacArthur and John Piper cite him constantly. The entire modern Reformed resurgence flows in no small measure from Spurgeon's wells.

His major works remain among the most-read Christian literature: The Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit (63 volumes of sermons — the largest in English history), The Treasury of David (Psalms commentary), Morning and Evening (beloved devotional), All of Grace (gospel summary), and Lectures to My Students (preaching instruction still vital today).

To study Spurgeon is to be converted again — not to a new religion, but to a deeper love for Christ and fiercer commitment to His glory. His life poses a question:

Will I compromise for comfort?

Will I water down truth for numbers?

Will I let the culture shame me into silence about the sovereignty of God?

Spurgeon's answer was a thunderous No.

And in that refusal, he became the preacher who shaped Christendom.

Go back to that snowy evening. A boy in a back pew, coat still wet, shivering. A man too simple to be impressive says three words: Look. Look. Look. And the boy looks — not because he generated the capacity to see, but because a sovereign God, in that precise moment, in that tiny chapel buried in snow, opened eyes that had been blind since birth.

Spurgeon never forgot that he contributed nothing to that moment. That is why he preached grace for thirty years without apology. He knew — the way you know your own name — that the looking was a gift.

May we follow him as he followed Christ — into the arms of a God whose grace never lets go.
