Every Decision Point

Which side do the Scriptures really take?

At the heart of this site is a simple observation: throughout Scripture, God takes sides. And at every crucial doctrinal decision point, there are only two positions—a man-centered one and a God-centered one. What follows is not a straw man of either position. These are the actual, logical conclusions of each framework when followed honestly to the end.

The striking pattern becomes undeniable once you see it: at EVERY decision point, man-centered theology sides with man. God-centered theology sides with God. And the Scripture verdict is unambiguous.

This companion to "Whose Side Are You On?" shows the systematic nature of the divide. There is no neutral ground here. Every doctrinal question forces a choice: Whose will is ultimate? Who gets to decide salvation? On whom should the sinner's hope rest? The framework you choose determines how you answer all ten of these questions.

1.Who Initiates Salvation?

Man-Centered View
Man initiates. The gospel is presented, and the sinner decides whether to accept it. The Holy Spirit creates the opportunity; man creates the response. God knocks on the door; man opens it. Salvation begins when man says yes.
God-Centered View
God initiates and completes the entire action. God chose His people before the foundation of the world. God ordained their salvation. God drew them, called them, and regenerated them. Man's response comes AFTER God's action, not before.
Scripture Verdict:
"He chose us in him before the foundation of the world" (Ephesians 1:4). God's choice is BEFORE anyone believed. "You did not choose me, but I chose you and appointed you that you should go and bear fruit" (John 15:16). The initiative is God's. The choice is His. The timing is before time.
EPHESIANS 1:4, JOHN 15:16

2.What Is the Human Condition?

Man-Centered View
Man is sick but able. Trapped in sin, weakened, unable to see clearly—but not completely powerless. The will remains free. The ability to reach for God remains. The sinner is like a patient in a hospital, not a corpse in a grave. Help is needed, but response is possible.
God-Centered View
Man is dead in sin. Not sick. Not weak. Dead. "As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins" (Ephesians 2:1). A corpse cannot decide to live. A slave sold to sin cannot free himself. The natural man is incapable of choosing God because sin has destroyed his capacity to will toward Him.
Scripture Verdict:
"You were dead in your transgressions and sins" (Ephesians 2:1-5). Dead. Not "dying" or "very sick." Dead. "No one seeks for God. All have turned aside; together they have become worthless" (Romans 3:10-12). Not one seeks. Not one understands. Not one does good. This is total depravity—not total inability in every act, but inability to produce saving faith.
EPHESIANS 2:1-5, ROMANS 3:10-12
At this decision point, the framework chooses between dignity and depravity. Man-centered theology protects human dignity—you are sick but able, you have the power to reach for God. God-centered theology accepts the horror of depravity—you are dead, and only resurrection can save you. The flesh cannot bear to be dead. It must be sick. It must be able. It must have contributed something.
— The Spirit Resisting the Truth

3.Where Does Faith Come From?

Man-Centered View
Faith is generated by the human will. The Spirit may enable the will, but the will itself produces the faith. Man believes. Man has faith. Faith is the sinner's contribution to salvation. It is the one thing the sinner alone can provide. It is, ultimately, a work of man's will.
God-Centered View
Faith is a gift from God. "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Ephesians 2:8-9). God grants faith to whom He will. "For it has been granted to you that for the sake of Christ you should not only believe in him but also suffer for his sake" (Philippians 1:29). Faith is granted, not generated by the self.
Scripture Verdict:
"Faith itself is the gift of God—not your own doing." The grammar is clear: even the faith is not of yourself. It is the gift. This is the Crown Jewel truth on which everything turns. If faith is a gift, you did not contribute to your salvation. You received it. If you received it, you cannot claim credit for it. To claim credit for a gift is works-righteousness.
EPHESIANS 2:8-9, PHILIPPIANS 1:29

4.For Whom Did Christ Die?

Man-Centered View
Christ died for everyone without exception. His death made salvation possible for all. It creates the opportunity. But whether anyone actually benefits depends on whether they choose to accept it. The cross is a universal offer, not a guaranteed purchase. Christ died for people who will ultimately reject Him.
God-Centered View
Christ died for His people specifically. "He shall save His people from their sins" (Matthew 1:21). "I am the good shepherd; I know my own and my own know me... and I lay down my life for the sheep" (John 10:11, 14-15). Christ's death accomplishes its purpose. It does not merely create possibility—it purchases redemption for those whom the Father has given Him.
Scripture Verdict:
"I am the good shepherd; I know my own and my own know me" (John 10:14). There is a definite people. They are known. They are His. Christ did not die for a hypothetical universal group. He died for specific people—"His people," "His sheep." The atonement is particular, not universal in efficacy.
MATTHEW 1:21, JOHN 10:11-15

5.Can Grace Be Resisted?

Man-Centered View
Yes. The Holy Spirit does not override the will. He offers grace, creates opportunity, but respects human freedom. If a person chooses to refuse God, even after the Spirit draws, God will not override that choice. The sinner's will is the final say. God waits. God pleads. But the power is ultimately in the hands of man.
God-Centered View
No. Grace is irresistible. "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him" (John 6:44). Not "unless he accepts the invitation." Unless the Father DRAWS him. Those whom the Father draws COME. The language is passive—the dead are drawn, and the dead come. Grace accomplishes its purpose. God raises the dead, and the dead rise.
Scripture Verdict:
"All that the Father gives me will come to me" (John 6:37). Not "may come" or "are invited." Will come. It is a promise, not an invitation to possibly accept. The Father's giving is effective. The coming is guaranteed. "I will put my Spirit within you and cause you to walk in my statutes" (Ezekiel 36:27). God CAUSES. The transformation is not an offer; it is an action completed.
JOHN 6:37, JOHN 6:44, EZEKIEL 36:27
Here is where the emotional weight of the disagreement becomes unbearable for the man-centered framework. The idea that God would force faith, that grace could not be rejected, that the will is not ultimately sovereign—this feels like a violation of everything we hold dear about human dignity and freedom. But Scripture is not asking what we feel. It is declaring what is true: God's will is sovereign, and those whom He draws will come.
— The Resistance to Irresistible Grace

6.Can Salvation Be Lost?

Man-Centered View
Yes, salvation can be lost if the believer fails to maintain it. The initial faith is the sinner's contribution, so the continuation of that faith is also the sinner's responsibility. If you fail to persevere, if you fail to keep believing, then you lose what you once had. Assurance is contingent on your ongoing faithfulness.
God-Centered View
No, salvation cannot be lost. "My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand" (John 10:28-29). "The one who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6). God guards. God preserves. God finishes what He started. The chain of Romans 8:29-30 has no broken links.
Scripture Verdict:
"For I am sure that neither death nor life...nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord" (Romans 8:38-39). Nothing can separate the redeemed from God's love. Not weakness, not failure, not sin. The preservation is God's work, not the believer's. A child of God who cannot lose his salvation is a child of God whose Father is infinitely more faithful than he is.
JOHN 10:28-29, PHILIPPIANS 1:6, ROMANS 8:29-30, ROMANS 8:38-39

7.Who Gets the Glory?

Man-Centered View
Glory is shared. God gets glory for making salvation possible; man gets glory for making the choice. The initiative is God's, but the decision is man's. The credit is divided. The sinner can say, "I chose God." This is how the sinner receives recognition for their faith, their decision, their commitment, and their contribution.
God-Centered View
Glory belongs to God alone. "So, whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do, do it all for the glory of God" (1 Corinthians 10:31). "To him be glory in the church and in Christ Jesus throughout all generations, forever and ever" (Ephesians 3:21). God is not willing to share His glory with another. If you are saved, it is God who chose, God who called, God who regenerated, God who preserved. God gets all the glory.
Scripture Verdict:
"I am the Lord; that is my name; my glory I give to no other" (Isaiah 42:8). God will not share His glory. All glory belongs to Him. "Soli Deo Gloria." Salvation is to the praise of His glory, not to the praise of man's choice (Ephesians 1:12-14). The invisible hand of the Father is the hand that saves. The credit, recognition, and praise belong to Him alone.
ISAIAH 42:8, EPHESIANS 1:12-14, 1 CORINTHIANS 10:31

8.What Explains the Difference Between Saved and Unsaved?

Man-Centered View
The difference is the human choice. The saved chose God; the unsaved did not. Both heard the gospel. Both had the opportunity. But the saved ones made the better choice. This is how human dignity is preserved and human responsibility is maintained. The saved are saved because they decided to be saved.
God-Centered View
The difference is God's mercy. "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy" (Romans 9:16). Those who are saved are those on whom God has had mercy. Those who are not are those whom He did not choose. Both groups are equally sinful. Both deserve judgment. The difference is God's electing grace, not human decision.
Scripture Verdict:
"It does not depend on the man who wills or the man who runs, but on God who has mercy" (Romans 9:16). This is the watershed verse. Does salvation depend on human will? No. Does it depend on human effort? No. It depends on God's mercy. The difference between those who belong to God and those who do not is not that some made better choices—it is that God chose them. This is grace.
ROMANS 9:16, ROMANS 9:22-23
This is the moment the foundations shift. If the difference between saved and unsaved is a human choice, then credit belongs to the chooser. But if the difference is God's mercy, then the lost cannot blame God—they rejected a gift they were unworthy to receive. And the saved cannot boast—they were chosen despite being equally unworthy. They were vessels prepared for mercy (Romans 9:23). They were never the heroes of their own salvation stories.
— The Socratic Trap Closes

9.What Is the Basis of Election?

Man-Centered View
God elected those whom He foreknew would believe. God looked down the corridors of time, saw who would choose Him, and based His election on that foreseen faith. Election is not arbitrary—it is based on God's knowledge of human decisions. This preserves the idea that God's choice is informed by man's choice.
God-Centered View
God elected those whom He chose, for His own sovereign pleasure, without regard to foreseen faith (which would be a basis in man, not in God). "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated. Before they were born or had done anything good or bad—in order that God's purpose of election might stand" (Romans 9:11-13). The basis is God's will alone. The reason is His sovereign pleasure.
Scripture Verdict:
"Before they were born or had done anything good or bad...God's purpose of election might continue to stand" (Romans 9:11-13). Before they were born. Before they did anything. Election is not based on anything foreseen or generated by the elect. It is based on God's purpose. Not what He foresaw in them, but what He determined for them. "According to the purpose of his will" (Ephesians 1:5). The basis is purely God's sovereign pleasure.
ROMANS 9:11-13, EPHESIANS 1:5

10.What Secures Your Assurance?

Man-Centered View
Your continued faithfulness. You believed in the past, but your assurance depends on believing in the future. Your security rests on your ongoing commitment, your continued obedience, your sustained choice to follow Christ. If you slip, if you fail, if you stumble—assurance wavers. You must maintain the faith you contributed.
God-Centered View
God's unchanging purpose. "And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose. For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son... And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified" (Romans 8:28-30). The chain is complete. No link breaks.
Scripture Verdict:
"The one who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6). Your assurance does not rest on your faithfulness—it rests on God's faithfulness. "I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me" (Philippians 4:13). He began it. He will complete it. The golden chain of Romans 8:29-30 is unbreakable: foreknown, predestined, called, justified, glorified. No one is lost from the chain. No one falls through a gap.
ROMANS 8:28-30, PHILIPPIANS 1:6

The Cumulative Pattern

Do you see what has happened? At every single decision point, when you follow the logic of each framework to its honest conclusion, you discover that man-centered theology sides with man, and God-centered theology sides with God.

The man-centered framework says: man initiates (humans make the first move), man is able (humans retain the power to choose), man generates faith (humans produce it), Christ died for everyone (humans will determine who benefits), grace can be resisted (humans retain control), salvation can be lost (humans can fail), man shares the glory (humans get credit), the difference is human choice (some humans are better), election is based on foreseen faith (humans are the basis), and assurance depends on human faithfulness (humans maintain it).

Do you see the pattern? At every point, the system exalts the human will. At every point, it reserves some crucial power, some final say, some portion of credit for man.

This is why people resist God-centered theology: It threatens everything the flesh holds dear—dignity, power, credit, control, autonomy. It strips away every opportunity for boasting. It leaves the sinner with nothing but the grace of God. And the natural man cannot bear that. The natural man must believe he contributed something, chose something, maintained something. Without that belief, he feels powerless and worthless.

But here is what the elect discover: once you see the complete pattern, you cannot unsee it. Once you have walked through this logic ten times and found that Scripture sides with God at every point, the truth becomes inescapable. And when the truth finally settles into your bones, you realize something miraculous: you are free.

Free from the burden of maintaining your own salvation. Free from the terror of possibly losing it. Free from the illusion that you are the author of your own faith. Free from the weight of trying to be good enough. And most of all, free to rest in the arms of a God whose grip has never loosened, whose purpose has never wavered, whose love has never been contingent on your performance.

This is the freedom that grace provides—not freedom to sin, but freedom to finally stop trying to earn what was never earned. You were chosen before you were broken. Redeemed before you realized you were lost. Held all along.
